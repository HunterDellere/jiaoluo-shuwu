    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="不">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p>The oracle-bone form of <strong>不</strong> shows a bird in flight with wings spread — or, in another interpretation, a plant whose roots go deep into the earth while its upper stem strains upward toward something <em>beyond reach.</em> Either image encodes the same abstract: something that goes toward a limit and cannot arrive there. Negation as <em>perpetual non-arrival.</em></p>
      <p>This etymology is surprisingly deep. 不 is not simply "the absence of X" — it is X being aimed at and missed, an action directed at a destination that remains out of reach. This is why 不 negates intention and state (不去 = not going, 不好 = not good) while 没 méi negates completed events (没去 = didn't go). 不 is the refusal of arrival; 没 is the absence of having arrived.</p>
      <p>不 is by most frequency counts the single most used character in written and spoken Mandarin. Every sentence of negation — and most sentences of qualification — passes through it.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── TONE SANDHI ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="sandhi"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">变调规律</span>
      <span class="sh-py">biàndiào guīlǜ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Tone Sandhi — bù → bú before Tone 4</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">变调规则 biàndiào guīzé · The Rule</span>
      <strong>不 bù (tone 4) + tone 1/2/3 syllable</strong> → stays bù (tone 4)<br>
      <strong>不 bù (tone 4) + tone 4 syllable</strong> → becomes bú (tone 2)<br><br>
      不去 bù qù · 不好 bù hǎo · 不来 bù lái (stays 4th)<br>
      <strong>不是 bú shì</strong> · <strong>不对 bú duì</strong> · <strong>不要 bú yào</strong> (shifts to 2nd)
    </div>

    <div class="note">
      <span class="note-label">语音 yǔyīn · Why This Happens</span>
      Two consecutive 4th-tone syllables clash acoustically — both drop sharply, making the boundary between words hard to hear. The shift of 不 to tone 2 before another tone 4 creates acoustic contrast. This is the same principle behind the 一 yī sandhi rule. In pinyin dictionaries, 不 is listed as bù, but in natural speech before a 4th-tone syllable, it sounds like bú.
    </div>

    <div class="note" style="margin-top:1rem;">
      <span class="note-label">关键区别 · The Core Distinction</span>
      不 negates <em>willingness, state, or habit</em>. 没 negates <em>occurrence</em>. "I don't eat meat" (habit) = 我不吃肉. "I didn't eat meat [that time]" (event) = 我没吃肉. With 有 yǒu, only 没 is used: 没有 méiyǒu (don't have / there isn't). 不有 does not exist.
    </div>

          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">不好意思，我来晚了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Bù hǎoyìsi, wǒ lái wǎn le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Sorry, I'm late. (mild, not a full apology)</div>

        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">不错</span>
          <span class="card-py">búcuò</span>
          <span class="card-en">not bad; pretty good; quite good</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adj 形容词 xíngróngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          不 bù + 错 cuò (wrong; mistake). "Not wrong" = pretty good. A classic understatement — the Chinese preference for measured praise. 不错 is the standard compliment that doesn't oversell. 错 alone means "wrong/mistaken."
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这个菜做得不错。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhège cài zuò de búcuò.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This dish turned out quite well.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">不得了</span>
          <span class="card-py">bùdéliǎo</span>
          <span class="card-en">extremely; terrible; amazing (intensifier)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Expr 表达 biǎodá</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          不 + 得了 déliǎo (can be concluded; can be over). "Cannot be concluded" → extreme beyond measure. Used after adjectives as a strong intensifier: 好得不得了 = incredibly good. Also standalone: 不得了！= This is unbelievable / It's a disaster! (depending on context).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">她高兴得不得了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā gāoxìng de bùdéliǎo.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">She's incredibly happy / over the moon.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">不得不</span>
          <span class="card-py">bùdébù</span>
          <span class="card-en">cannot but; have no choice but to</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V pattern 动词句型</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          不得不 (not-obtain-not) = double negation creates strong obligation: "cannot not do" = must do, compelled by circumstances. Stronger than 必须 bìxū in the sense of reluctant necessity. The speaker has no choice.

            <div class="ex-en">He had no choice but to admit he was wrong.</div>

    <!-- ── RHETORICAL USES ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="rhetorical"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">修辞用法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">xiūcí yòngfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Rhetorical Uses — Double Negatives &amp; Understatement</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="不">
      <div class="scholar-label">双重否定 shuāngchóng fǒudìng · Double Negation</div>
      <p>Chinese uses double negation extensively for emphasis, understatement, and rhetorical force. Unlike English where "double negatives cancel out" in prescriptive grammar, Mandarin double negatives follow logic: <em>cannot not</em> = must. Key patterns:</p>
      <p><strong>不…不</strong>: 不来不行 (if you don't come, it won't work = you must come). <strong>不得不</strong>: compelled necessity. <strong>不…也不</strong>: neither…nor. <strong>不是不…而是</strong>: it's not that I don't X, it's that… (a nuanced face-saving refusal pattern very common in polite speech).</p>
      <p>The understatement pattern is equally important: instead of saying 很好 (very good), a measured Chinese speaker says 不错 (not bad). Instead of 我非常同意 (I strongly agree), they may say 我没有什么不同意的 (I have nothing I disagree with). Negation is a vehicle for politeness and modesty.</p>
    </div>

        <span class="cy-py">bù yán ér yù</span>
        <span class="cy-en">goes without saying — self-evident, needing no words</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: not-speak-and-understood. The Chinese equivalent of "needless to say." 喻 yù = to understand by analogy. High-frequency in written Chinese for flagging obvious conclusions.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">不可思议</span>
        <span class="cy-py">bù kě sīyì</span>
        <span class="cy-en">inconceivable; unimaginable; mind-boggling</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: not-can-think-discuss. A Buddhist term originally: that which cannot be grasped by ordinary thought. Now everyday for anything astounding or hard to believe.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">不三不四</span>
        <span class="cy-py">bù sān bù sì</span>
        <span class="cy-en">neither one thing nor another — shady, dubious, not upright</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: not-three not-four. Three and four are "middle" numbers — something that is neither here nor there, neither proper nor improper. Used for shady people or behavior that doesn't conform to standards.</span>
      </div>

        <span class="cy-en">to give up halfway — to abandon something before it's complete</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: half-way-and-abandon. The criticism leveled at those who don't see things through. Often paired with 不 in advice: 做事不能半途而废 (You can't give up halfway through things).</span>
