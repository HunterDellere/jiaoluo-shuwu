    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="有">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p>The oracle-bone form of <strong>有 yǒu</strong> shows a hand <strong>又 yòu</strong> holding a piece of meat <strong>月 yuè</strong> (the early graph for flesh, later shared with the moon radical). The original meaning was literal: <em>to hold meat = to possess.</em> From the concrete act of gripping food came the abstract notion of having and owning anything at all. The existential sense — "there is; there exists" — developed because possession and existence merge at a fundamental level: if something is "held in the world," it exists; if no hand holds it, it may as well not be.</p>
      <p>The component that now appears at the top of 有 is written as a simplified 又 (hand/again), but in oracle-bone script it was explicitly an outstretched hand reaching downward to grasp. The bottom component — often glossed as 月 moon — is actually the meat radical 肉, which collapsed into the same form as 月 in the clerical script period. 有 is thus hand + meat, not hand + moon, though both components look identical in modern script.</p>
      <p>In Japanese, 有る aru (to exist, for inanimate objects) derives from the same root. 有無 u-mu (existence and nonexistence) remains active in Japanese philosophical vocabulary — the same Daoist binary. In Chinese, 有 versus 无 wú is one of the great organizing oppositions of metaphysics: the Daodejing's first lines turn precisely on this pair, asking whether 有 and 无 share the same origin or arise from different principles.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">构词规律 gòucí guīlǜ · Three Templates</span>
      <strong>有 + noun</strong> → possession: 我有钱 wǒ yǒu qián · I have money<br>
      <strong>Place/Time + 有 + noun</strong> → existential: 桌上有书 zhuō shàng yǒu shū · On the table there are books<br>
      <strong>没有</strong> → negation of both uses (NEVER 不有 — only 没有 negates 有); 所有/具有/拥有 → formal possession spectrum

    <!-- ── POSSESSION ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="possession"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">拥有</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yōngyǒu</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Possession — to have</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <!-- 有 -->
      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">有</span>
          <span class="card-py">yǒu</span>
          <span class="card-en">to have; to own; to possess</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The possessive use: subject + 有 + object. 我有一本书 (I have a book). 他有时间 (He has time). 她有很多朋友 (She has many friends). Unlike English "have," 有 is never used as an auxiliary verb — there is no equivalent of English "I have done" formed with 有. Completed actions use aspect markers 了, 过, etc. instead. 有 remains a main verb only.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我有一个问题想问你。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ yǒu yī gè wèntí xiǎng wèn nǐ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I have a question I'd like to ask you.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">她有两个孩子。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā yǒu liǎng gè háizi.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">She has two children.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">你有没有时间？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Nǐ yǒu méiyǒu shíjiān?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Do you have time? (A-not-A form)</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">语法 yǔfǎ · 有 vs. 是</span>
          <strong>有</strong> = to have (possession/existence). <strong>是</strong> = to be (identity/equation). 我有书 (I have a book) vs. 这是书 (This is a book). The two are never interchangeable — a very common beginner error is using 是 where 有 is required.
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- 没有 -->
      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">没有</span>
          <span class="card-py">méiyǒu</span>
          <span class="card-en">not have; there isn't; did not (negation of 有 and past actions)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          没 méi (not; lacking) + 有. The <strong>only</strong> negation form for 有 — never 不有. 没有 also negates past actions: 他没有来 (He didn't come). This double function is critical: 没有 is both "don't have" and "didn't do." The shortened form 没 méi (without 有) works in rapid speech before a verb: 他没来. Before a noun, the full 没有 is standard: 我没有钱.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我没有钱，不能去。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ méiyǒu qián, bù néng qù.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I don't have money — I can't go.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他昨天没有来上课。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā zuótiān méiyǒu lái shàngkè.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He didn't come to class yesterday. <em>[past negation]</em></div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">没有什么问题。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Méiyǒu shénme wèntí.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">There's no problem at all.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">语法 yǔfǎ · The Critical Rule</span>
          <strong>NEVER use 不有.</strong> 有 is unique among Chinese verbs in being negated exclusively with 没 rather than 不. All other verbs can be negated with 不 in some context, but 有 cannot. This is the single most important grammatical fact about 有.
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- 有没有 -->
      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">有没有</span>
          <span class="card-py">yǒu méiyǒu</span>
          <span class="card-en">do you have? / is there? (A-not-A question)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The A-not-A question form of 有. Used both for possession ("do you have X?") and for existence ("is there X?"). In rapid speech, 有没有 is often compressed or the object follows immediately: 有没有时间？ Also used as a sentence-final tag: 你来过北京，有没有？ — "You've been to Beijing, haven't you?"
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这里有没有厕所？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhèlǐ yǒu méiyǒu cèsuǒ?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Is there a restroom here?</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">你有没有看过这部电影？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Nǐ yǒu méiyǒu kàn guò zhè bù diànyǐng?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Have you seen this film?</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

    </div><!-- /cards possession -->

    <!-- ── EXISTENCE ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="existence"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">存在</span>
      <span class="sh-py">cúnzài</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Existence — There Is / There Are</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="有">
      <div class="scholar-label">语法洞见 yǔfǎ dòngjiàn · Grammar Insight</div>
      <p>The <strong>existential 有</strong> follows a fixed word order: <code>[place/time] + 有 + [thing]</code>. The thing being asserted to exist is <em>always</em> indefinite or generic — existential 有 introduces new information, not given information. 桌上有一本书 (There's a book on the table) is natural; 桌上有那本书 (That book is on the table) is unnatural — because 那本书 is already known. For definite location of a known object, use 在: 那本书在桌上 (That book is on the table).</p>
      <p>This is the <strong>有 vs. 在 distinction</strong>: 有 introduces existence of something new into the discourse; 在 locates something already known. 房间里有人 (There's someone in the room — new info) vs. 他在房间里 (He is in the room — locating a known person).</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <!-- 有人 -->
      <div class="card c-vn">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">有人</span>
          <span class="card-py">yǒu rén</span>
          <span class="card-en">there is someone; someone (existential + indefinite reference)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-vn">V/N 动名词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          有 in the existential construction introduces a person into the discourse. 有人 can function as the subject of a following verb: 有人说… (Someone said…; There are people who say…). This is a common way to introduce an indefinite agent — "some people say," "someone thinks," "there are those who believe."
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">外面有人等你。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wàimiàn yǒu rén děng nǐ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">There's someone outside waiting for you.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">有人认为这样做是不对的。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Yǒu rén rènwéi zhèyàng zuò shì bú duì de.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Some people think this approach is wrong.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- 有意思 -->
      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">有意思</span>
          <span class="card-py">yǒu yìsi</span>
          <span class="card-en">interesting; meaningful; amusing (lit. "has meaning")</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adj 形容词 xíngróngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          有 + 意思 yìsi (meaning; intention; interesting quality). One of the most common compliments in everyday Chinese. The possessive-existential structure of 有 creates a transparent logic: "this thing holds meaning / interest within it." The negative form 没有意思 or 没意思 méi yìsi = boring, pointless, meaningless.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这本书很有意思，你应该读一读。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè běn shū hěn yǒu yìsi, nǐ yīnggāi dú yī dú.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This book is very interesting — you should give it a read.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">开会真没意思。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Kāihuì zhēn méi yìsi.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Meetings are really boring.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">构词 gòucí · The X 有 Pattern</span>
          有意思 is one instance of a vast productive pattern: 有 + noun = "to have/possess the quality of X." 有名 yǒumíng (famous, lit. "has a name") · 有用 yǒuyòng (useful, lit. "has use") · 有钱 yǒuqián (wealthy, lit. "has money") · 有趣 yǒuqù (interesting, lit. "has interest/fun").
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- 桌上有书 pattern -->
      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">桌上有书</span>
          <span class="card-py">zhuō shàng yǒu shū</span>
          <span class="card-en">The existential pattern: place + 有 + thing</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">构式 gòushì · Construction</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The canonical existential 有 structure. Place word or place phrase comes first, followed by 有, followed by the indefinite thing being asserted to exist. The place functions as the topic — the frame within which existence is being asserted. The thing at the end carries the new information and is always new or indefinite to the discourse.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">冰箱里有什么？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Bīngxiāng lǐ yǒu shénme?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">What's in the fridge?</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">北京有很多好吃的地方。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Běijīng yǒu hěn duō hǎochī de dìfang.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Beijing has many good places to eat.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">公司附近有一家咖啡馆。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Gōngsī fùjìn yǒu yī jiā kāfēiguǎn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Near the office there's a café.</div>
          </div>
        </div>

    <!-- ── DEGREE PARTICLES ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="degree"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">有点 / 一点</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yǒudiǎn / yīdiǎn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Degree Particles — a Critical Distinction</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <!-- 有点 -->
      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">有点</span>
          <span class="card-py">yǒudiǎn</span>
          <span class="card-en">a bit; slightly (pre-verbal, negative or complaint coloring)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adv 副词 fùcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          有 + 点 diǎn (a dot; a small amount). Always appears <strong>before verbs and adjectives</strong> as a pre-modifier. The key nuance: 有点 carries a <strong>slightly negative or complaint-inflected</strong> tone. You use it for problems, inconveniences, or things that fall short. 有点贵 (a bit expensive — implying it's too much), 有点累 (a bit tired), 有点难 (a bit difficult — slightly problematic).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">今天有点冷，你多穿一件吧。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Jīntiān yǒudiǎn lěng, nǐ duō chuān yī jiàn ba.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">It's a bit cold today — put on an extra layer.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这个问题有点复杂。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhège wèntí yǒudiǎn fùzá.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This problem is a bit complicated.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我有点不舒服，想早点回去。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ yǒudiǎn bù shūfu, xiǎng zǎodiǎn huí qù.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I'm not feeling well — I'd like to head back early.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">辨析 biànxī · 有点 vs. 一点</span>
          <strong>有点 yǒudiǎn</strong>: pre-verbal/pre-adjectival · negative/complaint coloring · means "somewhat; a bit too…" · 有点贵 (a bit pricey — implied problem). <strong>一点 yīdiǎn</strong>: post-verbal/post-adjectival · neutral or positive · means "a little" · 便宜一点 (a little cheaper — request, positive direction). Never swap them: 有点便宜 is odd; 一点贵 is ungrammatical.
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- 一点 -->
      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一点</span>
          <span class="card-py">yīdiǎn</span>
          <span class="card-en">a little; a bit (post-verbal, neutral or positive)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adv 副词 fùcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          一 yī (one) + 点 diǎn (dot; bit). Appears <strong>after</strong> adjectives and verbs, typically in commands, requests, or comparisons. Neutral to positive in coloring. 便宜一点 (a little cheaper — as a request), 快一点 (a little faster), 多吃一点 (eat a little more). In comparisons: 比昨天冷一点 (a little colder than yesterday).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">能不能便宜一点？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Néng bu néng piányí yīdiǎn?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Could you make it a little cheaper?</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">请说慢一点，我听不清楚。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Qǐng shuō màn yīdiǎn, wǒ tīng bù qīngchǔ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Please speak a little more slowly — I can't hear clearly.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">今天比昨天暖和一点。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Jīntiān bǐ zuótiān nuǎnhuo yīdiǎn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Today is a little warmer than yesterday.</div>
          </div>
        </div>

    <!-- ── FORMAL POSSESSION ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="formal"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">所有</span>
      <span class="sh-py">suǒyǒu</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Formal Possession — Learned Compounds</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <!-- 所有 -->
      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">所有</span>
          <span class="card-py">suǒyǒu</span>
          <span class="card-en">all; every; everything one has</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adj/Det 形容词/限定词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          所 suǒ (that which; a place; nominalizing particle) + 有 (have/exist). 所有 as a determiner: 所有的学生 (all the students). As a noun: 他失去了所有 (He lost everything he had).     <span class="section-anchor" id="wuwu"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">有无</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yǒu wú</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Being &amp; Nonbeing — The Daoist Binary</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="无">
      <div class="scholar-label">哲学洞见 zhéxué dòngjiàn · Philosophical Insight</div>
      <p>In classical Chinese metaphysics — especially Daoism — <strong>有 yǒu</strong> and <strong>无 wú</strong> are not simple opposites but complementary poles of reality. The Daodejing opens: 无名天地之始，有名万物之母 "The nameless is the beginning of Heaven and Earth; the named is the mother of the ten thousand things." 有 and 无 share the same source — they are two aspects of the same Dao, observed from different angles. This is why the Daodejing insists: 有无相生 "Being and nonbeing give rise to each other."</p>
      <p>The practical consequence: 无 wú (without; lacking; non-existence) is not the negation of 有 but its complement and generative partner. A wheel is useful because of the empty hub 无. A room is useful because of the empty space 无. The ancient binary is alive in modern Chinese: 有无 as a compound noun means "whether or not; the presence or absence of"; 无 as a prefix produces literary negation: 无聊 (without interest = bored), 无论 (no matter), 无数 (without number = countless).</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <!-- 无 -->
      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">无</span>
          <span class="card-py">wú</span>
          <span class="card-en">without; lacking; non-existence (classical / literary)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V/Adv 动词/副词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The classical and literary counterpart to 没有. In modern spoken Chinese, 没有 handles negation of 有; 无 wú is largely confined to literary, formal, or fixed-phrase contexts. But 无 remains enormously productive as a compound element — hundreds of words are built with 无: 无聊 bored · 无数 countless · 无论 regardless · 无奈 helpless · 无辜 innocent · 无私 selfless. Learning 无 unlocks a vast tier of the vocabulary.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">无论发生什么，我都会支持你。</div>

            <div class="ex-en">No matter what happens, I will support you.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他对这件事感到无奈。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā duì zhè jiàn shì gǎndào wúnài.</div>

        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">构词 gòucí · 无 as Prefix</span>
          无 + noun/adj creates "without X" compounds: 无聊 wúliáo (bored) · 无私 wúsī (selfless) · 无辜 wúgū (innocent) · 无数 wúshù (countless) · 无奈 wúnài (helpless) · 无情 wúqíng (heartless) · 无论 wúlùn (regardless of). Each is a fixed word — not freely generated in speech, but highly frequent in reading.

      <div class="scholar-label">记忆法 jìyìfǎ · Master Retention Image</div>
      <p>Picture a hand rising from the earth, gripping a piece of meat. That is <strong>有 yǒu</strong>: something grasped, held, real — tangibly in the world. When the hand lets go — 没有 — the meat falls back into nothingness. The empty air where it was is 无.</p>
      <p>But 有 and 无 are not opposites so much as complements — the hand and the empty air between fingers. A fist is useful because it can hold; an open palm is useful because it can receive. 有备无患: having preparation removes disaster. The having and the lacking define each other, generate each other, need each other. The Daodejing knew this. So does the grammar: 有 and 没有 are two faces of the same verb, the same gesture, the same hand over the same void.</p>
