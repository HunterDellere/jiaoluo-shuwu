    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="一">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p>一 is a single horizontal stroke. There is nothing to decompose, no phonetic component, no semantic loan — it <em>is</em> what it depicts: the mark of one. The oracle-bone, bronze-script, and modern forms are identical. No character in the written language has changed less across three thousand years.</p>
      <p>一 is its own radical (部首 bùshǒu), the first in the Kangxi radical sequence, and the root from which — philosophically if not always graphically — all other characters derive. Xu Shen's Shuōwén Jiézì (說文解字, 121 CE) opens with 一 and frames it in explicitly cosmological terms:</p>
      <blockquote style="margin: 1rem 0 0.5rem 0; padding: 0.75rem 1rem; border-left: 3px solid var(--accent); font-style: normal;">
        <div style="font-size: 1.2rem; font-family: 'Noto Serif SC', serif; letter-spacing: 0.05em; margin-bottom: 0.5rem;">一，惟初太始，道立于一，造分天地，化成萬物。</div>
        <div style="color: var(--ink-mid); font-size: 0.92rem;">Yī: the primordial beginning; the Dao is established in One; it divides to create heaven and earth, transforms to form the ten thousand things.</div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>Xu Shen did not describe a number. He described a cosmogonic event — the moment before differentiation, the undivided whole from which the universe unfolds. A character that takes one brushstroke to write carries, in the Chinese written tradition, the weight of everything that follows.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── TONE SANDHI ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="sandhi"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">变调规律</span>
      <span class="sh-py">biàndiào guīlǜ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Tone Sandhi — 一 shifts before all four tones</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">变调规则 biàndiào guīzé · The Complete Rule</span>
      <strong>一 yī + tone 4 syllable</strong> → becomes <strong>yí</strong> (tone 2)<br>
      一个 <strong>yí</strong> gè · 一次 <strong>yí</strong> cì · 一样 <strong>yí</strong> yàng · 一定 <strong>yí</strong> dìng<br><br>
      <strong>一 yī + tone 1, 2, or 3 syllable</strong> → becomes <strong>yì</strong> (tone 4)<br>
      一天 <strong>yì</strong> tiān · 一年 <strong>yì</strong> nián · 一起 <strong>yì</strong> qǐ · 一本 <strong>yì</strong> běn<br><br>
      <strong>一 yī in isolation, at end of phrase, or as ordinal</strong> → stays <strong>yī</strong> (tone 1)<br>
      第一 dì yī (first) · 统一 tǒng yī (unification) · 一 alone
    </div>

    <div class="note">
      <span class="note-label">语音 yǔyīn · Why the Rule Exists</span>
      Tone sandhi for 一 operates on a principle of acoustic contrast: consecutive syllables of the same tone lose their individual contours, making word boundaries harder to perceive. 一 before tone 4 becomes tone 2 to prevent two falling tones colliding (the same logic governs 不 bù → bú before tone 4). Before tones 1, 2, and 3 the shift goes the other way — to tone 4 — creating contrast against a level, rising, or dipping preceding tone. The original tone 1 of 一 is preserved only where no following syllable creates the collision problem. This sandhi is mandatory in standard speech, not optional.
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一个</span>
          <span class="card-py">yí gè</span>
          <span class="card-en">one (with general measure word)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">Meas 量词 liàngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          一 + 个 (general measure word). 一 before tone 4 (个 gè) → yí. The most frequent combination of 一 in everyday speech: 一个人 yí gè rén (one person), 一个问题 yí gè wèntí (one question), 一个地方 yí gè dìfang (one place). 个 gè is the default measure word when no specific one applies.
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一起</span>
          <span class="card-py">yìqǐ</span>
          <span class="card-en">together; all at once</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adv">Adv 副词 fùcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          一 before tone 3 (起 qǐ) → yì. Means "in one place / at one time" — together, jointly. 我们一起去 (let's go together), 一起吃饭 (eat together). Very common in invitations and coordination.
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一定</span>
          <span class="card-py">yídìng</span>
          <span class="card-en">certainly; definitely; a certain (amount)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adv">Adv 副词 fùcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          一 before tone 4 (定 dìng) → yí. As adverb: 你一定要来 (you must definitely come). As adjective: 一定程度上 (to a certain degree). The certainty reading and the "a fixed amount" reading are both live uses.
        </div>
      </div>

    <!-- ── COSMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="cosmology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">宇宙论</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yǔzhòulùn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Cosmology — 道生一 · The Daodejing Passage</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="道">
      <div class="scholar-label">道德经 第四十二章 · Daodejing Chapter 42</div>
      <blockquote style="margin: 0.5rem 0 1rem 0; padding: 0.75rem 1rem; border-left: 3px solid var(--accent); font-style: normal;">
        <div style="font-size: 1.3rem; font-family: 'Noto Serif SC', serif; letter-spacing: 0.08em; line-height: 2; margin-bottom: 0.75rem;">
          道生一，<br>
          一生二，<br>
          二生三，<br>
          三生万物。<br>
          万物负阴而抱阳，<br>
          冲气以为和。
        </div>
        <div style="color: var(--ink-mid); font-size: 0.92rem; line-height: 1.8;">
          Dào shēng yī, yī shēng èr, èr shēng sān, sān shēng wànwù.<br>
          Wànwù fù yīn ér bào yáng, chōng qì yǐ wéi hé.<br><br>
          The Dao generates One. One generates Two. Two generates Three. Three generates the ten thousand things.<br>
          The ten thousand things carry yin on their backs and embrace yang, and through the mixing breath arrive at harmony.
        </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>This is the most compressed cosmogony in classical Chinese literature. The Dao (道) is prior to One — it is the unnamed, undifferentiated ground. One (一) is the first act of the Dao, the primordial unity before division. Two (二) is the split into yin and yang. Three is the interaction of yin, yang, and the breath that mediates them. From Three, everything unfolds.</p>
      <p>一 here is not a number in an arithmetic sequence. It is the cosmological threshold: the moment the formless Dao makes its first mark. Xu Shen's Shuowen gloss — 道立于一，造分天地 — reads Laozi into the character directly. To learn 一 is to encounter this threshold.</p>
      <p>In Confucian thought, 一 appears differently: as the thread of unity. Analects 4.15 records Confucius telling his disciple Zengzi: 吾道一以贯之 — "My way is threaded through by one." The commentarial tradition identifies that one thread as 仁 rén (benevolence). For Confucius, 一 is not cosmological but ethical — the single principle that organizes all conduct.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── PATTERN ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="pattern"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">句型：一…就</span>
      <span class="sh-py">jùxíng</span>
      <span class="sh-en">As Soon As — 一…就 Construction</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">句型结构 jùxíng jiégòu · Pattern Structure</span>
      <strong>一 + [action/condition] + 就 + [result/consequence]</strong><br><br>
      "As soon as [action], then [result]." The 一 anchors the trigger event; 就 signals the immediate consequence. The two clauses share a subject or each has their own. Temporal and conditional at once — the moment X happens, Y follows without interval.<br><br>
      一看<strong>就</strong>懂 · yī kàn jiù dǒng — understand as soon as you look<br>
      一回来<strong>就</strong>打电话 · yī huí lái jiù dǎ diànhuà — call as soon as you get back<br>
      一说<strong>就</strong>笑 · yī shuō jiù xiào — starts laughing as soon as you bring it up
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一看就懂</span>
          <span class="card-py">yī kàn jiù dǒng</span>
          <span class="card-en">understand at a glance; self-explanatory</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V pattern 动词句型</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The prototype 一…就 sentence. Used to describe something immediately clear, a design that needs no instructions, or a person who grasps something with no effort. 这个图表一看就懂 (This chart is self-explanatory).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这本书写得很清楚，一看就懂。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè běn shū xiě de hěn qīngchǔ, yī kàn jiù dǒng.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This book is written very clearly — you understand it as soon as you look.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一走了之</span>
          <span class="card-py">yī zǒu liǎo zhī</span>
          <span class="card-en">to simply leave; to walk away from a problem</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          一 + 走了之 — "just walk off and be done with it." A set phrase for leaving without dealing with the situation. Pejorative: the person who 一走了之 is abandoning responsibility. Used critically: 你不能就这样一走了之 (you can't just walk away like that).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">事情没处理好，他就一走了之了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Shìqíng méi chǔlǐ hǎo, tā jiù yī zǒu liǎo zhī le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Things weren't handled well, and he just walked away.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一到就</span>
          <span class="card-py">yī dào jiù</span>
          <span class="card-en">as soon as [someone/something] arrives</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V pattern 动词句型</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          Productive frame for arrival-triggered actions. 他一到就开始工作 (He started working as soon as he arrived). Pairs naturally with 回来 huí lái (return) and 到家 dào jiā (get home): 一回来就 / 一到家就.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">老师一到，同学们就安静了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Lǎoshī yī dào, tóngxuémen jiù ānjìng le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">As soon as the teacher arrived, the students fell quiet.</div>
          </div>
        </div>

    <!-- ── UNITY COMPOUNDS ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="unity"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">统一构词</span>
      <span class="sh-py">tǒngyī gòucí</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Unity Compounds</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">统一</span>
          <span class="card-py">tǒngyī</span>
          <span class="card-en">unification; to unify; uniform</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-vn">V/N 动名词 dòng míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          统 tǒng (to govern; to bind together) + 一. The political constant of Chinese civilization: 天下统一 (unification of all under heaven). As verb: 统一思想 (unify thinking), 统一标准 (standardize). As adjective: 统一规格 (uniform specifications). The character 一 in this compound carries its full cosmological sense — returning multiplicity to oneness.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">秦始皇统一了六国。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Qínshǐhuáng tǒngyī le liù guó.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">The First Emperor of Qin unified the six kingdoms.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">一致</span>
          <span class="card-py">yīzhì</span>
          <span class="card-en">unanimous; consistent; in agreement</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adj 形容词 xíngróngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          一 + 致 zhì (to reach; to arrive at). All arriving at one point. 意见一致 (unanimous opinion), 行动一致 (acting in concert), 保持一致 (maintain consistency). Higher register than 一样 yīyàng — used in formal and official contexts. 众口一致 (everyone speaking with one mouth = unanimous) is a near-chengyu variant.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">大家意见一致，都同意这个方案。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Dàjiā yìjiàn yīzhì, dōu tóngyì zhège fāng'àn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Everyone was in agreement — all approved the plan.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">唯一</span>
          <span class="card-py">wéiyī</span>
          <span class="card-en">the only one; sole; unique</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adj 形容词 xíngróngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          唯 wéi (only; alone) + 一. Used before nouns: 唯一的选择 (the only choice), 唯一的儿子 (only son). Stronger than 只有 zhǐyǒu in its emphasis on singularity — the uniqueness is total, not merely circumstantial. In formal or literary contexts: 唯一真神 (the one true God, in religious translation).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这是我们唯一的机会，不能放弃。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè shì wǒmen wéiyī de jīhuì, bù néng fàngqì.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This is our only chance — we can't give it up.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">合而为一</span>
          <span class="card-py">hé ér wéi yī</span>
          <span class="card-en">to merge into one; to unite into a single whole</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V phrase 动词短语</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          合 hé (to combine; to close) + 而 ér (and then; thereby) + 为 wéi (become) + 一. A classical four-character phrase describing the merging of separate things into wholeness. Used in philosophy, business mergers, artistic synthesis, and descriptions of cultural fusion. The 而 gives it a classical register absent from 合并 hébìng (to merge).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">两家公司合而为一，共同发展。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Liǎng jiā gōngsī hé ér wéi yī, gòngtóng fāzhǎn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">The two companies merged into one and developed together.</div>
          </div>
        </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="chengyu"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">成语</span>
      <span class="sh-py">chéngyǔ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Idioms &amp; Set Phrases</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="chengyu-grid">

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">一以贯之</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yī yǐ guàn zhī</span>
        <span class="cy-en">one thread running through all — a single principle governing everything</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Source: Analects 4.15. Confucius tells Zengzi 吾道一以贯之 — "My way is threaded through by one." When Zengzi explains to other disciples, he names that one thing as 忠恕 zhōngshù (loyalty and reciprocity). The phrase is now used for any single organizing principle that unifies a large body of work or conduct. One of the most quoted four-character expressions from the Lunyu.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">一举两得</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yī jǔ liǎng dé</span>
        <span class="cy-en">one action, two gains — killing two birds with one stone</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: one-raise two-obtain. Describes any action that simultaneously achieves two objectives. More positive in connotation than the English "two birds" — the emphasis is on efficiency and cleverness, not on the metaphor of destruction. 一石二鸟 yī shí èr niǎo (one stone, two birds) is a near-synonym borrowed into Chinese from European languages via Japanese.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">万众一心</span>
        <span class="cy-py">wànzhòng yī xīn</span>
        <span class="cy-en">ten thousand hearts as one — united as one body</span>
        <span class="cy-note">万众 wànzhòng = the ten thousand people / the multitudes. 一心 = one heart / one mind. Used for collective unity in times of national effort or crisis. A rallying phrase in political rhetoric and public discourse — the aspiration that multiplicity collapses back into the cosmological One. Related: 同心同德 tóngxīn tóngdé (same heart, same virtue).</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">一石二鸟</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yī shí èr niǎo</span>
        <span class="cy-en">one stone, two birds — achieving two things at once</span>
        <span class="cy-note">A direct calque of the English idiom via Japanese (一石二鳥), now naturalized in Chinese as a slightly more colloquial alternative to 一举两得. The two idioms are functionally synonymous. 一石二鸟 appears more in casual speech and contemporary writing; 一举两得 has the longer classical pedigree and appears more in formal contexts.</span>
      </div>

    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="一">
      <div class="scholar-label">记忆 jìyì · Memory Anchor</div>
      <p>一 is the simplest mark a brush can make: lift the brush, place it, draw it across the page, lift again. One gesture. In a writing system of hundreds of strokes and thousands of character combinations, 一 is the atom — the element that cannot be reduced further.</p>
      <p>What Laozi found in that stroke: the origin of everything. The Dao generates One, and One generates Two, and from that first division the entire visible world cascades into existence. Every character you will ever learn contains, somewhere inside it, the memory of that first horizontal line — because 一 is radical number one, the foundation of the Kangxi classification system that organizes all Chinese characters.</p>
      <p>The tone sandhi of 一 is its own thread running through all of Mandarin pronunciation: before tone 4, it rises to match; before tones 1, 2, 3, it falls to contrast. 一 accommodates every surrounding tone, shifting to preserve acoustic clarity — one rule that governs every encounter. 吾道一以贯之.</p>
