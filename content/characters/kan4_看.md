    <!-- SECTION: Etymology -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="看">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p><strong>看 = 𠂇</strong> (an abbreviated form of 手 shǒu, hand — the three-fingered variant that sits above the eye component) <strong>+ 目 mù</strong> (eye). The hand positioned horizontally above the eye: the gesture of shading the brow to scan into the distance, cutting glare so the eye can reach further. Oracle bone and seal script forms confirm this directly — the hand floats above the eye in both, the two components in exactly the relationship the meaning demands.</p>
      <p>What the character encodes is effort. Ordinary seeing (见 jiàn) requires no gesture; the image simply arrives. 看 requires the raised hand, the deliberate positioning of the body, the active direction of attention toward something. The hand is not incidental to the etymology — it is the etymology. Remove the hand and 看 becomes just 目, the passive organ. The hand is what makes it looking rather than merely seeing.</p>
      <p>The 目 mù radical anchors 看 in the large family of vision-related characters. 眼 yǎn (eye, the everyday word for the physical organ), 盲 máng (blind), 睡 shuì (to sleep — eyes pressed shut), 眨 zhǎ (to blink), 瞪 dèng (to stare), 瞥 piē (to glance quickly). 看 sits at the center of this cluster as the most common action word — the default verb for directed visual attention in every register of modern Mandarin.</p>
      <p>The tone variant kān (first tone) appears in a narrower set of compounds with a watching-over, guarding sense: 看守 kānshǒu (to guard, to watch over — as a prison guard), 看护 kānhù (to nurse, to tend a patient). In these compounds the visual attention is sustained and protective rather than episodic. The semantic split between kàn (to look at something) and kān (to watch over something) is productive and worth tracking.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- SECTION: 看 vs 见 -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="vsjiàn"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">看 vs 见</span>
      <span class="sh-py">kàn vs jiàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Process vs Result — Active Looking and Passive Seeing</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="看">
      <div class="scholar-label">语法洞见 yǔfǎ dòngjiàn · Grammar Insight</div>
      <p>看 and 见 describe two different relationships between the looker and the seen. 看 is the <em>process</em>: the directed, sustained act of turning attention toward something. 见 is the <em>result</em>: the moment of contact when something is registered by the eye — or, by extension, by the mind in the case of meeting (见面 jiànmiàn, to meet face-to-face). 看 can fail; 见 cannot. You can look without seeing, but you cannot "see" without something actually arriving.</p>
      <p>This is why the resultative complement 看见 kànjiàn works as a unit: 看 (the looking effort) + 见 (the result of contact) = to catch sight of, to see successfully. The pair 我看了但没看见 (Wǒ kàn le dàn méi kànjiàn — I looked but didn't see) shows the logic explicitly: the effort happened (看了) but the result did not arrive (没看见). The complement 见 functions here exactly as English "see" does in "I looked but didn't see it."</p>
      <p>见 also carries a social meaning that 看 does not: to meet, to receive, to have an audience with. 见朋友 (to meet a friend) vs 看朋友 (to visit a friend, to check on someone). The difference is subtle but real — 看朋友 has a caring, caretaking quality; 见朋友 is the encounter itself. Classical Chinese uses 见 as a passive marker in some constructions (被...所见 = to be seen by), an archaic function not relevant to modern learners but worth noting for reading texts.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">看 vs 见 — process and result, with contrasting examples</span>
      <strong>看 kàn</strong> — the act of looking; directed visual attention (the process)<br>
      <strong>见 jiàn</strong> — to see; to catch sight of; to meet (the result or instantaneous contact)<br>
      <strong>看见 kànjiàn</strong> — to catch sight of; to successfully see (看 effort + 见 result)<br>
      <strong>我看了但没看见。</strong> — I looked but didn't see it. (effort without result)<br>
      <strong>我看书。</strong> — I'm reading. &nbsp;&nbsp; <strong>我见到他了。</strong> — I (managed to) meet him.
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="opinion"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">看法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">kànfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Opinion &amp; Judgment — 看 as Cognitive Evaluation</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="看">
      <div class="scholar-label">看 as mental stance — from visual to evaluative</div>
      <p>The move from physical looking to mental evaluation is not metaphorical in Chinese — it is lexicalized directly in the compounds. 看法 kànfǎ (viewpoint, opinion: literally "the method/way of looking") treats a held view as a particular angle of seeing. 我看 wǒ kàn (in my view, I think) introduces opinions in conversation the way "I see it as" does in English: the speaker is claiming a visual angle, a vantage point, not just a conclusion.</p>
      <p>The evaluative extensions of 看 run across a spectrum of social judgment. 看好 kànhǎo (to favor, to back, to see as likely to succeed — literally "to look at [it] as good") is used for investment, sports predictions, and support. 看不起 kànbuqǐ (to look down on, to disdain — literally "cannot look up at") codes contempt as the failure to raise the eyes high enough to meet someone. 看穿 kànchuān (to see through — literally "to look through [something solid]") describes the ability to read through a performance or deception to the reality beneath.</p>
    </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">走马观花</span>
        <span class="cy-py">zǒu mǎ guān huā</span>
        <span class="cy-en">"look at flowers from a galloping horse" — a superficial, rushing view</span>
        <span class="cy-note">走马 (galloping horse) + 观花 (observe flowers). The mounted rider moves too fast to see the flowers properly — their gaze skims the surface without stopping to appreciate what is there. A criticism of rushed, inattentive engagement with a subject. Used for tourists who photograph rather than observe, students who skim rather than read, travelers who visit without encountering. 观 guān carries a more deliberate, elevated register than 看: this phrase uses the elevated verb to expose its failure in practice.</span>
      </div>
      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">雾里看花</span>
        <span class="cy-py">wù lǐ kàn huā</span>
        <span class="cy-en">"looking at flowers through mist" — seeing something obscurely, without clarity</span>
        <span class="cy-note">雾 (mist, fog) + 里 (inside, within) + 看花 (looking at flowers). The object of perception is real, but the medium prevents clear sight. Used for situations where the truth is obscured — murky political signals, ambiguous intentions, confusing circumstances. The phrase comes from the Tang poet 元稹's poem on growing old, in which diminished faculties produce this feeling of looking through fog. Today it describes institutional opacity, mixed messages, and deliberately obscured information.</span>
      </div>
      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">一目了然</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yī mù liǎo rán</span>
        <span class="cy-en">"one glance makes everything clear" — immediately obvious at a single look</span>
        <span class="cy-note">一目 (one glance, one eye-sweep) + 了然 (completely clear, thoroughly understood). The 目 connects directly to 看's radical, and the phrase is its opposite emotional pole from 雾里看花: where that is confusion, this is instant total clarity. Used for well-designed presentations, obvious solutions, transparent situations. 图表一目了然 — the chart makes everything immediately clear. The single eye-sweep achieves complete understanding: maximum seeing, minimal effort.</span>
      </div>
      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">刮目相看</span>
        <span class="cy-py">guā mù xiāng kàn</span>
        <span class="cy-en">"wipe the eyes and look again" — to see someone with new respect after they've changed</span>
        <span class="cy-note">刮目 (to wipe / rub the eyes) + 相看 (to look at each other; to regard). Clear your eyes and look again at the person in front of you — because they are no longer who you thought they were. From the Three Kingdoms story of Lü Meng 吕蒙, a general dismissed as unlearned, who returned after intensive study utterly transformed. Sun Quan told the scholar Lü Su to 刮目相待 — stop looking with the old eyes. The idiom encodes the idea that people can outgrow the categories others put them in.</span>

      <div class="scholar-label">记忆法 jìyìfǎ · Master Retention Image</div>
      <p>The gesture is the character. Raise your hand above your eyes and squint toward the horizon — you are performing 看. Every person who has ever shaded their eyes against sun-glare on water, on snow, on an open field, has made this character with their body. The ancient scribes who encoded it did not abstract the action. They drew the hand directly above the eye, and that drawing is what survived into the modern form.</p>
      <p>The range of 看's meanings fans out from this gesture. To look at a book is to direct that same visual attention downward onto a page. To watch television is to hold it against a screen. To visit a friend is to turn it toward a person you care about. To see a doctor is to apply it, simultaneously, from both sides of the examination room. To hold a 看法 (viewpoint) is to maintain a stable angle from which you see the world. The hand over the eye — scanning, effortful, directed — is present in every extension.</p>
      <p>The contrast with 见 jiàn sharpens the meaning further. 见 is what happens when something arrives at the eye without effort. 看 is the effort itself. The character for the act of looking carries the instrument of effort — the hand — built into its structure, so that looking is never passive, never accidental. Every 看 is a choice to attend.</p>
