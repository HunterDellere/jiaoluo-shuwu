    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology — To Use, To Carry</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="以">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p>In oracle bone and bronze script, <strong>以</strong> shows a person (人) carrying or leading something — the act of taking by hand, guiding forward, employing. The core sense is <em>to use, to take, to employ.</em> The <em>Shuōwén Jiězì</em> defines 以 as <strong>用也</strong> — "to use."</p>
      <p>As a preposition, 以 marks the instrument or means by which an action is carried out: 以刀切 (cut with a knife), 以德报怨 (repay with virtue). As a conjunction, it marks purpose or cause: 以防万一 (in order to guard against any eventuality). Both functions descend directly from the root sense of "employing" one thing in the service of another.</p>
      <p>In modern Mandarin, 以 has retreated significantly into formal and written registers. Colloquial speech replaces most 以 + verb constructions with 用 (to use), 因为 (because), or 为了 (in order to). But 以 persists — embedded in dozens of fixed compounds, in classical quotation, and in the formal prose of newspapers, law, and academia.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── 以…为 CONSTRUCTION ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="yiwei"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">以…为</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yǐ X wéi Y</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The 以…为 Construction — Taking X as Y</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="以">
      <div class="scholar-label">核心句型 héxīn jùxíng · Core Construction</div>
      <p>The most productive classical pattern using 以 is <strong>以 X 为 Y</strong> — "to take X as Y; to treat X as Y; to regard X as Y; to use X in the role of Y." This construction is the engine of classical Chinese argument, the standard way of asserting a standard or a valuation.</p>
      <p><strong>以天下为己任</strong> yǐ tiānxià wéi jǐ rèn — to take all under heaven as one personal responsibility. The defining aspiration of the Confucian public servant: the world is not someone else's problem.</p>
      <p><strong>以和为贵</strong> yǐ hé wéi guì — to take harmony as the highest value. From the Analects I.12 (有子): 礼之用，和为贵 — "In the application of ritual, harmony is what matters most." The most cited Confucian statement on why social harmony is the end toward which ritual points.</p>
      <p><strong>以德报怨</strong> yǐ dé bào yuàn — to repay resentment with virtue. This phrase crosses Laozi and the Analects. Confucius in the Analects (XIV.36) responds with characteristic concreteness: "What then would you use to repay virtue? Repay resentment with uprightness (以直报怨), and repay virtue with virtue." A direct, contested ethical position, not a simple endorsement of unlimited forgiveness.</p>
      <p><strong>以人为本</strong> yǐ rén wéi běn — to take the human as the root and foundation. Central to modern Chinese social policy framing, the phrase has deep classical roots: the Guanzi reads 夫霸王之所始也，以人为本 — "The beginning of kingship lies in taking the people as the root."</p>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">以…为 句型 jùxíng · Construction Template</span>
      <strong>以 + [X] + 为 + [Y]</strong> → to take X as / treat X as / regard X as Y<br>
      以天下为己任 — take the world as personal responsibility<br>
      以和为贵 — take harmony as what is precious<br>
      以德报怨 — repay (using virtue) the resentment<br>
      以人为本 — take the person as the root<br>
      以身作则 — set a standard through one body
    </div>

    <!-- ── 以 AS PREPOSITION ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="preposition"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">介词用法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">jièci yòngfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">以 as Preposition — By Means Of and In Order To</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="以">
      <div class="scholar-label">两种介词功能 liǎng zhǒng jièci gōngnéng · Two Prepositional Functions</div>
      <p><strong>以 + noun phrase = by means of / using / with.</strong> This is 以 marking the instrument or method: 以笔为剑 (using the brush as a sword), 以理服人 (to convince people by means of reason). In classical texts this construction appears constantly. In modern formal writing it persists for rhetorical weight: 以实际行动证明 (to prove through practical action).</p>
      <p><strong>以 + verb phrase = in order to / so as to.</strong> Here 以 marks purpose — what the action is aimed at achieving. This is the source of the productive set of 以 + verb compounds in modern Chinese: 以免 (in order to avoid), 以便 (in order to facilitate), 以防 (as a precaution against). These three are fully modern and appear in ordinary written language.</p>
    </div>

      <!-- 以身作则 -->
      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">以身作则</span>
          <span class="card-py">yǐ shēn zuò zé</span>
          <span class="card-en">to set an example through personal conduct; to lead by example</span>
        </div>

        <div class="card-def">

        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">作为父母，要以身作则。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zuòwéi fùmǔ, yào yǐ shēn zuò zé.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">As parents, one must lead by personal example.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">领导者应该以身作则，才能赢得下属的尊重。</div>

            <div class="ex-en">Leaders should lead by example, only then can they earn the respect of those below them.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">文化 wénhuà · Cultural Note</span>
          以身作则 is among the most-cited Confucian leadership principles in contemporary Chinese management writing, parenting discourse, and political speeches. The Confucian ideal of 正名 (rectifying names) — that titles must correspond to actual conduct — makes this phrase the practical expression of that principle.

      <!-- 以为 -->
      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">以为</span>
          <span class="card-py">yǐwéi</span>
          <span class="card-en">to (mistakenly) think; to assume incorrectly</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          以 (taking) + 为 (to be; to regard as). In classical Chinese the construction meant "to regard X as Y." In modern speech, 以为 has narrowed to almost always imply that the assumption turned out to be wrong — making it a useful signal of correction or disappointment.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我以为他走了，结果他还在。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ yǐwéi tā zǒu le, jiéguǒ tā hái zài.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I thought he had left, but it turned out he was still there.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">你以为我不知道？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Nǐ yǐwéi wǒ bù zhīdào?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">You assumed I did not know?</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">辨析 biànxī · 以为 vs 认为</span>
          <strong>以为</strong> often implies the belief was wrong — "I thought (but I was mistaken)." <strong>认为</strong> rènwéi is neutral — "I think, I consider, I am of the view that" — with no implication of error. Use 认为 for genuine opinions; 以为 for assumptions that turned out to be incorrect.
        </div>
      </div>

      <!-- 所以 -->
      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">所以</span>
          <span class="card-py">suǒyǐ</span>
          <span class="card-en">therefore; so; that is why</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">Conj 连词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          所 (that which) + 以 (the means/reason by which). The most common consequential conjunction in modern Chinese. Follows a cause clause (often introduced by 因为) and introduces the result: 因为下雨，所以我没去 (because it rained, so I did not go).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">因为堵车，所以我迟到了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Yīnwèi dǔchē, suǒyǐ wǒ chídào le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Because of the traffic jam, I was late.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这就是我所以来的原因。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè jiù shì wǒ suǒyǐ lái de yuányīn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This is the reason I came.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">语法 yǔfǎ · Pattern</span>
          Standard pairing: <code>因为 A，所以 B</code>. In speech, either 因为 or 所以 can be dropped; both together is emphatic or formal. In classical Chinese, 所以 meant "that by which / the means by which" — the modern consequential meaning is a grammaticalization of that original sense.
        </div>
      </div>

    <!-- ── CLASSICAL REGISTER ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="classical"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">文言用法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">wényán yòngfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Classical Register — 以 in Literary Chinese</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="以">
      <div class="scholar-label">文言文 wényánwén · Literary Chinese</div>
      <p>In classical Chinese (文言文), 以 has a wider and more syntactically flexible role than in modern speech. Several patterns appear repeatedly in canonical texts:</p>
      <p><strong>以…故</strong> yǐ…gù — "because of; for the reason of." A common causal connector in classical prose: 以此故 (for this reason), 以贫故不仕 (because of poverty, one did not take office). In modern Chinese, 因此 and 所以 cover this ground.</p>
      <p><strong>Sentence-final and medial 以 marking means just used.</strong> In Mencius: 申之以孝悌之义 — "Instilling in them the principles of filial piety and brotherly respect [by these means]." The 以 phrase comes after the main verb, specifying the instrument of the action. This post-verbal position is a classical feature that modern Chinese inverts: means phrases now precede the verb.</p>
      <p><strong>以此</strong> yǐ cǐ — "by means of this; therefore" — a formal connector drawing a consequence from what was just stated.</p>
      <p><strong>Classical proverb structure: 以 X 喻 Y</strong> — to use X as an illustration of Y. The standard formula for analogy in classical argument.</p>
      <p>The Analects compound 一言以蔽之 yī yán yǐ bì zhī — "to cover it all with one word; to sum up in a phrase" — preserves the classical 以 + verb structure intact and is still used in modern formal writing as a rhetorical opener.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">成语</span>

    <div class="chengyu-grid">

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">以德报怨</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yǐ dé bào yuàn</span>
        <span class="cy-en">to repay resentment with virtue</span>
        <span class="cy-note">以 (using) + 德 (virtue) + 报 (to repay) + 怨 (resentment). Appears in the Daodejing (ch. 63) and the Analects (XIV.36). In the Analects, Confucius pushes back against it directly: "What then do you use to repay virtue? Repay resentment with uprightness (以直报怨), repay virtue with virtue." A deliberately contested ethical principle, not a simple maxim.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">以身作则</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yǐ shēn zuò zé</span>
        <span class="cy-en">to set a standard through personal conduct — lead by example</span>
        <span class="cy-note">以 (by means of) + 身 (one whole person; conduct) + 作则 (set a rule/standard). 身 is the entire person understood as embodied conduct — the leader is not separate from the example set. Among the most-cited leadership principles in Chinese management and parenting discourse. 作为领导者，必须以身作则。(As a leader, one must lead by example.)</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">以和为贵</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yǐ hé wéi guì</span>
        <span class="cy-en">to hold harmony as precious — harmony as the highest value</span>
        <span class="cy-note">From the Analects I.12 (有子's saying): 礼之用，和为贵，先王之道，斯为美。 — "In the application of ritual, harmony is what is precious; this is the beauty of the ways of the former kings." Often cited as the Confucian reason why formal rules (礼) exist: they are instruments toward harmony, not ends in themselves.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">一言以蔽之</span>
        <span class="cy-py">yī yán yǐ bì zhī</span>
        <span class="cy-en">to cover it all in one word — in a word; to sum up</span>
        <span class="cy-note">From the Analects II.2, where Confucius summarizes the 300 poems of the Shijing: 诗三百，一言以蔽之，曰思无邪。 — "The three hundred poems can be covered in one phrase: 'no depraved thoughts.'" 以 marks the instrument (one word) used to accomplish the action (cover/sum up). Still used today as a formal rhetorical opener for summaries.</span>
      </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="以" style="margin-top:2.5rem;">
      <div class="scholar-label">记忆法 jìyìfǎ · Master Retention Image</div>
      <p>Picture 以 as a hand extended outward, offering one thing in service of another. That gesture — "here, use this" — is the root of everything 以 does. When 以 precedes a noun it says: "here is the instrument." When 以 precedes a verb phrase it says: "here is the purpose." When 以 anchors 以…为 it says: "take this, treat it as that."</p>
      <p>The three chengyu that carry 以 deepest into Chinese civilization all share this logic. 以身作则: here is the whole self, offered as the standard. 以德报怨: here is virtue, offered in return for resentment. 以和为贵: here is harmony, elevated into what is held precious. One character, one gesture, centuries of ethical argument.</p>
