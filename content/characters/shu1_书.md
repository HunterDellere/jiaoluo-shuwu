    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="書">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p>The traditional form <strong>書</strong> shows: a hand (聿 yù — a brush held in the hand) over 曰 yuē (to say; speech). The brush held above speech: <em>the transcription of words.</em> 聿 alone means "writing instrument" — it is the original brush. The simplified 书 retains only an abstracted hook form, but the calligraphic origin is still felt in the stroke.</p>
      <p>书 covers the entire semantic field of written civilization: the physical book, the act of writing, the art of calligraphy, official documents, personal letters, and Scripture (经书 jīngshū). In classical Chinese, a 书 could be a letter, a governmental memorial, a canonical text, or a style of script. All were facets of the same civilizational act: using a brush to preserve thought.</p>
      <p>The simplified form appeared in the People's Republic's 1956 and 1964 script reform, reducing 書 from 10 strokes to 4. The traditional form 書 is still used in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and formal calligraphy contexts.</p>
    </div>

            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ zài túshūguǎn jiè le zhè běn shū.</div>

        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          书 shū + 屋 wū (room; small dwelling). A room given over to books — intimate, personal, unlarge. The word appears in study names of scholars and poets: 鲁迅的百草园 is a garden; his workroom was a 书屋. It is also the second half of this site's name: 角落書屋.

        <div class="examples">

            <div class="ex-en">He read in his study for an entire day.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

        <div class="card-head">

          <span class="card-py">dúshū</span>
          <span class="card-en">to study; to go to school; to read books</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-vn">V/N 动名词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          读 dú (to read aloud; to study) + 书 shū. The standard word for education and study — not just reading but the whole project of cultivation through books. 读书人 dúshūrén = a scholar; a literate, cultivated person. One of the highest social compliments in the Confucian tradition.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">

            <div class="ex-en">Education changes fate. <em>[common saying]</em></div>

    <!-- ── WRITING & SCRIPTS ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="writing"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">书写与字体</span>
      <span class="sh-py">shūxiě yǔ zìtǐ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Writing &amp; The Five Scripts</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="書">
      <div class="scholar-label">五体 wǔtǐ · The Five Scripts</div>
      <p>Chinese calligraphy tradition recognizes five script styles (书体 shūtǐ), each with its own aesthetic character and historical moment:</p>
      <p><strong>篆书 zhuànshū</strong> (seal script) — the oldest, rounded and archaic; used in seals and formal inscriptions. <strong>隶书 lìshū</strong> (clerical script) — the Han dynasty civil-service hand; flatter, with horizontal flicks. <strong>楷书 kǎishū</strong> (regular/standard script) — the basis for printed type and school learning; what you're reading now. <strong>行书 xíngshū</strong> (running script) — the everyday handwriting style; connected, fluid. <strong>草书 cǎoshū</strong> (cursive/grass script) — the most abstract; strokes reduced to near-symbols; readable only by trained calligraphers.</p>
      <p>A skilled calligrapher moves between all five. The five scripts are one of China's most distinctive contributions to world art.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── CALLIGRAPHY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="calligraphy"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">书法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">shūfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Calligraphy — The Method of the Brush</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-n">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">书法</span>
          <span class="card-py">shūfǎ</span>
          <span class="card-en">calligraphy; the art of Chinese writing</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          书 shū (writing) + 法 fǎ (method; law; the Dharma). The <em>method</em> — or the <em>law</em> — of writing. Calligraphy is not decoration in Chinese culture; it is an ethical practice. The quality of one's brushwork was believed to reveal character. The great calligraphers (王羲之 Wáng Xīzhī, 颜真卿 Yán Zhēnqīng, 苏轼 Sū Shì) are remembered alongside poets and philosophers.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">书法是中国传统艺术的精髓。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Shūfǎ shì Zhōngguó chuántǒng yìshù de jīngsuǐ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Calligraphy is the essence of traditional Chinese art.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">文化 wénhuà · Culture</span>
          书法 is listed as a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage. It is taught in Chinese schools and practiced by millions as a meditative discipline. The brush is the instrument; stillness and breath are the preparation; the stroke cannot be corrected.
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-n">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">书法家</span>
          <span class="card-py">shūfǎjiā</span>
          <span class="card-en">calligrapher</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          书法 shūfǎ + 家 jiā (specialist; master of a domain). A practitioner of calligraphy at the level of mastery. 王羲之 Wáng Xīzhī (4th c.) is the canonical master — his 兰亭序 Lántíng Xù (Preface to the Orchid Pavilion) is the most celebrated piece of Chinese calligraphy.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">王羲之是历史上最伟大的书法家。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wáng Xīzhī shì lìshǐ shàng zuì wěidà de shūfǎjiā.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Wang Xizhi is the greatest calligrapher in history.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

    <div class="chengyu-grid">

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">书香门第</span>
        <span class="cy-py">shūxiāng méndì</span>
        <span class="cy-en">a family with a fragrance of books — a family of scholars and learning</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: book-fragrance-door-lineage. A family where books are the tradition and education runs through the generations. High cultural prestige in Chinese society. 书香 shūxiāng "the fragrance of books" is itself a vivid compound.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">读万卷书，行万里路</span>
        <span class="cy-py">dú wàn juǎn shū, xíng wàn lǐ lù</span>
        <span class="cy-en">read ten thousand scrolls; travel ten thousand li — theory and experience together</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Classical saying attributed to Du Fu's father. 万 = 10,000 (metaphor for "vast"). The principle that education requires both books and lived experience — one of the most quoted educational proverbs.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">手不释卷</span>
        <span class="cy-py">shǒu bù shì juǎn</span>
        <span class="cy-en">hand never releasing the scroll — to be an insatiable reader</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: hand-not-release-scroll. To be so devoted to books that you never put them down. A compliment for scholars. 卷 juǎn = scroll; the classical form of the book.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">学富五车</span>
        <span class="cy-py">xué fù wǔ chē</span>
        <span class="cy-en">learning filling five carts — to be vastly learned</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Pre-paper era idiom: a scholar's books required five ox-carts to transport. Now means "enormously knowledgeable." The scale reveals how precious books were before printing.</span>
