    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="見">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · an eye on legs</div>
      <p>The traditional graph 見 stacks two parts: 目 (mù, eye) on top of 儿 (rén, the standing-person component, a variant of 人 used as a base). The whole character is a person with the eye drawn oversized, sight pictured as the act of a body, not just an organ. The oracle-bone form makes the picture even plainer: a kneeling or standing figure with a single huge eye for a head. Seeing, in the visual logic of the script, is the body turned into one big sense organ.</p>
      <p>The Shuōwén Jiězì glosses 見 as 視也 (shì yě, "to look at"), but Chinese kept a distinction the gloss flattens. 看 (kàn) is the active verb of looking, the directed gaze. 見 / 见 is the verb of result, what registered, what landed. 我看 means I look. 我看见 means I looked and the seeing took. The same split runs through 听 / 听见 (listen / hear), 找 / 找到 (search / find), 学 / 学会 (study / master). Chinese marks the moment a directed action achieves its object, and 见 is one of the oldest of those result markers.</p>
      <p>From that core, the meaning radiated. To see a person became to meet them (见面 jiànmiàn). What one has seen, the accumulated record of a life's looking, became opinion (意见 yìjiàn) and insight (见解 jiǎnjiě). When a thing comes into view from hiding, it is said 见; the verb flips into intransitive showing-forth (出现 chūxiàn carries the modern sense, but classical 見 already did). Read with the second pinyin 现 (xiàn), the same graph means "to appear, to manifest," a reading preserved in the chengyu 图穷匕见 (tú qióng bǐ xiàn, "the map rolled out, the dagger appeared").</p>
      <p>The simplified 见 keeps the structure of the traditional but redraws the eye in cursive shorthand: the four-stroke shape that now serves as its own radical. Japanese inherits 見 with the readings <em>ken</em> (意見 iken, opinion) and <em>mi(ru)</em> (見る, to see). Korean keeps 견 (gyeon). Vietnamese reads <em>kiến</em>. The character travels with its full cluster of senses intact: see, meet, view, appear.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── SEEING ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="seeing"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">看见</span>
      <span class="sh-py">kànjiàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Seeing &amp; Perceiving, 看 vs 见</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">verb + 见 · "managed to perceive"</span>
      <p>见 attaches to a perception verb to mark that the perception succeeded. The pattern is fully productive across the senses:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>看 (kàn, look) + 见 → 看见 (kànjiàn, to see, to spot)</li>
        <li>听 (tīng, listen) + 见 → 听见 (tīngjiàn, to hear)</li>
        <li>闻 (wén, smell/sniff) + 见 → 闻见 (wénjiàn, to catch a scent)</li>
        <li>梦 (mèng, dream) + 见 → 梦见 (mèngjiàn, to see in a dream)</li>
        <li>遇 (yù, encounter) + 见 → 遇见 (yùjiàn, to run into)</li>
      </ul>
      <p>The bare verbs (看, 听) describe the activity. The verb-见 compounds describe the moment something registered. 我听了，但是没听见 (Wǒ tīng le, dànshì méi tīngjiàn): "I was listening, but I didn't hear it." The negation lands on the result, not the activity.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">v</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">看见</span>
          <span class="card-py">kànjiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">to see, to spot</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">看 (look) + 见 (perceive). The everyday verb of seeing as result. Use it when something entered your field of vision and registered. Past-tense scenarios use it constantly: 你看见我的钥匙了吗？(Nǐ kànjiàn wǒ de yàoshi le ma?), "Have you seen my keys?"</p>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我在街上看见他了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ zài jiē shàng kànjiàn tā le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I saw him on the street.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">天太黑，什么也看不见。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tiān tài hēi, shénme yě kàn bù jiàn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">It's so dark you can't see a thing.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note"><strong>看 vs 看见.</strong> 看电视 (kàn diànshì) is to watch TV, the activity. 看见电视 is to spot the TV (in a furniture store, behind a curtain), the result. Films, books, and shows take 看; objects entering your view take 看见.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">v</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">听见</span>
          <span class="card-py">tīngjiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">to hear</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">听 (listen) + 见. Same logic in the auditory channel. 听 is the activity (to listen, attend); 听见 is the result (the sound landed). 没听见 (méi tīngjiàn), "didn't catch that," is the gentler way to ask someone to repeat themselves.</p>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">外面有人，你听见了吗？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wàimiàn yǒu rén, nǐ tīngjiàn le ma?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">There's someone outside, did you hear?</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">v</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">梦见</span>
          <span class="card-py">mèngjiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">to see in a dream, to dream of</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">梦 (dream) + 见. The verb for dreaming about a specific thing or person. 我昨晚梦见了你 (Wǒ zuówǎn mèngjiàn le nǐ): "I dreamed about you last night." The 见 marks that the dream-vision actually appeared, not just that you slept.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

    <!-- ── MEETING ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="meeting"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">见面</span>
      <span class="sh-py">jiànmiàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Meeting People · 见 as encounter</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="面">
      <div class="scholar-label">见 + 面 · seeing the face</div>
      <p>To meet someone in Chinese is, literally, to see their face. 见面 (jiànmiàn) is the standard verb: 见 (see) + 面 (miàn, face). The compound foregrounds the visual encounter, not the appointment or the conversation. You can plan a meeting, schedule a meeting, talk on the phone for hours, yet until the faces are in the same room, 见面 has not happened.</p>
      <p>The verb separates around its object the way English splits "make sense" with "make any sense": 我们什么时候见面？(Wǒmen shénme shíhòu jiànmiàn?), "When are we meeting?" 见过面 (jiànguò miàn), "have met before." 第一次见面 (dì yī cì jiànmiàn), "the first time we met." For the meeting itself as event, 会面 (huìmiàn) and 会议 (huìyì, formal meeting) take over; 见面 stays close to the personal, face-to-face register.</p>
      <p>Two adjacent set phrases live here. 再见 (zàijiàn, "see again") is the standard goodbye, with the optimism baked in that there will be a next time. 久仰大名，今日得见 (jiǔ yǎng dà míng, jīnrì dé jiàn), "long admired your great name, today I get to see you," is the formal opening when meeting someone whose reputation preceded them. Both compress the whole social act of meeting into the single verb 见.</p>
    </div>

        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">v</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">再见</span>
          <span class="card-py">zàijiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">goodbye (lit. "see again")</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">再 (again) + 见. The neutral, all-register goodbye. Built on the same assumption every culture's farewell formula carries: that this is not the last time. Casual Mandarin also reaches for 拜拜 (bàibài, from English "bye-bye") in friendly contexts, but 再见 covers everything from the corner shop to a state visit.</p>

        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">v</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">遇见</span>
          <span class="card-py">yùjiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">to run into, to encounter</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">遇 (meet by chance) + 见. The unplanned meeting. Use it for bumping into an old classmate at the supermarket, finding a stray cat, encountering a problem you did not foresee. The chance flavor is built in: scheduled meetings take 见面, not 遇见.</p>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我在地铁上遇见了一位老朋友。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ zài dìtiě shàng yùjiàn le yí wèi lǎo péngyǒu.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I ran into an old friend on the subway.</div>

    <!-- ── OPINION ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="opinion"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">意见</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yìjiàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Opinion &amp; Judgement · what one has seen</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="意">
      <div class="scholar-label">见 as the record of looking</div>
      <p>An opinion in Chinese is what you have seen. 意见 (yìjiàn) joins 意 (yì, intent, mind) with 见 (sight, view): the mind's view, the position one has arrived at by looking. Across Confucian and modern usage the framing holds: a 见 is a take that came from somewhere, not a feeling that floated up. To have an opinion is to claim you have looked.</p>
      <p>The vocabulary tree from this root is dense. 见解 (jiànjiě, "seeing-and-untangling") is an interpretation, an analytical view. 意见 (yìjiàn) is the everyday opinion, the one you offer in a meeting. 偏见 (piānjiàn, "slanted seeing") is prejudice, sight gone crooked. 远见 (yuǎnjiàn, "far seeing") is foresight. 浅见 (qiǎnjiàn, "shallow seeing") is the modest hedge a writer uses for their own view. 主见 (zhǔjiàn) is the capacity to hold your own view under pressure; lacking it (没有主见) is one of the more cutting things a Chinese speaker can say about a person.</p>
      <p>The Analects 16.10 places 见 at the center of the gentleman's discipline: 君子有九思…見得思義 (jūnzǐ yǒu jiǔ sī… jiàn dé sī yì), "the gentleman has nine considerations… when he sees gain, he thinks of righteousness." Confucius does not begin with feeling or with rule, but with the act of looking and what the look summons in the mind that has been trained.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">n</span></div>

          <span class="card-cn">意见</span>

          <span class="card-en">opinion; suggestion; complaint</span>

        <p class="card-def">意 (mind, intent) + 见. The ordinary noun for opinion. Note the second meaning: 对…有意见 (duì… yǒu yìjiàn) means "to have an issue with" someone or something. 老板对你有意见, "the boss has a complaint about you." Whether 意见 is neutral suggestion or pointed criticism depends entirely on the verb pattern around it.</p>

          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">大家有什么意见，请直接说。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Dàjiā yǒu shénme yìjiàn, qǐng zhíjiē shuō.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">If anyone has thoughts, please say so directly.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">n</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">见解</span>
          <span class="card-py">jiànjiě</span>
          <span class="card-en">view, interpretation, take</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">见 + 解 (untangle, explain). A more bookish, considered word than 意见. 独到的见解 (dúdào de jiànjiě), "an original take." Used for the substantive position a scholar, critic, or thoughtful speaker has arrived at, not the show-of-hands kind of opinion.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">n</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">偏见</span>
          <span class="card-py">piānjiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">prejudice, bias</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">偏 (slanted, off-center) + 见. Sight that has tilted off true. 对…有偏见, "to be prejudiced against." A fully modern word that sits unchanged at the heart of debates about discrimination, fairness, and journalism.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">n</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">远见</span>
          <span class="card-py">yuǎnjiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">foresight, long-range vision</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">远 (far) + 见. The capacity to see what is coming. Praise reserved for leaders, planners, and investors who called something correctly years before the rest. 有远见 (yǒu yuǎnjiàn), "has foresight," is one of the highest compliments in business and political commentary.</p>
      </div>

    <!-- ── APPEARING ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="appearing"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">出现</span>
      <span class="sh-py">chūxiàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Appearing &amp; Visibility · 见 as showing forth</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">见 / 现 · the same graph, two readings</span>
      <p>Read 见 with its second pronunciation 现 (xiàn) and the verb flips from "to see" to "to appear, to make visible." Modern Chinese splits the two readings between two graphs (见 jiàn for seeing, 现 xiàn for appearing), but classical and idiomatic uses keep both readings under the original 見:</p>
      <ul>
        <li>出现 (chūxiàn): to appear, to come out</li>
        <li>发现 (fāxiàn): to discover</li>
        <li>表现 (biǎoxiàn): to show, to display, performance</li>
        <li>实现 (shíxiàn): to realize, to bring into being</li>
        <li>现在 (xiànzài): now (literally, "appearing-existing")</li>
      </ul>
      <p>The 现 family runs through every register of modern speech, and every one of them is a descendant of the same 見 image: a thing that was not in view becoming visible. When something 出现s, the world has stepped into your field of seeing.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">v</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">看不见</span>
          <span class="card-py">kàn bù jiàn</span>
          <span class="card-en">cannot see; invisible</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">看 + 不 + 见. The standard potential negation. Marks the inability to perceive, regardless of effort. 太远了，看不见 (Tài yuǎn le, kàn bù jiàn): "Too far, I can't make it out." The positive form 看得见 (kàn de jiàn, "can see") works the same way. Compare to 看不到 (kàn bù dào), which carries the same meaning with slightly different distributional preferences in the south.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">v</span></div>
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">不见了</span>
          <span class="card-py">bú jiàn le</span>
          <span class="card-en">to be missing, to have disappeared</span>
        </div>
        <p class="card-def">不 + 见 + 了. Idiomatic. The thing is no longer in view, therefore gone. 我的钱包不见了 (Wǒ de qiánbāo bú jiàn le): "My wallet's gone." The phrasing is gentler than 丢了 (diū le, "lost"): it leaves open that the object might still turn up.</p>
      </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="chengyu"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">成语</span>
      <span class="sh-py">chéngyǔ</span>

      <span class="sh-rule"></span>

    <div class="chengyu-grid">
      <div class="cy">
        <div class="cy-cn">一见钟情</div>
        <div class="cy-py">yí jiàn zhōng qíng</div>
        <div class="cy-en">"one look, settled feeling": love at first sight</div>
        <div class="cy-note">钟 (zhōng) here is the literary verb "to fix, to concentrate"; 钟情 is to fasten one's affections on. The whole phrase compresses the romance plot into four characters: a single act of seeing, and the heart already decided. Modern usage covers both serious romance and casual enthusiasm; 我对这家咖啡店一见钟情 (I fell for this café the moment I saw it) is unremarkable.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="cy">
        <div class="cy-cn">见义勇为</div>
        <div class="cy-py">jiàn yì yǒng wéi</div>
        <div class="cy-en">"see what is right, act bravely": to do the right thing without hesitation</div>
        <div class="cy-note">From the Analects 2.24: 見義不為，無勇也 (jiàn yì bù wéi, wú yǒng yě), "to see what is right and not do it is cowardice." The chengyu inverts the warning into a praise. Used today for citizens who intervene in street crimes, rescue strangers, or stand up to public wrongs. Many Chinese cities maintain 见义勇为 funds that pay out medical and legal costs for people who acted on the principle.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="cy">
        <div class="cy-cn">图穷匕见</div>
        <div class="cy-py">tú qióng bǐ xiàn</div>
        <div class="cy-en">"the map runs out, the dagger appears": true intentions revealed at the end</div>
        <div class="cy-note">From the Shǐjì account of 荆轲 Jīng Kē, who in 227 BCE attempted to assassinate the future First Emperor of Qin under the cover of presenting a tribute map. As the scroll unrolled to its end, the hidden dagger came into view. Used for any moment when a long pretense finally collapses into its real motive: political, commercial, personal. Note the 见 here reads <em>xiàn</em> (appear), not <em>jiàn</em>, preserving the older reading.</div>
      </div>
      <div class="cy">
        <div class="cy-cn">见仁见智</div>
        <div class="cy-py">jiàn rén jiàn zhì</div>
        <div class="cy-en">"some see benevolence, some see wisdom": opinions legitimately differ</div>
        <div class="cy-note">From the Yìjīng 易经 commentary on the Way: 仁者見之謂之仁，知者見之謂之知 (the benevolent see it and call it benevolence; the wise see it and call it wisdom). The chengyu became the standard polite move when a Chinese speaker wants to acknowledge that a question has no single right answer without conceding any particular position. Common in essays, panel discussions, and family arguments about how to raise children.</div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="見">
      <div class="scholar-label">记忆法 jìyìfǎ · the eye on legs</div>
      <p>Hold the traditional 見 in mind: a body with the eye drawn so large it has become the head. Sight in this script is not a mental event behind the face; it is the whole body turned toward the world. Every modern compound is a different angle on that one stance. To see (看见) is for the world to land on the eye. To meet (见面) is for two of these eye-bodies to face each other. To have an opinion (意见) is to report what the eye-body has gathered over a life of looking.</p>
      <p>When you see 一见钟情, picture the eye-body the moment it stops walking. When you see 偏见, picture the eye-body tilted sideways, sight come in crooked. When you see 远见, picture it on a hilltop, looking past where the road runs out. The character is small enough to fit in a single brushstroke and big enough to hold every verb in Chinese for what the eyes do.</p>
      <p>Pair it always with 看 (kàn), the verb of looking. 看 is the gaze going out; 见 is the world coming back. Together they cover the whole shape of perception, and between them they generate a startling proportion of HSK 1.</p>
