    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="好">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p><strong>好 = 女 nǚ</strong> (woman) <strong>+ 子 zǐ</strong> (child). The oracle-bone form shows a woman holding or beside a child. The classical interpretation: a mother with her child — the most fundamental image of goodness, completeness, and right order. What is <em>good</em> is what is in its proper, natural place.</p>
      <p>This etymology has attracted feminist critiques in modern Chinese scholarship, but the compositional logic is simply Shang-dynasty cultural values: mother-and-child was the baseline image of the good. The character has outlasted the ideology that produced it, and 好 now spans everything from casual "okay" to formal "excellent."</p>
      <p>好 is also one of the most expressive characters in spoken Mandarin. Tone, length, and context transform it completely: 好 (flat) = okay / yes · 好！(emphatic) = Great! · 好好好 (rapid) = fine fine fine (slightly impatient) · 好嘛 (wheedling) = okay? please? · 好了好了 = alright alright (enough).</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── TWO READINGS ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="tones"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">多音字</span>
      <span class="sh-py">duōyīnzì</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Two Readings: hǎo vs. hào</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">读音分工 dúyīn fēngōng · Reading Split</span>
      <strong>hǎo (3rd tone)</strong> → adjective: good, fine, well, easy to; greeting; okay<br>
      <strong>hào (4th tone)</strong> → verb: to like, to be fond of, to have a taste for (more formal/literary)
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">好 hǎo</span>
          <span class="card-py">hǎo</span>
          <span class="card-en">good; fine; well; easy to; okay</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adj 形容词 xíngróngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The everyday adjective. Covers quality (好书 good book), health (你好 you-good = hello), ease (好写 easy to write), completion (好了 done/ready), and agreement (好 okay). It also functions as an adverb before adjectives: 好大 very big (spoken intensifier).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这本书写得很好。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè běn shū xiě de hěn hǎo.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This book is written very well.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">饭好了，快来吃吧。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Fàn hǎo le, kuài lái chī ba.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">The meal is ready, come eat.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">好 hào</span>
          <span class="card-py">hào</span>
          <span class="card-en">to like; to be fond of; to have a predilection for</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The verbal reading — a habitual liking or tendency. More formal and literary than 喜欢 xǐhuān. Common in compounds: 好奇 hàoqí (curious — fond of the strange), 好学 hàoxué (studious — fond of learning), 爱好 àihào (hobby — love-fondness). The 4th tone signals the verbal, active sense.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他好学不倦。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā hàoxué bùjuàn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He is studious and tireless in learning.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">她很好奇，什么都想知道。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā hěn hàoqí, shénme dōu xiǎng zhīdào.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">She is very curious — she wants to know everything.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">辨析 biànxī · 好 hào vs. 喜欢 xǐhuān</span>
          好 hào = a deep, habitual, character-defining fondness. 喜欢 = to like (broader, everyday). 爱 ài = to love. 好 hào sounds more literary and appears mainly in compounds; in speech, 喜欢 is the default.
        </div>
      </div>

    </div>

    <!-- ── CORE USES ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="core"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">核心用法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">héxīn yòngfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Core Uses — Good, Well, Easy, Done</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">好 + V (easy to V)</span>
          <span class="card-py">hǎo + V</span>
          <span class="card-en">easy to verb / pleasant to verb</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Pattern 句型 jùxíng</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          好 before a verb = easy/pleasant to do that action. 好吃 hǎochī (tasty — easy/good to eat), 好看 hǎokàn (good-looking), 好听 hǎotīng (pleasant to hear), 好用 hǎoyòng (handy — easy to use), 好写 hǎo xiě (easy to write). The negative form 不好 + V = hard/unpleasant to do: 不好说 (hard to say).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这道菜真好吃！</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè dào cài zhēn hǎochī!</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This dish is really delicious!</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这个问题不好回答。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhège wèntí bù hǎo huídá.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This question is hard to answer.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">好了</span>
          <span class="card-py">hǎo le</span>
          <span class="card-en">done; ready; okay; alright (change of state)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Expr 表达 biǎodá</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          好 + 了 (change-of-state marker) = has become ready/done/okay. One of the most frequent two-syllable expressions in spoken Mandarin. Announces completion, signals readiness, and in reduplicated form (好了好了) means "alright, enough, okay."
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">作业写好了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zuòyè xiě hǎo le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">The homework is done.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">好了好了，别再说了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Hǎo le hǎo le, bié zài shuō le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Alright alright, stop talking.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-n">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">爱好</span>
          <span class="card-py">àihào</span>
          <span class="card-en">hobby; interest; pastime</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          爱 ài (to love) + 好 hào (to be fond of). A thing one loves doing — a hobby or passion. One of the first personal vocabulary items learners need: 你有什么爱好？(What are your hobbies?)
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我的爱好是读书和爬山。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ de àihào shì dúshū hé páshān.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">My hobbies are reading and hiking.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-adj">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">好奇</span>
          <span class="card-py">hàoqí</span>
          <span class="card-en">curious; inquisitive</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-adj">Adj 形容词 xíngróngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          好 hào (fond of) + 奇 qí (the strange, the unusual). Fond of the unusual — curious. 好奇心 hàoqíxīn = curiosity (the heart of fondness for strangeness).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">孩子天生好奇，什么都想摸。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Háizi tiānshēng hàoqí, shénme dōu xiǎng mō.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Children are naturally curious — they want to touch everything.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-n">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">好处</span>
          <span class="card-py">hǎochù</span>
          <span class="card-en">benefit; advantage; good point</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          好 hǎo (good) + 处 chù (place; aspect; point). The good points of something — its advantages. Contrast: 坏处 huàichù (bad points; disadvantages).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">运动有很多好处。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Yùndòng yǒu hěn duō hǎochù.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Exercise has many benefits.</div>
          </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">好事多磨</span>
        <span class="cy-py">hǎo shì duō mó</span>
        <span class="cy-en">good things take time — good outcomes require much grinding and effort</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Lit: good-things-much-grinding. Used to console someone experiencing delays or obstacles on the path to something valuable. The consolation: friction is the price of worth.</span>

        <span class="cy-note">Lit: good-scenery-not-long. An expression of Buddhist impermanence and Chinese realism about fortune. Used when pleasant circumstances are ending or were brief.</span>
