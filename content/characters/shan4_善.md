    <!-- ── ETYMOLOGY ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology &amp; Structure</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="善">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p><strong>善 shàn</strong> is composed of <strong>羊 yáng</strong> (sheep; ram) at the top and two <strong>口 kǒu</strong> (mouth) at the bottom. The sheep is not decorative. In oracle-bone script, 羊 appears inside a cluster of characters all pointing toward what is good, beautiful, or auspicious: <strong>美 měi</strong> (beautiful) encodes a large, well-adorned sheep; <strong>祥 xiáng</strong> (auspicious) places the sheep beside the altar spirit. The sheep was the prized sacrificial animal of early Chinese ritual — the one brought to the most solemn occasions — and its presence carried the sense of something fitting, correct, and approved.</p>
      <p>The two mouths below encode the spoken dimension of goodness: declaring something good, speaking well of it, or reaching agreement. Goodness in 善 is both a quality of the thing and a social act of recognition. The <em>Shuōwén Jiězì</em> defines 善 tersely: <strong>吉也</strong> — "auspicious." That single gloss collapses ethics and luck into one concept, which is precisely how early Chinese thought treated them: to be good was to be in accord with the way things ought to go.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── MENCIAN AXIS ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="mencius"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">性善</span>
      <span class="sh-py">xìng shàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Innate Goodness — the Mencian Axis</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="善">
      <div class="scholar-label">哲学洞见 zhéxué dòngjiàn · Philosophical Insight</div>
      <p>善 is the pivot of the most consequential debate in Chinese moral philosophy: <strong>性善论 xìng shàn lùn</strong>, Mencius's doctrine of the innate goodness of human nature. Against Gaozi, who argued that human nature is morally neutral — like water that flows wherever a channel directs it — Mencius held that goodness is the original condition, the default before neglect or corruption sets in: <em>人之性善也，犹水之就下也</em> — "The goodness of human nature is like water flowing downward" (Mencius 2A:2). The tendency is there already. What requires explanation is evil, not goodness.</p>
      <p>The argument rests on the <strong>四端 sì duān</strong>, the four moral sprouts. Mencius observed that anyone who sees a child about to fall into a well feels an immediate alarm and compassion — not from calculation, not from a desire to impress the child's parents, but spontaneously. That response is evidence of something already present in human nature. He identified four such spontaneous responses, each the seed of a full virtue:</p>
      <p><strong>恻隐之心 cèyǐn zhī xīn</strong> — compassion, the distress felt at another's suffering. This is the sprout of <strong>仁 rén</strong>, benevolence: the core Confucian virtue, the love that radiates outward from family to all people.<br>
      <strong>羞恶之心 xiū'è zhī xīn</strong> — shame and dislike of wrongdoing, directed both inward at one's own failings and outward at wrong in others. This is the sprout of <strong>义 yì</strong>, righteousness: acting as one ought, regardless of reward.<br>
      <strong>辞让之心 círàng zhī xīn</strong> — deference and the instinct to yield, to give way and not push forward unnecessarily. This is the sprout of <strong>礼 lǐ</strong>, ritual propriety: the social fabric of respectful conduct.<br>
      <strong>是非之心 shìfēi zhī xīn</strong> — the ability to discriminate right from wrong, the moral sense that recognizes the difference without being taught. This is the sprout of <strong>智 zhì</strong>, moral wisdom.</p>
      <p>Cultivating these sprouts produces 善. Neglecting them allows them to wither, which is how people become cruel without any single dramatic turn toward evil — through inattention, through bad circumstances, through never developing what was always latent. Moral cultivation on this account is not the acquisition of something foreign; it is the development of what is already present.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">善于</span>
      <span class="sh-py">shànyú</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Skill &amp; Aptitude — the secular register</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">构词规律 gòucí guīlǜ · The Skill Pattern</span>
      In classical texts, 善 appears bare before an activity: <strong>善弈者</strong> shàn yì zhě = "one who is good at chess." In modern Chinese, the productive compound is <strong>善于 shànyú</strong> + verb phrase: 善于表达 = good at expressing; 善于倾听 = good at listening. The moral dimension recedes; the meaning is simply competence.
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <!-- 善于 -->
      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">善于</span>
          <span class="card-py">shànyú</span>
          <span class="card-en">to be good at; skilled in</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          善 shàn + 于 yú (at; in; toward — a classical preposition that here marks the domain of competence). The most common modern compound for natural ability or cultivated skill. Takes a verb or activity directly: <code>善于 + 动词 verb phrase</code>. Implies a degree of mastery, not just familiarity.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">她善于表达自己的想法。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā shànyú biǎodá zìjǐ de xiǎngfǎ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">She is good at expressing her ideas.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他善于倾听，从不打断别人。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā shànyú qīngtīng, cóng bù dǎduàn biéren.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He is skilled at listening and never interrupts others.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">善于利用时间是成功的关键之一。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Shànyú lìyòng shíjiān shì chénggōng de guānjiàn zhī yī.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Being good at using time is one of the keys to success.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">辨析 biànxī · 善于 vs. 擅长</span>
          <strong>善于</strong> shànyú takes a verb phrase: 善于沟通 (good at communicating). <strong>擅长</strong> shàncháng (note: 擅 is a distinct character) takes a noun or bare verb: 擅长钢琴 (excels at piano); 擅长写作 (specializes in writing). Both entered the "skilled at" semantic field but through different routes: 善 from the moral root, 擅 from an older sense of unauthorized seizure that shifted toward self-assertion in a domain.
        </div>

    <!-- ── DAOIST ANGLE ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="daoist"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">上善</span>
      <span class="sh-py">shàng shàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Daoist Angle — 上善若水</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="善">
      <div class="scholar-label">道家洞见 dàojiā dòngjiàn · Daoist Insight</div>
      <p>Laozi's <em>Daodejing</em> opens its eighth chapter with one of the most quoted lines in classical Chinese: <strong>上善若水</strong> shàng shàn ruò shuǐ — "The highest good is like water." 上 shàng here means "highest" or "supreme," not a direction. 若 ruò means "is like." The line is deceptively simple.</p>
      <p>What follows explains the analogy: <em>水善利萬物而不爭，處衆人之所惡，故幾於道</em> — "Water benefits the ten thousand things without contending for it; it occupies the places people despise. Therefore it is nearly the Dao." The highest 善 is not moral performance, not the cultivation of sprouts, not accumulation of merit. It is the effortless and unconditional support of life — the giving that asks nothing back, that goes precisely to the low and unglamorous places.</p>
      <p>This Daoist 善 sits in productive tension with the Mencian one. Mencius's goodness is active: it requires attending to the four sprouts, developing what is latent, resisting the pressures that cause the moral endowment to wither. Laozi's goodness is receptive: it requires releasing the impulse to assert, to strive, to take the high ground. Both traditions use the same character — 善 — but one points toward cultivation and the other toward release. The tension between them runs through centuries of Chinese moral philosophy and has never been fully resolved, which may be why it remains generative.</p>
    </div>
