      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Vocabulary · 词汇 cíhuì</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">丝绸之路</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">sīchóu zhī lù</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">The German geographer Ferdinand von Richthofen coined <em>Seidenstraße</em> in 1877. Chinese scholars adopted 丝绸之路 only retroactively, long after the routes had ceased to function.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="name"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">名称的来源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">míngchēng de láiyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Name &amp; coinage</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>
    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="丝">
      <div class="scholar-label">词源 cíyuán · Etymology</div>
      <p>The compound breaks into three parts. <strong>丝绸 sīchóu</strong> is silk: 丝 sī is silk thread (the character depicts twisted silk filaments), and 绸 chóu is woven silk fabric (silk radical 纟 plus phonetic 周). <strong>之 zhī</strong> is the classical genitive particle — the literary equivalent of "of." <strong>路 lù</strong> is road, route, way. Together: <em>the road of silk.</em></p>
      <p>Ferdinand von Richthofen, a Prussian geologist who spent four years surveying China in the 1860s, introduced <em>Seidenstraße</em> in his 1877 monograph <em>China</em>. The term was convenient — it gave a single memorable label to a tangle of routes that had no unified Chinese name. Chinese scholars transliterated the concept as 丝绸之路 in the twentieth century. The routes had been operating for roughly fifteen centuries before anyone thought to name them collectively.</p>
      <p>The Chinese at the time referred to routes by destination or region: <strong>西域 xīyù</strong> (the Western Regions — Central Asia beyond the Jade Gate), or <strong>通西方</strong> (the road reaching to the West). The Silk Road as a unified concept is a retrospective European invention that China has since enthusiastically adopted.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="what"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">路网，而非一条路</span>
      <span class="sh-py">lùwǎng, ér fēi yī tiáo lù</span>
      <span class="sh-en">A network, not a road</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>
    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="路">
      <div class="scholar-label">历史 lìshǐ · History</div>
      <p>The Silk Road was a shifting, branching network of overland and maritime passages connecting China to Central Asia, Persia, Arabia, and the Mediterranean. No single caravan ever traveled the full length. Merchants operated in relay: Chinese goods passed through Sogdian, Parthian, and Arab intermediaries before reaching Rome. The routes were active from roughly 130 BCE, when the Han diplomat <strong>张骞 Zhāng Qiān</strong> first reached the Ferghana Valley, until the fifteenth century, when expanding maritime trade and the disruption of Mongol-era stability made overland commerce increasingly unviable.</p>
      <p>Emperor Wu of Han (汉武帝 Hàn Wǔdì) sent Zhang Qian westward in 139 BCE to forge a military alliance with the Yuezhi people against the Xiongnu. Zhang Qian was captured almost immediately and held for ten years. He escaped, pressed on to Bactria, found the Yuezhi uninterested in anti-Xiongnu alliance, and returned to Chang'an in 125 BCE. He had failed his diplomatic mission entirely. What he brought back was intelligence — detailed accounts of Ferghana, Sogdia, Parthia, and the lands beyond. Emperor Wu began sending regular missions, and the routes solidified around trade because they already existed for diplomacy.</p>
      <p>One import obsessed Emperor Wu above all others: the <strong>汗血马</strong> hànxuě mǎ, the blood-sweating horses of Ferghana — tall, powerful animals far superior to the steppe ponies the Xiongnu rode. He sent armies to obtain them. The horses were the first Chinese import that mattered as much as silk mattered to Rome.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="exchange"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">双向流动</span>
      <span class="sh-py">shuāngxiàng liúdòng</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Exchange — what moved in both directions</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>
    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="绸">
      <div class="scholar-label">交流 jiāoliú · Exchange</div>
      <p><strong>East to West:</strong> Silk was the defining export — Rome's appetite was insatiable and Chinese emperors periodically tried to embargo the weaving techniques. Paper (replacing papyrus across the Islamic world after the 8th century), printing technology, gunpowder, cast iron, porcelain, and tea followed across centuries, each transforming the receiving civilization more thoroughly than silk had.</p>
      <p><strong>West to East:</strong> Grapes and winemaking, cotton, sesame, pomegranates, saffron, and the Ferghana horse traveled east. Glass arrived — a technology China lacked and valued highly. More consequentially: <strong>Buddhism</strong> entered China via the Kushan Empire in the first and second centuries CE, carried by monks along the same routes as silk. The monk <strong>玄奘 Xuánzàng</strong> traveled overland to India in 629 CE to retrieve Buddhist scriptures directly from the source, returning seventeen years later with 657 texts and spawning the novel <em>Journey to the West.</em></p>
      <p><strong>The plague also traveled the routes.</strong> The Justinianic Plague (541 CE) and later the Black Death (1340s) both followed trade corridors, carried in the fleas on rats in merchant caravans. The Silk Road moved civilization's greatest achievements and its most catastrophic pathogens with equal efficiency. Islam reached Tang-dynasty China by the seventh century, arriving with Arab merchants through both overland and maritime routes. The Hui Muslim community in China is the demographic descendant of those merchant settlements.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="maritime"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">海上丝绸之路</span>
      <span class="sh-py">hǎishàng sīchóu zhī lù</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Maritime Silk Road</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>
    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="海">
      <div class="scholar-label">海路 hǎilù · Sea routes</div>
      <p>The <strong>海上丝绸之路 hǎishàng sīchóu zhī lù</strong> ran from Chinese ports (Guangzhou, Quanzhou, Hangzhou) through Southeast Asia, across the Indian Ocean, through the Persian Gulf and Red Sea, to Arabia and East Africa. It became increasingly dominant after the ninth century, when Tang-dynasty instability disrupted overland passage and Song-dynasty maritime technology made ocean voyaging reliable.</p>
      <p>The apex of Chinese maritime projection came with <strong>郑和 Zhèng Hé</strong> — the Muslim eunuch admiral who led seven voyages between 1405 and 1433. His fleet at its largest comprised over 300 vessels and carried roughly 28,000 men. He reached the Persian Gulf, the Red Sea, and the East African coast. The voyages were exercises in tributary diplomacy rather than trade or conquest. After the Xuande emperor's death in 1435, the voyages ceased. The ships were dismantled. The logs were reportedly destroyed by a Confucian minister opposed to the enterprise. China turned inward, and maritime dominance passed to Portugal within a generation.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="modern"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">一带一路</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yī dài yī lù</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Belt &amp; Road — the political afterlife</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>
    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="带">
      <div class="scholar-label">当代 dāngdài · Contemporary</div>
      <p>In September 2013, Xi Jinping announced the <strong>丝绸之路经济带</strong> (Silk Road Economic Belt) in Astana, Kazakhstan — a land-infrastructure initiative through Central Asia. A month later he added the <strong>21世纪海上丝绸之路</strong> (21st Century Maritime Silk Road) in Jakarta. Together they became <strong>一带一路 yī dài yī lù</strong> — "One Belt, One Road," now translated as the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI).</p>
      <p>The historical reference is deliberately chosen. Invoking the Silk Road positions Chinese infrastructure investment as a restoration of natural historical patterns rather than a bid for geopolitical influence. The ancient routes are cast as proof that Chinese engagement with the world is benign by tradition. Critics note that the Silk Road as a unified concept was itself a German invention from 1877; the political mythology depends on a retrospective label to claim continuous historical identity. By 2023 the BRI had signed agreements with over 150 countries and committed hundreds of billions of dollars to ports, railways, power plants, and fiber optic lines across Asia, Africa, and Europe.</p>
    </div>

        <div class="tags"><span class="tag-n">noun</span></div>
        <p class="card-def">驼 tuó (camel) + 队 duì (column). Bactrian camels (双峰驼 — two-humped) were the primary transport animal on Central Asian routes, capable of carrying 200 kg for weeks without water.</p>

        <div class="tags"><span class="tag-n">proper noun</span></div>
        <p class="card-def">The Han-dynasty garrison town at the western edge of the Hexi Corridor, gateway to the Taklamakan Desert and the routes' split into northern and southern branches. The 莫高窟 (Mogao Caves) preserve Buddhist art from multiple civilizations across 492 decorated caves, 4th through 14th centuries.</p>
        <div class="examples">

        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head"><span class="card-cn">西域</span><span class="card-py">xīyù</span><span class="card-en">the Western Regions</span></div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag-n">noun</span></div>
        <p class="card-def">The Han and Tang Chinese term for Central Asia — everything west of the Jade Gate (玉门关) at Dunhuang. Included the Tarim Basin, Ferghana, Sogdia, Bactria, and Parthia. A term carrying both geographic and imaginative weight.</p>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="ex"><span class="ex-cn">张骞出使西域，开辟了丝绸之路。</span><span class="ex-py">Zhāng Qiān chūshǐ xīyù, kāipìle sīchóu zhī lù.</span><span class="ex-en">Zhang Qian's mission to the Western Regions opened the Silk Road.</span></div>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head"><span class="card-cn">玄奘</span><span class="card-py">Xuánzàng</span><span class="card-en">the monk Xuanzang</span></div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag-n">proper noun</span></div>
        <p class="card-def">Tang-dynasty Buddhist monk who traveled overland to India (629–645 CE) to retrieve scriptures. His account 大唐西域记 is a primary historical source for 7th-century Central Asia. His journey became the basis for 西游记 (Journey to the West), where he appears as the monk Tripitaka.</p>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="ex"><span class="ex-cn">玄奘的故事成为《西游记》的原型。</span><span class="ex-py">Xuánzàng de gùshi chéngwéi 《Xīyóujì》 de yuánxíng.</span><span class="ex-en">Xuanzang's story became the prototype for Journey to the West.</span></div>
