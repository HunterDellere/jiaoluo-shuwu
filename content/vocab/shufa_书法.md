      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Vocab · 词汇 cíhuì</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">书法</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">shū fǎ</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">The art where every stroke reveals the writer's mind — and where the quality of a character's governance was once judged by the quality of their calligraphy.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology — The Law of Writing</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="书">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p><strong>书 shū</strong> (writing; book) + <strong>法 fǎ</strong> (law; method; standard). 书法 = "the laws/methods of writing." Not "beautiful handwriting" (that is calligraphy's Western definition) — but writing <em>as a lawful system,</em> a disciplined practice with principles as rigorous as jurisprudence.</p>
      <p>The character 法 deserves attention: it originally depicted water (氵) flowing past a creature called 廌 zhì (a mythical animal associated with justice, used in trials to identify the guilty). Law as something that, like water, finds its own level — impartial, consistent, and revealing. The same 法 appears in: 佛法 Buddhist dharma · 语法 grammar · 方法 method · 算法 algorithm. All are systems of governing principles.</p>
      <p>This etymology explains why calligraphy in China was never merely decorative. It was evaluated by the same standards as moral character — the brush doesn't lie. A person of inner chaos cannot write with inner calm. This is why imperial examinations included calligraphy as a core competency: the quality of a candidate's brushwork revealed the quality of their mind.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="philosophy"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">哲学</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zhéxué</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Philosophy — Character Through the Brush</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="书">
      <div class="scholar-label">书如其人 shū rú qí rén · Writing Reveals the Person</div>
      <p>The classical principle <strong>书如其人</strong> — "writing is like the person" — holds that calligraphy reveals character as directly as conversation or action. Su Dongpo 苏东坡 wrote: "The character of calligraphy comes from the character of the writer's spirit." You cannot fake it — tension, impatience, elegance, and steadiness all leave their marks in the ink.</p>
      <p>The Daoist dimension: the ideal calligraphic state is <strong>无为 wúwéi</strong> — effortless action. The master calligrapher does not plan each stroke consciously; through years of practice, the body has internalized the principles, and writing flows from a place of stillness. Wang Xizhi 王羲之 described the ideal moment of composition: after wine, in good company, at a perfect temperature — the mind empties and the hand moves freely. The greatest works cannot be deliberately reproduced.</p>
      <p>Calligraphy has traditionally been ranked as the highest of the arts, above painting, music, and poetry, because it combines all four: the rhythm of music (in the brush's movement), the space of painting (in the composition), the meaning of poetry (in the text), and the structure of architecture (in the bones of each character).</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="scripts"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">书体</span>
      <span class="sh-py">shūtǐ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Five Scripts</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">五种书体 wǔ zhǒng shūtǐ · Historical Order (Slowest → Fastest)</span>
      <strong>篆书</strong> zhuànshū Seal Script — the oldest; archaic, formal, used on seals and bronze<br>
      <strong>隶书</strong> lìshū Clerical Script — Han dynasty administrative script; flat, horizontal, elegant<br>
      <strong>楷书</strong> kǎishū Regular Script — the standard form; what students learn first; structured<br>
      <strong>行书</strong> xíngshū Running Script — the most used; partially connected; readable speed<br>
      <strong>草书</strong> cǎoshū Cursive Script — wild, expressive, highly abbreviated; requires training to read
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-n">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">楷书</span>
          <span class="card-py">kǎishū</span>
          <span class="card-en">regular script — the foundation script</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          楷 kǎi (model; standard) + 书 shū (writing). The standard script — the foundation that every calligrapher must master before attempting other styles. Print fonts are based on regular script. 不会楷书，不学草书 "Don't attempt cursive without mastering regular script" — the traditional maxim.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">学习书法要从楷书开始，打好基础。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Xuéxí shūfǎ yào cóng kǎishū kāishǐ, dǎ hǎo jīchǔ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Learning calligraphy should begin with regular script — building a solid foundation.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-n">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">草书</span>
          <span class="card-py">cǎoshū</span>
          <span class="card-en">cursive script — the most expressive and wild form</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          草 cǎo (grass; rough; draft) + 书 shū. "Grass writing" — like wild grass, cursive is fast, flowing, and irregular. Individual strokes merge; some characters are radically abbreviated. Legible mainly to trained readers. The style most associated with artistic expression and emotional intensity. Zhang Xu 张旭 (Tang dynasty) was famous for writing cursive while drunk.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这幅草书作品气势磅礴，令人叹为观止。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè fú cǎoshū zuòpǐn qìshì pángbó, lìng rén tàn wéi guānzhǐ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This cursive calligraphy work has tremendous momentum — truly breathtaking.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="masters"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">书法家</span>
      <span class="sh-py">shūfǎjiā</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Great Masters</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="书">
      <div class="scholar-label">书圣 shūshèng · The Saint of Calligraphy</div>
      <p><strong>王羲之 Wáng Xīzhī</strong> (303–361 CE, Eastern Jin) — universally honored as 书圣 shūshèng, the "Sage of Calligraphy." His masterwork <em>兰亭序 Lántíng Xù</em> (Preface to the Orchid Pavilion Gathering) is considered the greatest piece of running script ever written. Written in a moment of inspiration at a spring gathering, he tried to reproduce it later and found it inferior. The original was reportedly buried with Emperor Taizong of Tang.</p>
      <p><strong>颜真卿 Yán Zhēnqīng</strong> (709–785, Tang) — the master of regular script. His 颜体 Yán style (broad, powerful, and upright) became the model for government documents and public monuments. His calligraphy was said to embody his moral character: the same uprightness that led him to resist the An Lushan Rebellion at age 77.</p>
      <p><strong>苏东坡 Sū Dōngpō</strong> (1037–1101, Song) — poet, statesman, calligrapher, and one of the most beloved figures in Chinese cultural history. His calligraphy is individualistic and playful; he broke the classical rules deliberately and joyfully, reflecting the Song aesthetic of personal expression over formal correctness.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">文房四宝</span>
      <span class="sh-py">wénfáng sì bǎo</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Four Treasures of the Scholar's Studio</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">文房四宝 wénfáng sì bǎo · The Four Treasures</span>
      <strong>笔</strong> bǐ — the brush (毛笔 máobǐ; animal hair — wolf, rabbit, goat — on a bamboo handle)<br>
      <strong>墨</strong> mò — ink stick (ground against the stone with water to produce ink; quality varies enormously)<br>
      <strong>纸</strong> zhǐ — paper (宣纸 xuānzhǐ Xuan paper from Anhui, the standard for painting and calligraphy)<br>
      <strong>砚</strong> yàn — ink stone (端砚 duānyàn from Duanxi, Guangdong; 歙砚 shèyàn from Anhui are the finest)
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="vocab"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">词汇</span>
      <span class="sh-py">cíhuì</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Essential Calligraphy Vocabulary</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-n">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">笔画</span>
          <span class="card-py">bǐhuà</span>
          <span class="card-en">stroke; brushstroke</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          笔 bǐ (brush; pen) + 画 huà (to draw; stroke). The individual strokes that make up a character. Chinese characters are analyzed by stroke count (几画 jǐ huà "how many strokes?") and stroke order (笔顺 bǐshùn). There are eight basic stroke types in regular script — 永字八法 yǒng zì bā fǎ (the eight methods of the character 永 "eternal" demonstrate all eight).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">书法老师纠正了他的笔画顺序。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Shūfǎ lǎoshī jiūzhèng le tā de bǐhuà shùnxù.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">The calligraphy teacher corrected his stroke order.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">临摹</span>
          <span class="card-py">línmó</span>
          <span class="card-en">to copy a master's work in order to learn</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V 动词 dòngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          临 lín (to face; to approach — as in approaching the model directly) + 摹 mó (to trace; to copy). The foundational method of calligraphic learning: copying master works character by character to internalize their structure and spirit. Not mere imitation — the goal is to absorb the principles so deeply they become one's own.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他临摹王羲之的兰亭序已经临了三年。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā línmó Wáng Xīzhī de Lántíng Xù yǐjīng lín le sān nián.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He has been copying Wang Xizhi's Orchid Pavilion Preface for three years.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
