    <header class="topic-hero">
      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Language · 语言 yǔyán</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">中文</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">zhōngwén</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">The most common term for "Chinese" among foreign learners, 中文 covers both the spoken language and the written script — but Chinese has at least five more names for itself, each carrying a different emphasis on ethnicity, standardization, region, or civilization.</p>
    </header>

    <!-- ── CORE DISTINCTION ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="core"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">核心区别</span>
      <span class="sh-py">héxīn qūbié</span>
      <span class="sh-en">中文 vs 汉语 — The Foundational Distinction</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="文">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Note</div>
      <p><strong>中文 zhōngwén</strong> — 中 (zhōng: center, middle, China) + 文 (wén: writing, civilization, pattern, culture). Literally "writing of the Middle Kingdom" or "civilization of the center." The character 文 is one of the most semantically loaded on the site: it encompasses writing, culture, learning, and civilizational refinement simultaneously. Because 文 foregrounds the written dimension, 中文 has a natural emphasis on the language as a literate tradition — which is why it appears on menus, signage, apps, and forms aimed at international audiences. In practice, however, 中文 is used freely for both spoken and written Chinese by learners and speakers abroad.</p>
      <p><strong>汉语 Hànyǔ</strong> — 汉 (Hàn: the Han people, the Han dynasty) + 语 (yǔ: spoken language, speech). Literally "the spoken language of the Han people." This is the formal linguistic term used in academia, official documents, government policy, and within China itself. The reference to the Han ethnic majority becomes significant in a country with 55 officially recognized ethnic minorities, each with their own language or dialect. When the government names the official standardized language, it uses 汉语 to make clear whose language is being standardized.</p>
      <p>The practical difference comes down to context. If you are filling out a Chinese government form, it will say 汉语水平考试 (HSK — Hànyǔ Shuǐpíng Kǎoshì). If you are telling a friend in another country what language you are studying, you say 我在学中文. Both are correct; neither is more accurate than the other. Context determines the choice.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── PUTONGHUA VS FANGYAN ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="putonghua"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">普通话与方言</span>
      <span class="sh-py">pǔtōnghuà yǔ fāngyán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Standard Mandarin and the Regional Languages</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="普">
      <div class="scholar-label">标准与地方 biāozhǔn yǔ dìfāng · Standard and Regional</div>
      <p><strong>普通话 Pǔtōnghuà</strong> — "common speech" — is the standardized form of Mandarin declared official in 1956, based primarily on the Beijing dialect with some phonological modifications. This is what is taught in every Chinese school, broadcast on state television, and used in all official contexts. A child in Guangdong and a child in Heilongjiang both learn the same 普通话 even if they grow up speaking entirely different languages at home.</p>
      <p>The same standard spoken variety carries different names across Chinese-speaking regions. Taiwan calls it <strong>国语 Guóyǔ</strong> (National Language) — the same phonological standard, but with a different political framing that emphasizes nationhood rather than commonality. Singapore uses <strong>华语 Huáyǔ</strong> (Language of Chinese people) — a term that foregrounds cultural and civilizational identity rather than ethnic or national categories.</p>
      <p><strong>方言 fāngyán</strong> (regional speech) is the standard term for what are often called Chinese dialects: Cantonese (粤语 Yuèyǔ), Shanghainese (吴语 Wúyǔ), Hokkien and Taiwanese (闽南语 Mǐnnányǔ), Hakka (客家话 Kèjiāhuà), and many others. A speaker of standard Mandarin who has never studied Cantonese cannot understand a Cantonese conversation. The linguistic distance is comparable to the gap between Portuguese and Spanish — or greater. The classification as "dialects" rather than separate languages reflects political and historical choices, not linguistic measurement.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── HANZI THE WRITING SYSTEM ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="hanzi"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">汉字</span>
      <span class="sh-py">hànzì</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Writing System — Simplified and Traditional</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="字">
      <div class="scholar-label">文字系统 wénzì xìtǒng · The Script System</div>
      <p><strong>汉字 Hànzì</strong> — Han characters — is the specific term for the Chinese writing system: the characters themselves, as a script. 汉字 is the correct technical term for the script regardless of which language it is representing. Japanese kanji are 汉字. Vietnamese classical texts were written in 汉字. Korean hanja are 汉字. The script transcends any single language; the name acknowledges its Han Chinese origin while recognizing its historical spread.</p>
      <p>The term 中文字 (zhōngwénzì) is sometimes heard informally for "Chinese written characters," but it is redundant and non-standard. 汉字 is the term scholars, educators, and linguists use.</p>
      <p>Within 汉字, learners face an immediate choice: <strong>简体字 jiǎntǐzì</strong> (simplified characters) or <strong>繁体字 fántǐzì</strong> (traditional characters). Simplified forms were standardized on the mainland in the 1950s and 1960s by reducing stroke counts and merging some characters. Traditional forms remain in use in Taiwan, Hong Kong, Macau, and many overseas Chinese communities. The spoken language is the same; the written forms differ enough that a reader fluent in one will sometimes struggle with the other, particularly for characters that were substantially redesigned. Learners must choose which to study based on where they plan to use the language.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── HUAWEN OVERSEAS ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="huawen"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">华文</span>
      <span class="sh-py">huáwén</span>
      <span class="sh-en">华文 and Overseas Chinese Usage</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="华">
      <div class="scholar-label">海外华人 hǎiwài huárén · Overseas Chinese Communities</div>
      <p><strong>华文 Huáwén</strong> is the term preferred in Southeast Asia for the Chinese language as used by overseas Chinese communities (华人 Huárén — people of Chinese cultural descent). Where 汉语 emphasizes ethnicity (Han people), 华文 and 华语 emphasize civilizational and cultural belonging. The distinction matters most in countries like Malaysia, Indonesia, and Singapore, where Chinese communities have lived for generations and have complex, sometimes fraught, relationships with both their ancestral homeland and the nations where they were born. Calling the language 华语 rather than 汉语 signals cultural heritage without asserting ethnic or political allegiance to mainland China.</p>
      <p>华 (huá) itself is a rich character: it means magnificent, splendid, Chinese, or flourishing — it carries connotations of civilizational grandeur rather than ethnic specificity. 中华 (Zhōnghuá) is a literary and formal name for China that blends center (中) and magnificence (华), and appears in the official names of both the People's Republic of China (中华人民共和国) and the Republic of China on Taiwan (中华民国).</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">中文</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">zhōngwén</div>
        <div class="card-en">center + writing/civilization</div>
        <div class="card-note">The default term when speaking to a non-Chinese audience or in international contexts. Use when: telling someone you study Chinese, selecting a language in an app, naming the subject on a language course syllabus. Covers spoken and written. Neutral on ethnicity.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">汉语</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">Hànyǔ</div>
        <div class="card-en">Han people + spoken language</div>
        <div class="card-note">The formal linguistic and academic term used within China. Use when: referring to the language in official documents, naming the HSK exam, speaking in an academic or policy context inside China. Marks the language as belonging to the Han ethnic majority.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">普通话</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">Pǔtōnghuà</div>
        <div class="card-en">common + speech</div>
        <div class="card-note">Standard Mandarin specifically — the phonological standard taught in schools and used in broadcast media. Use when: distinguishing the standard spoken form from regional varieties, discussing Chinese-language education policy, or asking if someone speaks the standard variety rather than a local language.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">汉字</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">Hànzì</div>
        <div class="card-en">Han people + characters</div>
        <div class="card-note">The writing system itself — the characters as a script. Use when: discussing the written form specifically, learning stroke order, talking about simplified vs traditional forms, or referring to the script system shared with Japanese kanji and Korean hanja. Distinct from any spoken language.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">华文 / 华语</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">Huáwén / Huáyǔ</div>
        <div class="card-en">civilizational magnificence + writing/language</div>
        <div class="card-note">Used in Southeast Asia (Malaysia, Singapore, Indonesia) by and for overseas Chinese communities. Preferred when cultural identity matters more than ethnic or national labeling. 华语 is the term Singapore uses for its official Mandarin standard; 华文 refers to Chinese-medium education in the region.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-hanzi">国语</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">Guóyǔ</div>
        <div class="card-en">national + language</div>
        <div class="card-note">Taiwan's official name for the same Mandarin standard that the mainland calls 普通话. The phonological standard is substantially the same; the political framing is different. Older speakers from mainland China who arrived in Taiwan in 1949 also used this term. Using 国语 signals Taiwan rather than mainland context.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-hanzi">方言</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">fāngyán</div>
        <div class="card-en">regional + speech</div>
        <div class="card-note">The umbrella term for regional varieties: Cantonese, Shanghainese, Hokkien, Hakka, and dozens of others. Officially classified as dialects of Chinese; linguistically, many are mutually unintelligible and better described as related languages within the Sinitic family. Use 方言 when distinguishing a regional language from 普通话 or discussing regional linguistic diversity.</div>
      </div>
