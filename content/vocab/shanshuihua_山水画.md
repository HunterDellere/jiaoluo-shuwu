      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Vocab · 词汇 cíhuì</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">山水画</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">shān shuǐ huà</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">Mountains and waters as cosmological pair -- the genre of Chinese painting that made ink a philosophical practice and landscape a meditation on the nature of reality.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="name"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">名字与宇宙</span>
      <span class="sh-py">míngzi yǔ yǔzhòu</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Name -- Mountains and Waters as Cosmological Pair</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="山">
      <div class="scholar-label">字义 zìyì · Meaning of the Name</div>
      <p><strong>山水画</strong> means, literally, mountain-water-painting. The genre's name is not a borrowing of the Western concept of "landscape" (a Dutch painters' term for a view of scenery as such) but a prior and distinct claim about what the outdoor world contains at its most essential. Where "landscape" is broadly neutral -- any scenery seen from a distance -- 山水 names a specific dyad: mountains and waters, the vertical and the horizontal, the stable and the flowing.</p>
      <p>The pairing is cosmological before it is painterly. In classical Chinese thought, mountains are <strong>阴 yīn</strong> -- stable, receiving, substantial, accumulating -- while waters are <strong>阳 yáng</strong> -- moving, reflecting, dispersing, generative. The same polarity underlies 太极 tàijí theory and the 五行 wǔxíng (Five Phases) framework of classical natural philosophy. To paint 山水 is therefore not simply to render scenery but to represent the fundamental binary of the cosmos in visible form. The genre's name is an argument about what landscapes contain.</p>
      <p>This also explains why Chinese landscape painting so consistently focuses on mountains and water to the near-exclusion of other landscape features. Sky, distant plains, cultivated fields -- these appear incidentally. What must be present are mountains (in various degrees of mist and solidity) and some form of water (rivers, waterfalls, lakes, rain). Everything else is context for this essential tension.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="liufa"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">六法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">liù fǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Six Principles -- Xie He and the Founding Theory</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="法">
      <div class="scholar-label">绘画理论 huìhuà lǐlùn · Painting Theory</div>
      <p>The founding text of Chinese painting theory is the preface to the <em>Gu Hua Pin Lu</em> 古画品录 (Record of the Classification of Old Paintings), written by the critic Xie He 谢赫 (fl. 5th-6th century CE). Xie He proposed <strong>六法 liùfǎ</strong> -- Six Principles -- as the criteria by which painting should be judged. They have been cited and debated for fifteen centuries:</p>
      <p><strong>1. 气韵生动 qìyùn shēngdòng</strong> -- Spirit resonance, life movement. The supreme principle. A painting that has 气韵 is alive; one that lacks it is mere craft. This principle concerns not technique but the painter's cultivation and inner vision. It cannot be directly taught; it must be cultivated.</p>
      <p><strong>2. 骨法用笔 gǔfǎ yòngbǐ</strong> -- Bone structure in brushwork. The structural quality of the brush stroke -- its tension, direction, and internal energy. Borrowed from calligraphy theory, where each stroke has a skeletal architecture.</p>
      <p><strong>3. 应物象形 yìngwù xiàngxíng</strong> -- Conforming to the object in forming shapes. Rendering the visible form of things -- not photographic realism, but the essential shape that identifies the subject.</p>
      <p><strong>4. 随类赋彩 suílèi fùcǎi</strong> -- Applying color according to classification. Color used according to the nature of the subject, not as observed at a particular moment or under particular light conditions.</p>
      <p><strong>5. 经营位置 jīngyíng wèizhì</strong> -- Planning and placing. Composition: how the elements of the painting are arranged relative to each other and within the picture plane. 经营 suggests deliberate management and care.</p>
      <p><strong>6. 传移模写 chuányí móxiě</strong> -- Transmission by copying. Learning the tradition by copying the masters. Not mere imitation but the internalization of technique and vision through attentive reproduction of great works.</p>
      <p>The ordering is deliberate. 气韵生动 comes first because it is the most essential and the least teachable. The principles that follow concern increasingly concrete and learnable matters. A painter who masters principles 2 through 6 but lacks 气韵 has acquired craft without art. A painter who has 气韵 but struggles with the technical principles can still produce living work.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="periods"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">朝代与风格</span>
      <span class="sh-py">cháodài yǔ fēnggé</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Major Periods -- Aesthetic Arcs from Tang to Qing</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="画">
      <div class="scholar-label">历史分期 lìshǐ fēnqī · Historical Periodization</div>
      <p><strong>唐 Tang (618-907):</strong> Landscape painting establishes its independence from figure painting as the primary genre. Li Sixun 李思训 and his son develop the blue-and-green tradition 青绿山水 qīnglǜ shānshuǐ: bright mineral pigments (azurite, malachite), precise outlines, decorative richness. Wang Wei 王维 (701-761) -- poet, official, and painter -- is traditionally credited with the development of ink monochrome wash 水墨 shuǐmò, painting with tonal gradations of ink rather than color. The critic Su Shi later wrote of Wang Wei: "in his poems there is painting; in his paintings there is poetry" (诗中有画，画中有诗 shī zhōng yǒu huà, huà zhōng yǒu shī) -- the canonical statement of literati aesthetic integration.</p>
      <p><strong>五代与北宋 Five Dynasties and Northern Song (907-1127):</strong> The classical period of monumental landscape. Jing Hao 荆浩, Guan Tong 关仝, Fan Kuan 范宽, and Guo Xi 郭熙 develop the towering vertical scroll: mountains rising from mist, tiny travelers dwarfed by rock and pine, waterfalls threading through fog. Guo Xi's theoretical text 林泉高致 (Lín Quán Gāo Zhì, Lofty Record of Forests and Streams) introduces the "three distances" 三远 sān yuǎn: 高远 gāo yuǎn (high distance, looking up from the base to the summit), 深远 shēn yuǎn (deep distance, looking through layers of recession), and 平远 píng yuǎn (level distance, looking across an open horizontal plane). These three modes of spatial recession remain the foundational taxonomy of Chinese landscape composition.</p>
      <p><strong>南宋 Southern Song (1127-1279):</strong> After the fall of the north to the Jurchen Jin dynasty, the court retreated to Hangzhou. The aesthetic pivots. Ma Yuan 马远 and Xia Gui 夏圭 develop the asymmetric composition: a mountain, a pine, a scholar -- placed in one corner of the silk, with the rest left as luminous empty space. The "one-corner Ma" style makes negative space the primary expressive element. The lost half of the empire is present in the emptiness of the painting.</p>
      <p><strong>元 Yuan (1271-1368):</strong> Under Mongol rule, many Han scholar-officials withdrew from public life. The literati painters led by Zhao Mengfu 赵孟頫 and the Four Yuan Masters (黄公望 Huang Gongwang, 吴镇 Wu Zhen, 倪瓒 Ni Zan, 王蒙 Wang Meng) develop the full literati aesthetic: painting as personal cultivation and expression, not as commission or decoration. Ni Zan's nearly empty compositions -- a few sparse rocks, bare bamboo, an uninhabited pavilion -- and his stated goal of capturing "the untrammeled spirit in my breast" (胸中逸气 xiōng zhōng yìqì) rather than any resemblance to external things, becomes the defining statement of literati aesthetic autonomy. For Ni Zan, a painting that merely looks like something is a failure.</p>
      <p><strong>明清 Ming and Qing (1368-1912):</strong> The individualists of the late Ming and early Qing push the tradition to its philosophical limits. Bada Shanren 八大山人 (Zhu Da, c. 1626-1705) painted fish with eyes rolled upward in what reads as defiant disdain, birds hunched on minimal branches, landscapes of radical emptiness. Shitao 石涛 (1642-1707), whose theoretical text 画语录 Huà Yǔ Lù argues that the 一划 yī huà ("single stroke") contains all the principles of painting and of existence itself, pushed painting theory into the territory of cosmology: "The single stroke is the origin of all existence, the root of all phenomena."</p>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">美学词汇 měixué cíhuì · The Conceptual Vocabulary of the Genre</span>
      <strong>气韵 qìyùn</strong> — spirit resonance · the vital, living quality that distinguishes great painting from mere skill<br>
      <strong>三远 sān yuǎn</strong> — three distances · high (looking up), deep (looking in), level (looking across) · Guo Xi's taxonomy of spatial recession<br>
      <strong>留白 liúbái</strong> — leaving white · deliberate unpainted space as active compositional element · the emptiness that breathes<br>
      <strong>水墨 shuǐmò</strong> — ink wash · the black-ink tradition; tonal modulation through ink concentration and water<br>
      <strong>文人画 wénrén huà</strong> — literati painting · painting by and for cultivated scholars, as self-expression rather than commission<br>
      <strong>意境 yìjìng</strong> — artistic conception · the imaginative world or mood a work creates beyond its literal content

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">水墨</span>
          <span class="card-py">shuǐmò</span>
          <span class="card-en">ink wash; the ink tradition</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          水 shuǐ (water) + 墨 mò (ink). The medium and the tradition: painting with black ink diluted in varying concentrations to produce the full tonal range. Contrasted with the 青绿 qīnglǜ (blue-and-green) color tradition. By the Yuan dynasty, 水墨 monochrome had become the prestige aesthetic for literati painters -- color was associated with professional decorative work.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这幅水墨山水画气韵生动。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè fú shuǐmò shānshuǐ huà qìyùn shēngdòng.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This ink-wash landscape has spirit resonance and life movement.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">留白</span>
          <span class="card-py">liúbái</span>
          <span class="card-en">leaving white; negative space</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-vn">VN 动名词 dòngmíngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          留 liú (to leave; to keep) + 白 bái (white; blank). The deliberate preservation of unpainted paper as an active compositional element. In landscape painting, blank areas read as mist, water, distance, or sky -- the forms that naturally dissolve into whiteness. The Daoist grounding: utility lies in emptiness (道德经 chapter 11). A great 留白 is not absence but presence of a different kind.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">南宋画家善用留白，以空白表现辽阔的水面。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Nán Sòng huàjiā shàn yòng liúbái, yǐ kòngbái biǎoxiàn liáokuò de shuǐmiàn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Southern Song painters mastered negative space, using the blank to represent expanses of water.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">气韵</span>
          <span class="card-py">qìyùn</span>
          <span class="card-en">spirit resonance; vital rhythm</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          气 qì (breath; vital energy) + 韵 yùn (resonance; rhyme; musical tone). The first and supreme of Xie He's Six Principles: 气韵生动 qìyùn shēngdòng, "spirit resonance, life movement." A painting that has 气韵 is alive; one that lacks it is mere skilled craft. Because 气韵 cannot be directly taught or technically produced, only cultivated through broad learning and deep personal development, it became the standard that separated artists from artisans.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">气韵生动是中国绘画六法之首。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Qìyùn shēngdòng shì Zhōngguó huìhuà liùfǎ zhī shǒu.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Spirit resonance and life movement is the foremost of the Six Principles of Chinese painting.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">三远</span>
          <span class="card-py">sān yuǎn</span>
          <span class="card-en">three distances; three modes of spatial recession</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          三 sān (three) + 远 yuǎn (far; distance). Guo Xi's taxonomy from 林泉高致 (1080s): 高远 gāo yuǎn (high distance -- looking up from base to peak, creates grandeur and vertical drama), 深远 shēn yuǎn (deep distance -- looking through receding layers of mountains and valleys, creates mystery and depth), 平远 píng yuǎn (level distance -- looking across an open horizontal plane, creates tranquility and expanse). A painter can deploy all three in one composition.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">郭熙的三远之法，是山水画构图的基础理论。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Guō Xī de sān yuǎn zhī fǎ, shì shānshuǐ huà gòutú de jīchǔ lǐlùn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Guo Xi's method of the three distances is the foundational theory of landscape composition.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">文人画</span>
          <span class="card-py">wénrén huà</span>
          <span class="card-en">literati painting; scholar painting</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          文人 wénrén (literatus; cultivated scholar-official) + 画 huà (painting). The tradition of painting as personal cultivation and self-expression rather than as commissioned craft. Theorized by Su Shi 苏轼 and Mi Fu 米芾 in the Northern Song, dominant from the Yuan onward. Its core claim: a painting by a 文人 is inherently superior because it bears the trace of a cultivated mind, regardless of technical virtuosity. Ink monochrome over color; character over skill.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">倪瓒是元代文人画最具代表性的画家之一。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Ní Zàn shì Yuán dài wénrén huà zuì jù dàibiǎoxìng de huàjiā zhī yī.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Ni Zan is one of the most representative painters of the Yuan dynasty literati tradition.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">意境</span>
          <span class="card-py">yìjìng</span>
          <span class="card-en">artistic conception; imaginative world</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          意 yì (intention; meaning; mind) + 境 jìng (realm; state; circumstance). The imaginative world or mood that a work of art creates -- the space of feeling and association that extends beyond the painted forms themselves. A painting with strong 意境 transports the viewer into a particular state of mind and a particular imagined world. Closely related to the concept of 韵味 yùnwèi (resonant flavor) and fundamental to 文人画 aesthetics.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这幅山水画意境深远，令人沉思。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè fú shānshuǐ huà yìjìng shēnyuǎn, lìng rén chénsī.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This landscape painting has a profound artistic conception -- it invites contemplation.</div>
          </div>
