      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Vocab · Grammar · 词汇语法</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">必须</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">bì xū</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">The strongest everyday modal of obligation. When a speaker chooses 必须 over 应该 or 要, they are flagging that the matter is not up for discussion: there is no acceptable alternative, the requirement is structural rather than advisory.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">字源</span><span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span><span class="sh-en">Etymology and Components</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="必">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p><strong>必 bì</strong> (certainly; must; inevitably): the early form depicts a long handle with a binding mark, often analyzed as a shaft fastened with a slanting peg. The semantic extension reads as fastening: what is bound cannot be otherwise. By the classical period 必 had become the standard adverb of necessity, occurring in proverb-form constructions (言必信，行必果, words must be trustworthy and actions must bring results) where it locks each clause to its outcome.</p>
      <p><strong>须 xū</strong> (to need; to require; must; whiskers): the early graph shows a face with strands of beard, the original sense being facial hair. The phonetic borrowing into the modal sense (to require, must) is well-attested by the Han period and entirely displaces the original meaning in modern usage; the beard sense survives only in fossilized compounds (胡须 húxū, beard).</p>
      <p>必 + 须 = a doubled emphasis, certainty fused with requirement. The compound is stronger than either component alone: 必须 says both that the action is necessary and that it is inevitable, leaving no rhetorical room for the listener to negotiate.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="pattern"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">用法</span><span class="sh-py">yòngfǎ</span><span class="sh-en">The Modal Pattern</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <p><strong>Subject + 必须 + Verb Phrase</strong></p>
      <p>必须 is a modal adverb-verb that sits before the main verb. Like 应该 and 要, it does not take 了 or 着, and it is negated only with 不 + 必须 in narrow contexts; the standard negation of obligation is the separate word 不必 (bùbì, not necessary) or 不需要 (bù xūyào, no need to).</p>
      <p class="example"><em>学生必须按时交作业。</em><br>Xuésheng bìxū ànshí jiāo zuòyè.<br>Students must turn in their homework on time.</p>
      <p class="example"><em>你必须现在就走，不然就来不及了。</em><br>Nǐ bìxū xiànzài jiù zǒu, bùrán jiù láibují le.<br>You have to leave right now, otherwise it will be too late.</p>
      <p>To negate the requirement, switch to 不必 or 不用:</p>
      <p class="example"><em>你不必担心，我自己能解决。</em><br>Nǐ bùbì dānxīn, wǒ zìjǐ néng jiějué.<br>You don't have to worry, I can handle it myself.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="distinctions"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">辨析</span><span class="sh-py">biànxī</span><span class="sh-en">The Modal Strength Ladder</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="须">
      <div class="scholar-label">语义层级 yǔyì céngjí · Semantic Hierarchy</div>
      <p>Mandarin has at least five everyday ways to mark obligation, and choosing between them is a register decision. From strongest to weakest:</p>
      <p><strong>必须 bìxū</strong>: must, structurally required, no alternative. Formal and emphatic. Used in rules, regulations, instructions, and serious advice. Often appears in written notices and policy.</p>
      <p><strong>得 děi</strong> (note the reading: děi, not de): have to, in a colloquial obligation sense. Same strength as 必须 in feeling but the spoken-language version. 我得走了 (I have to go) is what you actually say; 我必须走 sounds like a rule rather than a person leaving.</p>
      <p><strong>要 yào</strong>: should, need to, going to. Weaker, more advisory. Carries an aspect of intention as well as obligation, which softens it. 你要小心 (you need to be careful) is a warning, not a rule.</p>
      <p><strong>应该 yīnggāi</strong>: should, ought to. Moral or expectation-based, not structural. Allows the listener room to disagree. The distance between 你必须道歉 (you must apologize) and 你应该道歉 (you ought to apologize) is the distance between a demand and a suggestion.</p>
      <p><strong>需要 xūyào</strong>: to need. A neutral statement of need rather than a directive. 需要预约 (need to book in advance) is informational; it does not push the listener.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="trap"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">易混</span><span class="sh-py">yìhùn</span><span class="sh-en">必须 vs 必需 — the spelling trap</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="需">
      <div class="scholar-label">用字辨析 yòngzì biànxī · Character Choice</div>
      <p>Two near-homophones that even native speakers slip on. The distinction is grammatical rather than semantic.</p>
      <p><strong>必须 bìxū</strong>: 必 + 须. A modal adverb modifying a verb. It tells you that an action <em>must</em> happen. Always followed by a verb phrase.</p>
      <p class="example"><em>你必须出席。</em> , You must attend. (modifying the verb 出席)</p>
      <p><strong>必需 bìxū</strong>: 必 + 需. An adjective or noun-phrase modifier meaning <em>essential, necessary</em>. It describes a thing rather than commanding an action. Usually followed by 的 + noun, or stands as a noun.</p>
      <p class="example"><em>水是生活的必需品。</em> , Water is a necessity of life. (modifying 品, an item)</p>
      <p>Quick test: if you can replace it with "must (do)," use 必须. If you can replace it with "necessary (thing)," use 必需. Get this wrong in formal writing and the reader notices immediately.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="examples"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">例句</span><span class="sh-py">lìjù</span><span class="sh-en">Real Sentences</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">必须遵守</span>
          <span class="card-py">bìxū zūnshǒu</span>
          <span class="card-en">must comply with; must abide by (rules, laws)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-vn">Set collocation · 固定搭配</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The standard pairing in legal, regulatory, and policy language. 必须遵守法律 (must comply with the law). The phrase carries the formal-rules register that 必须 lives in: every Chinese workplace handbook, public notice, and company memo will use this collocation rather than its softer alternatives.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">所有员工必须遵守公司的规章制度。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Suǒyǒu yuángōng bìxū zūnshǒu gōngsī de guīzhāng zhìdù.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">All employees must abide by the company's rules and regulations.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">必须承认</span>
          <span class="card-py">bìxū chéngrèn</span>
          <span class="card-en">one must admit; it must be acknowledged that</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-vn">Discourse phrase · 话语短语</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          A common rhetorical move in essays and serious conversation, conceding ground before pivoting. The 必须 here is not commanding the listener; it is the speaker acknowledging an inescapable point. English equivalent: "it has to be said that," "one must concede that."
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">必须承认，他在这个领域确实有独到见解。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Bìxū chéngrèn, tā zài zhège lǐngyù quèshí yǒu dúdào jiànjiě.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">It must be admitted that he does have original insight in this field.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>
