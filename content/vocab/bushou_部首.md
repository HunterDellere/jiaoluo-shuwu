      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Language · 语言 yǔyán</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">部首</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">bù shǒu</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">Radicals are the indexing components of Chinese characters — the structural units that organize dictionaries, signal meaning, and reveal the logic of how characters are built.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="what"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">什么是部首</span><span class="sh-py">shénme shì bùshǒu</span><span class="sh-en">What Radicals Are — Not Letters, But Keys</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="部">
      <div class="scholar-label">定义 dìngyì · Definition</div>
      <p>Chinese has no alphabet. Characters are not assembled from phonetic letters — they are built from components that carry meaning, sound hints, or structural roles. A <strong>radical</strong> (部首 bùshǒu) is the primary indexing component of a character: the part under which it is filed in a traditional dictionary.</p>
      <p>部首 literally means "section head" — 部 (section, group) + 首 (head, first). When you look up a character in a radical-based dictionary, you first identify its radical, find that section, then locate the character by its remaining stroke count. Before digital input methods, this was the only way to look up an unfamiliar character.</p>
      <p>Radicals are not phonetic. They do not tell you how to pronounce a character. They tell you — roughly — what category of thing or action a character belongs to. 氵(water) signals moisture or flow. 木 (wood) signals trees and wooden things. 口 (mouth) signals speaking, eating, openings. This is an imperfect but real semantic signal.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="kangxi"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">康熙字典</span><span class="sh-py">Kāngxī Zìdiǎn</span><span class="sh-en">The 214 Kangxi Radicals — The Standard System</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="典">
      <div class="scholar-label">康熙字典 1716 · The Kangxi Dictionary</div>
      <p>The current standard of 214 radicals comes from the <strong>Kangxi Dictionary</strong> (康熙字典 Kāngxī Zìdiǎn), compiled by imperial decree under the Kangxi Emperor and completed in 1716. It catalogued 47,035 characters under 214 radicals, ordered by stroke count from 1 stroke (一) to 17 strokes (龠).</p>
      <p>This system became the global standard for Chinese dictionaries. It is still used in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Japanese kanji dictionaries today. Simplified Chinese dictionaries sometimes use modified radical lists (186 radicals is one common alternative), but the Kangxi 214 is the reference system underlying Unicode's CJK block organization and most scholarly tools.</p>
      <p>The assignment of a character to its radical is not always obvious — the same character might plausibly belong under two or three components, and traditional dictionary makers made judgment calls that modern learners sometimes find counterintuitive. 道 (way) is filed under 辶 (movement), not under 首 (head), despite 首 being visually prominent.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="semantic"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">表意功能</span><span class="sh-py">biǎoyì gōngnéng</span><span class="sh-en">Radicals as Meaning Signals — Semantic Components</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="意">
      <div class="scholar-label">语义信号 yǔyì xìnhào · Semantic Signals</div>
      <p>Many radicals function as reliable meaning indicators for the characters that contain them. This is most consistent with the <strong>phono-semantic compound</strong> type, which makes up roughly 80–90% of modern Chinese characters. In these characters, one component signals meaning (the semantic radical) and another hints at pronunciation (the phonetic component).</p>
      <p>Examples of radicals with consistent semantic signals: <strong>氵/ 水</strong> (water) — 河 river, 海 sea, 泪 tears, 汗 sweat, 清 clear. <strong>火 / 灬</strong> (fire) — 烧 to burn, 热 hot, 煮 to cook, 炒 to stir-fry. <strong>心 / 忄</strong> (heart/mind) — 爱 love, 思 to think, 忘 to forget, 悲 grief. <strong>木</strong> (wood/tree) — 树 tree, 桌 table, 椅 chair, 森 forest.</p>
      <p>Radicals are not guaranteed meaning-carriers. Some characters have been assigned to radicals purely for indexing convenience, and the radical no longer reflects the character's meaning. 买 (to buy) is filed under 乙 (the second heavenly stem — a bookkeeping radical) in some dictionaries, which tells you nothing about buying. Use radical meanings as probabilistic guides, not rules.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">变体形式</span><span class="sh-py">biàntǐ xíngshì</span><span class="sh-en">Radical Forms — How They Change by Position</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">水 → 氵</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">shuǐ → sān diǎn shuǐ</div>
        <div class="card-en">water → three-dot water (left-side form)</div>
        <div class="card-note">When 水 appears on the left side of a character it compresses to 氵(three dots). 河 hé (river), 海 hǎi (sea), 泪 lèi (tears).</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">火 → 灬</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">huǒ → sì diǎn dǐ</div>
        <div class="card-en">fire → four-dot bottom (bottom form)</div>
        <div class="card-note">When 火 appears at the bottom it becomes four dots 灬. 热 rè (hot), 煮 zhǔ (to cook), 照 zhào (to illuminate).</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">心 → 忄</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">xīn → shù xīn páng</div>
        <div class="card-en">heart → upright heart (left-side form)</div>
        <div class="card-note">心 on the left becomes 忄. 情 qíng (feeling), 怕 pà (to fear), 忘 wàng (to forget).</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">人 → 亻</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">rén → dān rén páng</div>
        <div class="card-en">person → standing person (left-side form)</div>
        <div class="card-note">人 on the left becomes 亻. 你 nǐ (you), 他 tā (he), 做 zuò (to do), 住 zhù (to live).</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">言 → 讠</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">yán → yán zì páng</div>
        <div class="card-en">speech → speech radical (simplified left-side form)</div>
        <div class="card-note">Simplified only: 言 compresses to 讠on the left. 说 shuō (speak), 请 qǐng (invite/please), 语 yǔ (language). Traditional keeps full 言.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">金 → 钅</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">jīn → jīn zì páng</div>
        <div class="card-en">metal → metal radical (simplified left-side form)</div>
        <div class="card-note">Simplified only: 金 compresses to 钅. 钱 qián (money), 铁 tiě (iron), 银 yín (silver). Traditional keeps full 金.</div>
      </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="common"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">常见部首</span><span class="sh-py">chángjiàn bùshǒu</span><span class="sh-en">Common Radicals — The Ones Worth Learning First</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="首">
      <div class="scholar-label">学习优先级 xuéxí yōuxiānjí · Learning Priority</div>
      <p>Of the 214 Kangxi radicals, about 50 appear in the vast majority of common characters. Knowing these 50 allows you to make educated guesses about unfamiliar words and look them up in traditional dictionaries. The highest-frequency radicals in common modern characters:</p>
      <p><strong>人亻</strong> (person) · <strong>口</strong> (mouth) · <strong>手扌</strong> (hand) · <strong>水氵</strong> (water) · <strong>木</strong> (wood) · <strong>心忄</strong> (heart/mind) · <strong>火灬</strong> (fire) · <strong>土</strong> (earth) · <strong>日</strong> (sun) · <strong>月</strong> (moon/flesh) · <strong>言讠</strong> (speech) · <strong>金钅</strong> (metal) · <strong>女</strong> (woman) · <strong>子</strong> (child) · <strong>山</strong> (mountain) · <strong>力</strong> (strength) · <strong>艹</strong> (grass/plant) · <strong>目</strong> (eye) · <strong>糸纟</strong> (silk/thread) · <strong>足</strong> (foot)</p>
      <p>The strategy: don't try to memorize all 214 at once. Learn the radical when you encounter a character that contains it, and build the association through the characters themselves.</p>
    </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">部首</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">bùshǒu</div>
        <div class="card-en">radical · section head</div>
        <div class="card-note">部 (section) + 首 (head). The indexing component of a character in a radical-based dictionary.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">偏旁</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">piānpáng</div>
        <div class="card-en">component · structural element</div>
        <div class="card-note">The broader term for any structural part of a character, whether or not it is the dictionary radical. 偏 (side) + 旁 (side). 偏旁部首 is the full compound: all components including the radical.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">笔画</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">bǐhuà</div>
        <div class="card-en">stroke · stroke count</div>
        <div class="card-note">The individual brush or pen strokes that compose a character. Used to sort characters within the same radical section in a dictionary.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">形声字</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">xíngshēng zì</div>
        <div class="card-en">phono-semantic compound</div>
        <div class="card-note">形 (form/meaning) + 声 (sound) + 字 (character). The dominant character type: one component signals meaning, one hints at sound. ~80% of modern characters.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">象形字</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">xiàngxíng zì</div>
        <div class="card-en">pictograph</div>
        <div class="card-note">Characters that derive from pictorial representations: 日 (sun), 月 (moon), 山 (mountain), 水 (water). The most visually intuitive type, but a small minority of total characters.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">六书</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">liù shū</div>
        <div class="card-en">the Six Scripts — the six ways characters are formed</div>
        <div class="card-note">The classical taxonomy: 象形 (pictograph), 指事 (ideograph), 会意 (logical compound), 形声 (phono-semantic), 转注 (derivative), 假借 (phonetic loan). Codified in the Shuowen Jiezi (100 CE).</div>
