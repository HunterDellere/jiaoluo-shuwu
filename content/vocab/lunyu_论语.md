      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Vocab · Classics · 词汇典籍</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">论语</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">Lún yǔ</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">A thin book of recorded sayings and conversations, compiled by the disciples of Confucius and their students after his death. About sixteen thousand characters, no plot, no system. It became the most quoted text in East Asian civilization and the textbook of imperial education for two millennia.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">字源</span><span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span><span class="sh-en">The Title — Why "Selected Sayings"</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="论">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p><strong>论 lún</strong> (in the title; lùn elsewhere): the character has two main readings. As lùn it means to discuss, to reason, to assess. In the title 论语 the standard pronunciation is lún, with a distinct sense of "to arrange, to compile, to put in order." The early commentator Liu Xiang explained the title precisely on this point: 论 here is editorial, not argumentative. The book is not the discussions of Confucius but the arrangement of his sayings.</p>
      <p><strong>语 yǔ</strong> (sayings; speech; words): from 言 (words) + 吾 (I, phonetic). 语 is more specific than 话 huà; it tends toward formal or recorded speech, the dictum rather than the chat. The title 论语 thus reads literally as "edited sayings" or "compiled words," and in practice as "the things the master said, gathered together."</p>
      <p>The title is descriptive in the most modest possible way. There is no claim to comprehensiveness, no argument for canonical status, no theory. Disciples, after the master's death around 479 BCE, set down what they remembered him saying. Their disciples added what they had heard from them. The book emerged across roughly a century. It contains contradictions, repetitions, and minor anecdotes alongside its weightiest passages. It is perhaps the least systematic foundational text any major civilization has produced.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="structure"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">结构</span><span class="sh-py">jiégòu</span><span class="sh-en">Structure — Twenty Books, No Plot</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="书">
      <div class="scholar-label">文本洞见 wénběn dòngjiàn · Textual Note</div>
      <p>The Analects is divided into twenty books (篇 piān), each named by the first two or three characters of its opening passage rather than by topic. Book 1 is 学而 (xué ér, "learning, and"); book 2 is 为政 (wéi zhèng, "to govern"); book 15 is 卫灵公 (Wèi Líng Gōng, after the duke who appears in the opening line). The naming convention is the same as Genesis being called בראשית in Hebrew, "in the beginning," after its first word.</p>
      <p>Within each book are short passages, traditionally numbered, called 章 zhāng. Most begin with 子曰 (Zǐ yuē, "the Master said"). Others record a question from a disciple and an answer; a few are anecdotes or conversations with rulers. The longest passage runs perhaps three hundred characters; many are under twenty.</p>
      <p>This atomic structure is part of the book's working method. Each saying is meant to be lived with, returned to, applied. The reader does not advance through an argument; they accumulate angles of vision. Two passages may seem to contradict one another (Confucius praising filial piety in one, qualifying it in another) and the resolution is not to choose between them but to hold both.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="themes"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">主题</span><span class="sh-py">zhǔtí</span><span class="sh-en">Core Themes</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">仁</span>
          <span class="card-py">rén</span>
          <span class="card-en">benevolence; humaneness; the cardinal virtue</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N · core concept</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The Analects mentions 仁 over a hundred times and never defines it once. Confucius answers each disciple who asks "what is 仁?" differently, calibrating the answer to the questioner. To Yan Hui he says 克己复礼 (master yourself and return to ritual); to Zigong he gives the negative golden rule, 己所不欲，勿施于人 (what you do not want, do not impose on others). 仁 is the virtue of a fully human person: the disposition to recognize others as oneself, expressed differently in every relation.
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">礼</span>
          <span class="card-py">lǐ</span>
          <span class="card-en">ritual; propriety; the forms of social life</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N · core concept</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The complement to 仁. If 仁 is the inner disposition, 礼 is the outer practice that shapes and expresses it. 礼 covers everything from court ceremony down to how one greets a parent. The Analects treats 礼 as both a discipline that forms the heart and a vocabulary through which the formed heart speaks. 不学礼，无以立 (without studying ritual, one cannot stand firm).
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">君子</span>
          <span class="card-py">jūnzǐ</span>
          <span class="card-en">the exemplary person; the moral ideal</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N · core concept</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The book's implied protagonist. Originally 君子 meant "son of a lord," a person of inherited rank; Confucius redefined it as a person of cultivated virtue, available to anyone who undertakes the work. The 小人 xiǎorén (petty person) is the foil. The Analects's working pedagogy is to draw the contrast across hundreds of small situations: the 君子 worries about his own failings; the 小人 worries about other people's. The 君子 is at ease without being arrogant; the 小人 is arrogant without being at ease.
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-violet">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">学</span>
          <span class="card-py">xué</span>
          <span class="card-en">to learn; to study; lifelong cultivation</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V · core practice</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The book opens with 学: 学而时习之，不亦说乎 (to learn and to practice it in season, is this not a delight?). 学 is the engine. Without it, the disposition toward 仁 stays unformed and the practice of 礼 stays mechanical. Confucius treats learning as the lifelong work of becoming a 君子: never finished, never sufficient, and (his own example) approached with humility about how far one still has to go.
        </div>
      </div>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="famous"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">名句</span><span class="sh-py">míngjù</span><span class="sh-en">Famous Lines</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="言">
      <div class="scholar-label">名句选 míngjù xuǎn · Selected Lines</div>
      <p>Lines from the Analects are quoted across the Chinese-speaking world the way Shakespeare and the Bible are quoted across English. These are a few that any educated speaker recognizes immediately.</p>
      <p class="example"><em>学而时习之，不亦说乎？</em><br>Xué ér shí xí zhī, bù yì yuè hū?<br>To learn and practice it in season, is this not a delight? (Book 1.1, the opening line)</p>
      <p class="example"><em>三十而立，四十而不惑，五十而知天命。</em><br>Sānshí ér lì, sìshí ér bú huò, wǔshí ér zhī tiānmìng.<br>At thirty I stood firm, at forty I had no doubts, at fifty I knew the decrees of Heaven. (Book 2.4)</p>
      <p class="example"><em>己所不欲，勿施于人。</em><br>Jǐ suǒ bú yù, wù shī yú rén.<br>What you do not want, do not impose on others. (Book 15.24, the negative golden rule)</p>
      <p class="example"><em>三人行，必有我师焉。</em><br>Sān rén xíng, bì yǒu wǒ shī yān.<br>Among any three people walking, there must be one I can learn from. (Book 7.22)</p>
      <p class="example"><em>知之为知之，不知为不知，是知也。</em><br>Zhī zhī wéi zhī zhī, bù zhī wéi bù zhī, shì zhì yě.<br>To know what you know and to know what you do not know, this is knowing. (Book 2.17)</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="legacy"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">影响</span><span class="sh-py">yǐngxiǎng</span><span class="sh-en">Imperial Legacy and the Modern Afterlife</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="经">
      <div class="scholar-label">影响洞见 yǐngxiǎng dòngjiàn · Influence</div>
      <p>The Analects is one of the Four Books (四书 sìshū) selected by the Neo-Confucian master Zhu Xi in the twelfth century as the gateway to Confucian study, alongside the Mencius (孟子), the Great Learning (大学), and the Doctrine of the Mean (中庸). When the imperial civil service examinations were reformed under the Yuan and Ming dynasties to focus on the Four Books with Zhu Xi's commentaries, the Analects became, literally, the textbook every aspiring official memorized in childhood and quoted for the rest of their career.</p>
      <p>This continued for roughly six hundred years, until the abolition of the examination system in 1905. Through that long stretch the book shaped how educated people in China, Korea, Vietnam, and Japan thought about virtue, governance, friendship, and self-cultivation. Even the Republican-era and Communist-era critics who attacked Confucianism wrote in language saturated with its phrases.</p>
      <p>In the twenty-first century the Analects has had a quiet renaissance in mainland China, where state-supported scholarship and popular bestsellers (Yu Dan's <em>论语心得</em>, 2006, sold over ten million copies) have reintroduced the text to new readers. It remains assigned reading in Chinese secondary education. Lines from it appear in political speeches, advertising, school slogans, and middle-school essay topics. The book that Confucius's disciples wrote down out of grief at losing their teacher is, two and a half millennia later, still in active circulation.</p>
    </div>
