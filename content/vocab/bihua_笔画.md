      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Language · 语言 yǔyán</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">笔画</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">bǐ huà</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">Chinese characters are built from a small repertoire of standard strokes. Every line in every character is one of these stroke types, written in a prescribed order that shapes both the final appearance and the rhythm of handwriting.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="what"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">什么是笔画</span><span class="sh-py">shénme shì bǐhuà</span><span class="sh-en">What Strokes Are — The Atomic Units</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="笔">
      <div class="scholar-label">定义 dìngyì · Definition</div>
      <p>A <strong>stroke</strong> (笔画 bǐhuà) is a single continuous mark made without lifting the brush or pen. It begins where the instrument touches the surface and ends where it lifts. The number of times you lift the pen equals the number of strokes in the character.</p>
      <p>笔画 literally means "brush mark": 笔 (brush, pen) + 画 (to draw, mark, stroke). The word applies equally to brush calligraphy and ballpoint pen writing; the stroke repertoire is the same.</p>
      <p>Chinese has a relatively small set of stroke types, around 30 named variants in strict calligraphic analysis but reducible to 8 fundamental shapes that cover all characters. Every component, every radical, every full character is assembled from these same building blocks. Knowing the strokes means you can reproduce any character you've seen, even one you've never written before.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="types"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">八种基本笔画</span><span class="sh-py">bā zhǒng jīběn bǐhuà</span><span class="sh-en">The Eight Basic Strokes — 永字八法</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="永">
      <div class="scholar-label">永字八法 yǒng zì bā fǎ · The Eight Methods of 永</div>
      <p>The classical mnemonic for the eight strokes is the character <strong>永 yǒng</strong> (eternity, permanence). It contains all eight fundamental stroke types in a single character, making it the traditional first practice character for beginning calligraphers.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">横 ー</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">héng</div>
        <div class="card-en">horizontal stroke — left to right</div>
        <div class="card-note">The most basic stroke. Runs left to right, slightly rising in brush calligraphy. Found in virtually every character: 一, 三, 王, 土.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">竖 |</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">shù</div>
        <div class="card-en">vertical stroke — top to bottom</div>
        <div class="card-note">Straight down. Can end with a hanging point (悬针竖) or a rounded base (垂露竖). Found in: 中, 十, 口, 日.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">撇 ノ</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">piě</div>
        <div class="card-en">left-falling stroke — top-right to bottom-left</div>
        <div class="card-note">Sweeps from upper right to lower left. One of the most visually distinctive strokes. Found in: 人, 入, 大, 月.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-hanzi">捺 ＼</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">nà</div>
        <div class="card-en">right-falling stroke — top-left to bottom-right, with a "wave"</div>
        <div class="card-note">Sweeps from upper left to lower right, with a characteristic pressure swell and lifting flick at the end. Found in: 人, 大, 木, 文.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">点 ·</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">diǎn</div>
        <div class="card-en">dot — a short angled mark</div>
        <div class="card-note">Not a true dot but a short angled stroke. Direction varies: can fall left or right. Found in: 心, 六, 言, 字. Water radical 氵is three dots.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">折 ∟</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">zhé</div>
        <div class="card-en">turning stroke — changes direction mid-stroke</div>
        <div class="card-note">A stroke that turns without lifting: horizontal then vertical (横折), vertical then horizontal (竖折), etc. Many compound strokes are classified as variants of 折. Found in: 口, 日, 国, 马.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">钩 J</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">gōu</div>
        <div class="card-en">hook — ends with a small kick or flick</div>
        <div class="card-note">A stroke ending with a quick lift that produces a small pointed hook. Variants: 竖钩 (vertical hook), 横钩 (horizontal hook), 弯钩 (curved hook). Found in: 子, 小, 水, 我.</div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-hanzi">提 ↗</div>
        <div class="card-pinyin">tí</div>
        <div class="card-en">rising stroke — short, rising left-to-right</div>
        <div class="card-note">A short stroke that rises from lower-left to upper-right. Seen in the base of 土, 王, and in the leftward radical form of certain components. Less common than the others but unmistakable in calligraphy.</div>
      </div>

    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="order"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">笔顺</span><span class="sh-py">bǐ shùn</span><span class="sh-en">Stroke Order — The Rules</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="顺">
      <div class="scholar-label">笔顺规则 bǐshùn guīzé · Stroke Order Rules</div>
      <p>Stroke order (笔顺 bǐshùn) is the prescribed sequence for writing a character's strokes, developed over two millennia of brushwork to produce the most efficient and aesthetically balanced result. The basic rules:</p>
      <p><strong>Top before bottom.</strong> Write upper components before lower ones: 三 is written top-line, middle-line, bottom-line.</p>
      <p><strong>Left before right.</strong> Write left components before right ones: 明 (bright), write 日 (left) then 月 (right).</p>
      <p><strong>Horizontal before vertical</strong> when strokes cross: write 十 as horizontal first, then vertical.</p>
      <p><strong>Enclosures: frame first, contents second, close last.</strong> 国: write the outer frame (three sides), fill the interior 玉, then close the bottom. The closing stroke comes last so the interior is not trapped.</p>
      <p><strong>Center before sides</strong> in vertically symmetric characters: 小 is written center vertical stroke first, then left dot, then right dot.</p>
      <p><strong>Left-falling before right-falling</strong> when both appear: 人, write 撇 (left-falling) first, then 捺 (right-falling).</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="count"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">笔画数</span><span class="sh-py">bǐhuà shù</span><span class="sh-en">Stroke Count — Why Numbers Matter</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="数">
      <div class="scholar-label">字典用途 zìdiǎn yòngtú · Dictionary Uses</div>
      <p>Stroke count is used in two main ways. First, within a radical section of a traditional dictionary, characters are ordered by the stroke count of their non-radical components. To find 清 (clear): identify radical 氵(3 strokes), subtract from total 11 strokes, look under 8-stroke entries in the water section.</p>
      <p>Second, stroke-count indexes (笔画索引) in dictionaries allow lookup by total stroke count when the radical is unclear, a backup method when you cannot identify which part of a character is the radical.</p>
      <p>Common stroke counts for reference: 一 = 1 stroke. 人 = 2. 山 = 3. 中 = 4. 生 = 5. 字 = 6. 我 = 7. 明 = 8. 南 = 9. 爱 = 10. Characters above 25 strokes are rare in everyday use. The most stroke-heavy character in classical texts is 龘 (dragons walking together) at 48 strokes.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="digital"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">数字时代</span><span class="sh-py">shùzì shídài</span><span class="sh-en">Strokes in the Digital Age — Input Methods</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="电">
      <div class="scholar-label">输入法 shūrùfǎ · Input Methods</div>
      <p>Most Chinese smartphone and computer users type using <strong>pinyin input</strong> (typing the romanization and selecting from a list) or <strong>voice input</strong>. Stroke-based input remains an option, particularly for users who know a character's pronunciation but not its spelling, or for users entering characters on small screens where handwriting recognition works well.</p>
      <p>The <strong>五笔输入法</strong> (Wǔbǐ, "five-stroke input") is a professional input method that maps every character to a sequence of stroke-group codes typed on a standard keyboard. Extremely fast for experienced users, it requires memorization of a full coding table and was dominant in Chinese offices through the 1990s and early 2000s before pinyin input improved.</p>
      <p>Handwriting recognition on modern devices (手写输入 shǒuxiě shūrù) has become accurate enough that the algorithm can usually identify a character even if strokes are written out of order. Correct stroke order still matters for calligraphy, for fast handwriting, and as a cultural competence signal.</p>
    </div>
