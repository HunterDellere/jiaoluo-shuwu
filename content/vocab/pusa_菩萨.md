      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Vocab · 词汇 cíhuì</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">菩萨</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">pú sà</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">菩萨 is the being who attains enlightenment but delays final nirvana out of compassion — vowing to remain in the world until all sentient beings have been liberated first.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="etymology"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字源</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zìyuán</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Etymology — Sanskrit Transliteration</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="萨">
      <div class="scholar-label">字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight</div>
      <p><strong>菩萨</strong> is a phonetic transliteration of Sanskrit <em>bodhisattva</em> (बोधिसत्त्व) — one of the most significant translation choices in the entire history of Buddhism's passage into Chinese. Rather than rendering the meaning directly, early translators chose characters whose sounds approximated the Sanskrit syllables: <strong>菩提</strong> pútí for Sanskrit <em>bodhi</em>, and <strong>萨</strong> sà for Sanskrit <em>sattva</em>.</p>
      <p><strong>菩提</strong> pútí means "awakening" or "enlightenment" in Sanskrit — the same word in <strong>菩提树</strong> pútí shù, the Bodhi tree under which Śākyamuni sat when he attained buddhahood at Bodh Gaya. The tree is a sacred fig (<em>Ficus religiosa</em>); the word 菩提 now carries all the weight of that moment in Chinese Buddhist usage.</p>
      <p><strong>萨</strong> sà renders Sanskrit <em>sattva</em>, meaning "being," "existence," or "sentient creature" — the same root as the Sanskrit compound <em>sarvasattva</em> (all sentient beings). Together, 菩提 + 萨埵 (the fuller transliteration 菩提萨埵 was shortened to 菩萨) means "awakening-being": a being oriented toward and moving toward awakening, for the benefit of all other beings.</p>
      <p>The abbreviation 菩萨 became so established that even when Chinese Buddhists wrote the full term 菩提萨埵 pútí sàduǒ, they understood it as a compound with 菩萨 as its living form. This compression is itself a feature of how Chinese absorbed Buddhist terminology — preserving the sound in a minimal, memorable shape.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="path"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">菩萨道</span>
      <span class="sh-py">púsà dào</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Bodhisattva Path — The Compassion Vow</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="道">
      <div class="scholar-label">菩萨道 púsà dào · The Mahayana Path</div>
      <p>In Mahayana Buddhism (大乘佛教 dàchéng fójiào — the "Great Vehicle"), the bodhisattva ideal is the defining spiritual model. A bodhisattva takes the <strong>菩提心</strong> pútíxīn — the "mind of awakening" — a formal resolution to attain buddhahood for the benefit of all sentient beings (众生 zhòngshēng), and to delay entry into complete nirvana (涅槃 nièpán) until that mission is fulfilled.</p>
      <p>This distinguishes the Mahayana path from the Theravada arhat ideal (罗汉 luóhàn), in which the practitioner seeks individual liberation. The bodhisattva explicitly refuses a private liberation — their vow binds their own release to the release of every other being. The phrase that captures this is: <strong>众生无边誓愿度</strong> — "Sentient beings are boundless; I vow to liberate them all."</p>
      <p>The formal expression of this commitment is the <strong>菩萨戒</strong> púsà jiè — the bodhisattva precepts — a set of vows taken in a ceremony that marks entry onto the bodhisattva path. These precepts include not seeking personal nirvana prematurely and actively working to reduce suffering wherever it exists.</p>
      <p>The bodhisattva path is structured around the <strong>六波罗蜜</strong> liù bōluómì — the six perfections (paramitas) — qualities to be cultivated over countless lifetimes:</p>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">六波罗蜜 liù bōluómì · The Six Perfections of the Bodhisattva Path</span>
      <strong>布施</strong> bùshī — generosity (giving without attachment)<br>
      <strong>持戒</strong> chí jiè — discipline (maintaining the precepts)<br>
      <strong>忍辱</strong> rěnrǔ — patience (enduring difficulty without aversion)<br>
      <strong>精进</strong> jīngjìn — diligent effort (sustained practice without slacking)<br>
      <strong>禅定</strong> chándìng — meditative concentration (samādhi)<br>
      <strong>般若</strong> bōrě — wisdom (prajñā — direct insight into emptiness and interdependence)
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="four"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">四大菩萨</span>
      <span class="sh-py">sì dà púsà</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Four Great Bodhisattvas of Chinese Buddhism</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="菩">
      <div class="scholar-label">四大道场 sì dà dàochǎng · Four Sacred Mountains</div>
      <p>Chinese Buddhism organized its bodhisattva devotion around four sacred mountains (四大名山 sì dà míngshān), each serving as the earthly seat of one great bodhisattva. Pilgrimage to these mountains remains a living practice. Each bodhisattva embodies a primary virtue that defines their mode of compassionate activity.</p>
      <p><strong>观音菩萨</strong> Guānyīn Púsà — Avalokiteśvara, the bodhisattva of compassion (慈悲 cíbēi). The most beloved figure in Chinese popular religion. 普陀山 Pǔtuó Shān (Putuo Island, Zhejiang) is the sacred seat. Originally depicted as male in Indian iconography, Guanyin transformed in Chinese Buddhism into a female form — the "Goddess of Mercy" — from around the Song dynasty onward, reflecting indigenous aesthetic and devotional sensibilities. The name 观音 is short for 观世音 Guān Shìyīn: "perceiving the sounds of the world" — she hears every cry of suffering and responds.</p>
      <p><strong>文殊菩萨</strong> Wénshū Púsà — Mañjuśrī, the bodhisattva of wisdom (智慧 zhìhuì). Always depicted with a sword that cuts through ignorance and a lotus-borne scripture. 五台山 Wǔtái Shān (Five Terrace Mountain, Shanxi) is the sacred seat — one of China's most important pilgrimage mountains, venerated since at least the Tang dynasty.</p>
      <p><strong>普贤菩萨</strong> Pǔxián Púsà — Samantabhadra, the bodhisattva of practice and vow (行愿 xíngyuàn). Rides a white six-tusked elephant. 峨眉山 Éméi Shān (Emei Mountain, Sichuan) is the sacred seat — a mountain famous for its mist, its golden summit, and the "Buddha's glory" rainbow halo phenomenon seen from the summit.</p>
      <p><strong>地藏菩萨</strong> Dìzàng Púsà — Kṣitigarbha, the bodhisattva of the underworld and the vow (大愿 dàyuàn). His vow is the most radical: he will remain in the hells, working to liberate beings from the lowest realms, until the hells are emptied. 九华山 Jiǔhuá Shān (Nine Flower Mountain, Anhui) is the sacred seat — a mountain of forty-nine peaks whose name was changed by the poet Li Bai after he marveled at their flower-like arrangement.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="folk"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">民间信仰</span>
      <span class="sh-py">mínjiān xìnyǎng</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Folk Religion — 菩萨 Beyond Buddhism</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="佛">
      <div class="scholar-label">民间宗教 mínjiān zōngjiào · Popular Religion</div>
      <p>In the religious landscape of everyday Chinese life, 菩萨 has expanded well beyond its precise Mahayana meaning. Ordinary worshippers use the term for virtually any divine figure who can intercede on their behalf — including Daoist deities, local earth gods (土地公 tǔdì gōng), and temple guardians who have no formal Buddhist identity. When a worshipper lights incense at a neighborhood temple and says 菩萨保佑 púsà bǎoyòu ("may the bodhisattva protect [me]"), they may be addressing a figure with no connection to the bodhisattva tradition in any textual sense.</p>
      <p>This blurring is characteristic of Chinese religion as a whole. The three teachings (三教 sānjião — Buddhism, Daoism, Confucianism) were never sharply separated in popular practice. A single temple might contain Guanyin, Guan Yu (the Daoist war god), and a Confucian worthy on neighboring altars, all addressed with similar ritual gestures.</p>
      <p>Guanyin's transformation into a female figure is the most striking example of this folk adaptation. The compassionate mother-figure she represents absorbed elements from pre-existing folk goddesses and resonated far more deeply with Chinese popular devotion than the male Avalokiteśvara of Indian origin. Today her image is present in homes, restaurants, taxis, and fishing boats across the Sinophone world — a figure of protection who belongs to everyone regardless of doctrinal affiliation.</p>
    </div>

        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">菩萨心肠</span>
          <span class="card-py">púsà xīncháng</span>
          <span class="card-en">a bodhisattva's heart; saintly compassion</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词短语 noun phrase</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          心肠 xīncháng = the heart-and-gut, the moral center of a person. 菩萨心肠 describes someone of exceptional compassion and forgiveness — a person who cannot bear to see others suffer and who gives without calculation. Used both admiringly and, occasionally, critically (implying someone is too soft to enforce necessary boundaries).
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">她对每个人都那么好，真是菩萨心肠。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā duì měi gè rén dōu nàme hǎo, zhēn shì púsà xīncháng.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">She is so kind to everyone — a true bodhisattva's heart.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">活菩萨</span>
          <span class="card-py">huó púsà</span>
          <span class="card-en">living bodhisattva; a living saint</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">N 名词 míngcí</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          活 huó = living, alive. Used in folk speech to describe a person of extraordinary virtue or generosity — a doctor who heals the poor without payment, a neighbor who gives without being asked. In Tibetan Buddhism, 活佛 huófó (living Buddha) has a formal meaning (a reincarnated lama); 活菩萨 in Chinese popular usage is informal and affectionate.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">村里的老大夫免费给穷人看病，大家都叫他活菩萨。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Cūn lǐ de lǎo dàifu miǎnfèi gěi qióngrén kàn bìng, dàjiā dōu jiào tā huó púsà.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">The village doctor treats the poor for free — everyone calls him a living bodhisattva.</div>
          </div>
        </div>

      <span class="sh-cn">记忆</span>
      <span class="sh-py">jìyì</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Memory Hook — The Threshold Image</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="菩">
      <div class="scholar-label">记忆钩 jìyì gōu · Retention Image</div>
      <p>Picture the bodhisattva standing at the entrance to nirvana — the final dissolution of suffering, the door every practitioner on the path orients toward. The door is open. They have earned the passage through lifetimes of practice. They could walk through.</p>
      <p>They turn back.</p>
      <p>The turning is the defining gesture. It is the vow made tangible: they will stand at that threshold, indefinitely, calling back to the beings still lost in the cycle — until the last one is ready. Then they will enter together.</p>
      <p>菩萨 is the one who turns back. 菩提 is the awakening they have reached. 萨 is the being they remain — still in the world, still in motion, still helping. The character holds both: the attainment (菩提) and the continued being (萨). Awakening-that-stays.</p>
