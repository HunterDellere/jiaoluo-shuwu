    <span class="section-anchor" id="forms"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Poetic Forms · 诗体 shītǐ</h2>
      <div class="scholar" data-glyph="诗">
        <div class="scholar-label">形式 xíngshì · Form</div>
        <p>The Tang (618–907 CE) did not invent Chinese poetry, but it perfected it. The dynasty inherited older <em>gushi</em> (古诗, "ancient verse") with its loose line lengths, then codified a far more demanding form: <em>jinti</em> (近体, "recent-style verse"), governed by strict rules of tone, parallelism, and line length.</p>
        <p>The flagship form is the <strong>lüshi</strong> (律诗, regulated verse): eight lines of five or seven characters each, with two mandatory couplets in lines 3–4 and 5–6 where every image and grammatical element must be paired. Tonal patterns were specified in detail: each position in the line was assigned either a level tone (平) or oblique tone (仄), creating a counterpoint system as rigorous as any Western sonnet.</p>
        <p>The <strong>jueju</strong> (绝句, "cut short") is a quatrain, four lines of five or seven characters each, and the form most associated with Tang poetry's lapidary perfection. A good jueju says everything in twenty syllables.</p>
      </div>


    <span class="section-anchor" id="libai"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Li Bai · 李白 Lǐ Bái</h2>
      <div class="scholar" data-glyph="白">
        <div class="scholar-label">诗仙 shīxiān · the Immortal Poet</div>
        <p>Li Bai (701–762) is the archetypal Chinese romantic genius: drinker, wanderer, Daoist mystic, impossible to domesticate. He wrote in a style that felt simultaneously effortless and untouchable: ancient diction, surreal imagery, and a bardic confidence that made regulation feel like a straitjacket he had no interest in wearing.</p>
        <p>His most celebrated poem, <strong>静夜思</strong> (Jìng Yè Sī, "Quiet Night Thoughts"), is four lines every Chinese child knows:</p>
        <div class="pattern">
          <div class="pattern-row">
            <span class="pattern-cn">床前明月光，</span>
            <span class="pattern-py">Chuáng qián míng yuè guāng,</span>
            <span class="pattern-en">Before my bed, the bright moonlight —</span>
          </div>
          <div class="pattern-row">
            <span class="pattern-cn">疑是地上霜。</span>
            <span class="pattern-py">Yí shì dì shàng shuāng.</span>
            <span class="pattern-en">I suspect it is frost on the ground.</span>
          </div>
          <div class="pattern-row">
            <span class="pattern-cn">举头望明月，</span>
            <span class="pattern-py">Jǔ tóu wàng míng yuè,</span>
            <span class="pattern-en">I raise my head and gaze at the bright moon,</span>
          </div>
          <div class="pattern-row">
            <span class="pattern-cn">低头思故乡。</span>
            <span class="pattern-py">Dī tóu sī gù xiāng.</span>
            <span class="pattern-en">I lower my head and think of home.</span>
          </div>
        </div>
        <p>Twenty characters. A complete emotional arc: moonlight → frost illusion → looking up → looking down → homesickness. The genius is in the motion: the raised head and lowered head enact the feeling rather than naming it.</p>
      </div>


    <span class="section-anchor" id="dufu"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Du Fu · 杜甫 Dù Fǔ</h2>
      <div class="scholar" data-glyph="甫">
        <div class="scholar-label">诗圣 shīshèng · the Sage Poet</div>
        <p>Where Li Bai soars, Du Fu witnesses. Du Fu (712–770) wrote through the An Lushan Rebellion (755–763), the civil war that tore the Tang apart, and his poetry is inseparable from that catastrophe. He is called <strong>诗圣</strong> (shīshèng, "Sage Poet") because he never looked away from the world.</p>
        <p>His <strong>春望</strong> (Chūn Wàng, "Spring View") begins:</p>
        <div class="pattern">
          <div class="pattern-row">
            <span class="pattern-cn">国破山河在，</span>
            <span class="pattern-py">Guó pò shānhé zài,</span>
            <span class="pattern-en">The state has fallen; mountains and rivers remain.</span>
          </div>
          <div class="pattern-row">
            <span class="pattern-cn">城春草木深。</span>
            <span class="pattern-py">Chéng chūn cǎomù shēn.</span>
            <span class="pattern-en">Spring comes to the city; grass and trees grow deep.</span>
          </div>
        </div>
        <p>The contrast (ruined civilization, indifferent nature) is the whole poem's tension in two lines. Du Fu's regulated parallelism is the tightest in the tradition; every syllable load-bearing.</p>
        <p>Later critics consistently rank him above Li Bai as a <em>craftsman</em>, even while acknowledging Li Bai's greater charisma. The two met once, in 744, and spent several months traveling together, one of the great literary friendships in world history.</p>
      </div>


    <span class="section-anchor" id="themes"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Enduring Themes · 主题 zhǔtí</h2>
      <div class="cards">
        <div class="card c-ochre">
          <div class="card-head">月 yuè · The Moon</div>
          <p>The moon is the single most common image in Tang poetry, linked to homesickness (思乡), the passage of time, and the absent friend. Because it is the same moon everywhere, it connects separated people. Hundreds of Tang poems end with someone looking at the moon and thinking of someone far away.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-teal">
          <div class="card-head">送别 sòngbié · Parting</div>
          <p>Travel in Tang China was slow, dangerous, and often permanent. The farewell poem (送别诗) is its own genre: the traveler leaves, the friend watches from the shore or city gate, and both know they may never meet again. Wang Wei's quiet farewells are its masterpieces.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-sienna">
          <div class="card-head">边塞 biānsài · Frontier</div>
          <p>The Tang military frontier (Central Asia, the Gobi, the northern steppes) produced its own sub-genre of frontier poetry (边塞诗). Sand, cold, homesick soldiers, the sound of the flute at night. Wang Changling and Cen Shen are its greatest voices.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-red">
          <div class="card-head">隐居 yǐnjū · Reclusion</div>
          <p>The retired official living among mountains and pines, writing about pine shadows, visiting monks, and ignoring the court, is a persistent Tang persona. Wang Wei lived this life after his political embarrassment during the An Lushan Rebellion and made the mountain recluse poem a genre unto itself.</p>
        </div>
      </div>


    <span class="section-anchor" id="legacy"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Legacy · 影响 yǐngxiǎng</h2>
      <div class="scholar" data-glyph="唐">
        <div class="scholar-label">传承 chuánchéng · Transmission</div>
        <p>The <strong>唐诗三百首</strong> (Tángshī Sānbǎi Shǒu, "Three Hundred Tang Poems"), compiled in 1763 by Sun Zhu, is the most widely reprinted anthology in Chinese history. It is still the standard curriculum in Chinese primary schools, still recited by children who have no idea what half the words mean, still the shared vocabulary of Chinese literary culture.</p>
        <p>To know Tang poetry is to participate in something larger than yourself. When a Chinese person quotes <em>举头望明月</em> in a moment of homesickness, they are citing a poem that has confirmed the same feeling for every reader between Li Bai and the present moment.</p>
        <p>Japan absorbed the Tang poetic tradition wholesale during the Nara period, and the forms influenced waka and haiku. The Tang influence on Korean sijo and Vietnamese poetry is equally profound. No other poetic tradition of the same era reached this geographic and temporal range.</p>
      </div>


    <span class="section-anchor" id="vocab"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Key Vocabulary · 词汇 cíhuì</h2>
      <div class="cards">
        <div class="card c-ochre">
          <div class="card-head"><span class="tag-n">n</span> 律诗 lǜshī</div>
          <p>Regulated verse — eight lines with strict tonal and parallelism rules. The dominant Tang poetic form.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-ochre">
          <div class="card-head"><span class="tag-n">n</span> 绝句 juéjù</div>
          <p>Quatrain — four lines of five or seven characters. The most concentrated Tang form.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-teal">
          <div class="card-head"><span class="tag-n">n</span> 对仗 duìzhàng</div>
          <p>Tonal and semantic parallelism — the requirement that paired couplet lines mirror each other grammatically, semantically, and tonally.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-teal">
          <div class="card-head"><span class="tag-n">n</span> 意象 yìxiàng</div>
          <p>Image, imagistic motif. Tang criticism valued the evocative image (意象) over explicit statement — show, don't tell, carried to its highest refinement.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-sienna">
          <div class="card-head"><span class="tag-n">n</span> 诗仙 shīxiān</div>
          <p>Immortal Poet — honorific for Li Bai, whose verse seemed to transcend ordinary human craft.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-sienna">
          <div class="card-head"><span class="tag-n">n</span> 诗圣 shīshèng</div>
          <p>Sage Poet — honorific for Du Fu, whose verse embodied moral gravity and historical witness.</p>
        </div>
