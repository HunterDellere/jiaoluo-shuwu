    <header class="topic-hero">
      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Philosophy · 哲学 zhéxué</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">正名</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">zhèngmíng</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">Confucius told Zilu that governing begins with calling things by their right names , because a ruler who does not rule like a ruler has no claim to the title 君.</p>
    </header>

    <!-- ── THE DOCTRINE ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="doctrine"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">学说</span>
      <span class="sh-py">xuéshuō</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Doctrine — Analects 13.3</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="正">
      <div class="scholar-label">经典文本 jīngdiǎn wénběn · Locus Classicus</div>
      <p>In <em>Analects</em> 13.3, Zilu asks Confucius: if the Duke of Wei were to ask you to govern, what would you do first? Confucius replies: 必也正名乎 , "I would certainly rectify names." Zilu pushes back: that sounds impractical, too far removed from actual governance. Confucius rebukes him sharply, then explains: 名不正则言不顺，言不顺则事不成 , "If names are not correct, speech does not follow; if speech does not follow, affairs cannot be completed." The argument continues down a chain: if affairs cannot be completed, ritual and music collapse; if ritual and music collapse, punishment misses its mark; if punishment misses its mark, the people have no ground to stand on.</p>
      <p>The claim is not about precision or pedantry. Confucius is making a political and moral argument. The titles of roles , 君 jūn (ruler), 臣 chén (minister), 父 fù (father), 子 zǐ (son) , are not mere labels. Each title carries a normative weight: to hold it is to be bound by the conduct it implies. A ruler who does not rule in the way a ruler should be ruling has no legitimate claim to the word 君. The title, worn without the conduct, is a lie embedded in the fabric of government. Everything downstream of that lie is corrupted.</p>
      <p>The Confucian formula for this in practice appears elsewhere in the <em>Analects</em> (12.11): 君君，臣臣，父父，子子 , "Let the ruler be a ruler, the minister a minister, the father a father, the son a son." The repetition enacts the doctrine: use the name only where the reality is present.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── POLITICAL CONTEXT ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="context"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">政治背景</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zhèngzhì bèijǐng</span>
      <span class="sh-en">The Duke of Wei and a Pointed Answer</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="名">
      <div class="scholar-label">历史背景 lìshǐ bèijǐng · Historical Context</div>
      <p>The Duke of Wei in Analects 13.3 was <strong>卫出公 Wèi Chūgōng</strong>, who ruled the state of Wei from 480 to 478 BCE (approximately, during Confucius's lifetime). His situation was a tangled succession: his grandfather Duke Ling had died in 493 BCE; his father Kuai Kui had been exiled for an assassination plot and was living abroad; the throne passed to Kuai Kui's son , which meant the reigning duke had taken a position that, by the logic of seniority and filial obligation, belonged to his father.</p>
      <p>Confucius was at that time a guest of Wei. When Zilu asked what the Master would do first if asked to govern there, the answer about rectifying names was not a philosophical abstraction. It was a diagnosis of the specific pathology in front of them: the state was governed by a man whose title as ruler was undercut by the fact that his own father's claim preceded his. A ruler whose legitimacy rested on displacing a father could not be called 君 in the full sense the word required. Governance under those conditions was, from the start, speech that did not follow from correct names.</p>
      <p>The doctrine was never abstract. Every invocation of 正名 in the classical period points back to a concrete case where a title was being worn by someone whose conduct did not match what the title demanded.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── WHAT NAME MEANS ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="ming"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">名的含义</span>
      <span class="sh-py">míng de hányì</span>
      <span class="sh-en">名 — The Duty Inside the Title</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="分">
      <div class="scholar-label">概念分析 gàiniàn fēnxī · Conceptual Analysis</div>
      <p>In classical Chinese thought, 名 míng (name) is a claim about what something is and therefore what it should do. To assign a name is to assign an obligation. This is the substance behind the compound 名分 míngfèn , "name and portion" , where 分 fèn means one's allotted share, duty, or station. The name entails the 分: to bear the name 臣 is to accept the conduct that a minister owes a ruler. Remove the conduct and the name becomes empty; retain only the name without the conduct and the name becomes a deception.</p>
      <p>The paired term 名实 míng shí (name and reality) captures the alignment that 正名 seeks to restore. 名 is what something is called; 实 is what it actually is. The goal of rectifying names is to close the gap between these two: to make the name a true report on the reality, or to refuse the name where the reality is absent. The Han dynasty later systematized this logic in the examination and recommendation system 察举 cháju, which evaluated officials against the standard their title implied. A man recommended as a filial son was held to the conduct that filial sonship required; if the reality and the title diverged, both the man and his recommender faced censure.</p>
    </div>

In modern Chinese, 正名 has acquired a narrower administrative sense: to officially rehabilitate someone's reputation or restore their proper designation after a period of false labeling, political persecution, or unjust censure. The classical and modern senses share the same core logic: closing the gap between what someone is called and what they actually are.

            <div class="ex-en">He did not keep to his station , though a minister, he meddled in the ruler's affairs.</div>
          </div>

        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">名实</span>
          <span class="card-py">míng shí</span>
          <span class="card-en">name and reality — the alignment that 正名 seeks</span>
        </div>
        <div class="card-def">
          名 míng (name) + 实 shí (reality, actuality). The pair names the problem: when 名 and 实 diverge, something false is being presented to the world. 名实相符 (name and reality correspond) is the condition 正名 aims to restore. The Mohists and later the Legalists were also preoccupied with 名实 alignment, but from different directions: the Mohists wanted formal definitions that held across cases; the Legalists (especially <strong>韩非子 Hánfēizǐ</strong>) used 名实 as an instrument of control , hold the minister to the exact claims his title and proposals entail, and reward or punish accordingly.

            <div class="ex-en">The name does not match the reality , an empty title only.</div>

        <div class="card-def">
          The Analects 12.11 formula for 正名 in practice, given when Duke Jing of Qi asked Confucius about governance. Each binome repeats the same character twice: the first instance is the title, the second is the standard the title entails. The structure does what it says , it uses a name to define the norm the name demands. When Duke Jing heard it, he reportedly replied: "Excellent! If the ruler is not a ruler, the minister not a minister, the father not a father, and the son not a son , even if I have grain, how would I get to eat it?"

    <!-- ── LATER USES ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="later"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">后世用法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">hòushì yòngfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Later Uses — Neo-Confucian, Modern, and Political</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="格">
      <div class="scholar-label">思想流变 sīxiǎng liúbiàn · Intellectual Afterlife</div>
      <p>The Song dynasty Neo-Confucians absorbed 正名 into their broader project of moral cultivation. <strong>朱熹 Zhū Xī</strong> (1130–1200 CE) placed the rectification of names alongside 格物 gé wù (the investigation of things) as a preliminary to all moral knowledge: before you can act correctly in a role, you must understand what that role actually demands. The concept 名分之教 , the teaching that each person's station entails specific moral obligations , became a structuring principle of Zhu Xi's social ethics and was used to organize everything from family relations to official conduct.</p>
      <p>In the twentieth century, 正名 acquired a specifically political meaning in Chinese: the official restoration of someone's reputation or status after unjust political treatment. During and after the Cultural Revolution (1966–1976), many officials, intellectuals, and ordinary people were falsely labeled as class enemies or counterrevolutionaries. The process of reversing these labels in the 1970s and 1980s was called 平反正名 píngfǎn zhèngmíng , "reversing a verdict and rectifying the name." The classical doctrine and the modern administrative act share the same logic: a false name attached to a person is a wrong that demands correction.</p>
      <p>In Taiwan, 正名 has become a term in sovereignty politics. Advocates for changing the formal name of the Republic of China to Taiwan invoke 正名 as a demand that the state's official title match the political reality of what and where it actually is. The doctrine of the Confucian tradition, originally aimed at the conduct of rulers and ministers in 5th-century BCE Wei, provides the vocabulary for a 21st-century constitutional debate.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── MOHIST AND DAOIST CRITIQUES ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="contrast"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">墨道两家的批评</span>
      <span class="sh-py">mò dào liǎng jiā de pīpíng</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Mohist and Daoist Critiques</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="辩">
      <div class="scholar-label">思想对比 sīxiǎng duìbǐ · Philosophical Contrast</div>
      <p>The Mohists developed 名辩 míngbiàn , a formal theory of names closer to logic than ethics. Where Confucius was concerned with whether a ruler's conduct matched his title, the Mohists asked how definitions could be made precise enough to hold across all cases without ambiguity. Their 辩 (disputation) tradition distinguished three types of names, analyzed how definitions are confirmed by comparison to objects, and tested arguments for consistency. The Mohist project is about the formal correctness of names across propositions; the Confucian project is about the moral weight names carry in social roles. These are different questions dressed in similar vocabulary.</p>
      <p>The Daoists rejected the entire project from the other direction. The opening lines of the 道德经 Dàodéjīng state: 道可道，非常道；名可名，非常名 , "The Dao that can be spoken is not the eternal Dao; the name that can be named is not the eternal name." For Laozi, applying any stable name to the Dao falsifies it, because the Dao exceeds every category language can provide. Names are human impositions on a reality that precedes and exceeds them. Where Confucius says names must match reality, Laozi says the reality that matters most cannot be captured by names at all.</p>
      <p>正名 sits in productive tension with both positions. Against the Mohists, Confucius is not building a formal theory of definition but a political and moral demand: stop using titles as though they carried no obligations. Against the Daoists, the Confucian says the domain of social life , governance, family, ritual , is precisely where names do carry weight, and where the failure to hold people to the standards their titles imply causes real harm. The Daoist dissolving of names happens at the level of metaphysics; 正名 operates at the level of courts, families, and the daily conduct of people who hold positions.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">成语</span>
      <span class="sh-py">chéngyǔ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Idioms on Name and Reality</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>

    <div class="chengyu-grid">

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">名不副实</span>
        <span class="cy-py">míng bù fù shí</span>
        <span class="cy-en">name does not match reality — the condition 正名 diagnoses</span>
        <span class="cy-note">名 (name) + 不 (does not) + 副 (match, correspond to) + 实 (reality). The set phrase for the gap that 正名 targets. Still in common use across every register: a product that doesn't live up to its marketing is 名不副实, as is a self-proclaimed expert who cannot answer questions in their supposed field, or a ruler who governs corruptly while holding the title of 君. The antonym 名副其实 míng fù qí shí , "the name matches the reality" , is used as a strong compliment.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">名正言顺</span>
        <span class="cy-py">míng zhèng yán shùn</span>
        <span class="cy-en">the name is correct and the words follow — authority grounded in legitimate title</span>
        <span class="cy-note">Drawn directly from the logic of Analects 13.3: when names are correct (名正), speech flows naturally (言顺) because the title and the conduct behind it are genuinely aligned. Used today to describe a situation where someone acts with full legitimacy: they hold the right title, have the proper authority, and are doing exactly what their role entitles them to do. The phrase carries a slight formal or official flavor — common in bureaucratic, legal, and news contexts where the question of legitimate mandate is at stake.</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">名实相符</span>
        <span class="cy-py">míng shí xiāng fú</span>
        <span class="cy-en">name and reality correspond — the goal of 正名</span>
        <span class="cy-note">名 (name) + 实 (reality) + 相符 (mutually correspond, match). The affirmative formulation of what 正名 aims to restore. Where 名不副实 names the problem and 名正言顺 names the condition of legitimacy in action, 名实相符 states the standard of alignment itself. Used as both a compliment — "the praise is fully deserved" — and as a criterion in evaluation: does the label accurately describe the thing?</span>
      </div>

      <div class="cy">
        <span class="cy-cn">徒有虚名</span>
        <span class="cy-py">tú yǒu xūmíng</span>
        <span class="cy-en">nothing but an empty name — a title with no substance behind it</span>
        <span class="cy-note">徒 (merely, only) + 有 (have) + 虚名 (empty name). The sharpest everyday formulation of what 正名 opposes. A famous general who wins no battles is 徒有虚名. A top university whose graduates learn nothing is 徒有虚名. The phrase appears frequently in critical commentary and is considerably more pointed than 名不副实, carrying a note of contempt for the pretension involved in holding the title without earning it. The 虚 (empty, hollow) is the heart of it: the name is structurally present but functionally void.</span>
      </div>
