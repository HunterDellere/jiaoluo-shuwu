  <p class="topic-hero-desc">Chinese tea is a single leaf — <em>Camellia sinensis</em> — transformed six different ways by oxidation and processing. This path walks the full spectrum, from fresh-green to aged-dark, then turns to ceremony and craft.</p>
</header>

<span class="section-anchor" id="overview"></span>
<div class="section-head"><h2>The Six Classes</h2></div>
<div class="scholar" data-glyph="茶">
  <div class="scholar-label">六大茶类 · liù dà chá lèi</div>
  <p>China's tea taxonomy is built on one variable: how much oxygen the leaf is allowed to touch before heat stops the process. At one pole sits green tea (no oxidation); at the other, fully-oxidised black tea. In between come white, yellow, oolong, and dark teas. Post-fermented dark teas — including 普洱 Pǔ'ěr — form a class of their own, aged rather than simply dried.</p>
  <p>Herbal tisanes (花草茶 huācǎo chá) fall outside the six-class system entirely: they use no tea leaf, so they carry no caffeine and no place on the oxidation ladder.</p>
</div>

<div class="section-head"><h2>Reading Path</h2></div>

  <div class="card c-teal">

      <span class="card-cn">字 · The Character</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/characters/cha2_茶.html">茶 · chá</a> — Start here: the glyph itself, its etymology (grass radical over 木+人), and the compounds that radiate outward into everything tea touches.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-teal">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">茶 · Overview</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_cha.html">茶 · Tea</a> — The broad frame: how a single plant became the world's most-consumed beverage, the role of the Tang dynasty in standardising the culture, and the vocabulary of tasting.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-ochre">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">绿茶 · Green</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_lücha.html">绿茶 · Green Tea</a> — Unoxidised. The oldest processed form; steamed in Japan, pan-fired in China. Longjing and Biluochun sit at the top of the canon.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-ochre">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">白茶 · White</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_baicha.html">白茶 · White Tea</a> — Barely touched: the bud and young leaves are simply withered and dried. Silvery, subtle, and fast-rising in international markets.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-ochre">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">黄茶 · Yellow</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_huangcha.html">黄茶 · Yellow Tea</a> — The rarest class. A brief smothering step (渥黄 wò huáng) after firing mutes the grassiness of green without full oxidation.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-sienna">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">乌龙 · Oolong</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_wulong.html">乌龙茶 · Oolong Tea</a> — Partially oxidised (15–85%), shaped into balls or twisted strips. The broadest flavour range of any class: floral Tieguanyin to roasted rock oolongs.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-sienna">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">红茶 · Black</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_hongcha.html">红茶 · Red Tea (Black Tea)</a> — Fully oxidised. What the West calls "black tea," Chinese calls red — for the colour of the liquor, not the leaf. Keemun and Dianhong are the benchmarks.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-sienna">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">黑茶 · Dark</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_heisha.html">黑茶 · Dark Tea & Pu-erh</a> — Post-fermented and often aged for years or decades. 普洱 Pǔ'ěr from Yunnan is the most famous; compressed cakes can be stored like wine.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-violet">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">花草茶 · Tisanes</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culinary/topic_tisanes.html">花草茶 · Tisanes & Herbal Infusions</a> — Outside the six: chrysanthemum, osmanthus, rose, and dozens of medicinal herbs steeped without a leaf of Camellia sinensis.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-violet">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">工夫茶 · Ceremony</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/vocab/gongfucha_工夫茶.html">工夫茶 · Gongfu Tea</a> — The slow art of brewing. Small pot, tiny cups, multiple short steeps — a discipline that turns preparation into attention.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-violet">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">茶具 · Utensils</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/vocab/chaju_茶具.html">茶具 · Tea Implements</a> — Gaiwans, Yixing clay pots, fairness cups, and bamboo tongs: the material culture of the table.</p>
  </div>

  <div class="card c-red">
    <div class="card-head">
      <span class="card-cn">茶文化 · Culture</span>
    </div>
    <p><a href="../../pages/culture/topic_cha_wenhua.html">茶文化 · Tea Culture</a> — Tea and politics, tea and poetry, tea and social hierarchy: how the leaf became a thread woven through all of Chinese civilisation.</p>
  </div>
</div>
