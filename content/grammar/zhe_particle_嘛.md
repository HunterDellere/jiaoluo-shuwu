    <header class="topic-hero">
      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Grammar · 语法 yǔfǎ</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">嘛</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">ma · (neutral tone)</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">A neutral-tone particle that frames whatever precedes it as already obvious — the Mandarin equivalent of "obviously," "of course," or the exasperated "it's right there."</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="overview"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">概览</span>
      <span class="sh-py">gàilǎn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">What 嘛 Does</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="嘛">
      <div class="scholar-label">语法洞见 yǔfǎ dòngjiàn · Grammar Insight</div>
      <p><strong>嘛 ma</strong> is a sentence-final modal particle (语气词 yǔqìcí), pronounced in the neutral tone (轻声 qīngshēng). It belongs to the same family as 吧 ba, 呢 ne, 啊 a, and 吗 ma — all of which attach to the end of a sentence to modify its pragmatic force without changing what the sentence says.</p>
      <p>嘛 has one core job: it tells the listener that the speaker's statement is <em>obvious</em>, self-evident, or already inferrable from the context. The speaker is not making a surprising claim; they are pointing at something the listener should already know, or could easily work out. In English this maps loosely to "obviously," "of course," "you know," or the slightly weary "it's just that..." depending on context.</p>
      <p>This single function branches into three distinct tones of voice: calm explanation (offering the obvious reason for something), soft justification (framing one's own choices as the natural thing to do), and mild exasperation (frustration that the obvious had to be stated at all). The particle is the same in all three. The tone shifts with context and intonation.</p>
      <p>嘛 is also distinct from its near-homophone 吗 ma, the yes/no question particle. The characters are different, the functions are completely different, and in careful speech the prosody differs slightly. Learners who hear 嘛 in conversation often mis-analyze it as 吗 and then wonder why nothing seems to be a question.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="obvious"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">显而易见</span>
      <span class="sh-py">xiǎn ér yì jiàn</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Function 1 · Stating the Obvious</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">基本用法 jīběn yòngfǎ · Core Pattern</span>
      <strong>[Statement] + 嘛</strong> → marks the statement as self-evident; the listener should already know or can easily see this<br>
      他是学生嘛。He's a student — (obviously, that's the point). ／ 这很简单嘛。This is simple — (come on, it's not hard). ／ 因为我喜欢嘛。Because I like it — (what other reason would there be?).
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-v">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">Statement + 嘛</span>
          <span class="card-py">Statement + ma</span>
          <span class="card-en">___, obviously / ___, of course / ___, as you can see</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">Particle 语气词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The speaker asserts something they consider self-evident and signals that the listener should already know it, or can easily infer it from what's around them. The claim is not up for debate. 嘛 closes the statement rather than opening it to discussion — no confirmation is being sought, no hedge is being added.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他是学生嘛，当然没有钱。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā shì xuésheng ma, dāngrán méiyǒu qián.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He's a student, obviously he has no money.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这很简单嘛，你再想想。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè hěn jiǎndān ma, nǐ zài xiǎngxiang.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This is simple — just think about it again.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">因为我喜欢嘛，还需要什么理由？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Yīnwèi wǒ xǐhuān ma, hái xūyào shénme lǐyóu?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Because I like it — what other reason do you need?</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">北京冬天冷嘛，多穿点儿。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Běijīng dōngtiān lěng ma, duō chuān diǎnr.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Beijing winters are cold, as you know — dress warmly.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他不吃辣嘛，点菜的时候注意一下。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā bù chī là ma, diǎncài de shíhou zhùyì yīxià.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He doesn't eat spicy food — you know that — so keep it in mind when ordering.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">语感 yǔgǎn · Feel</span>
          The speaker is not explaining something new. They are pointing at something already in the shared context — a fact the listener knows, a consequence that follows logically, a reason that should be obvious. The tone is matter-of-fact rather than urgent.
        </div>
      </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="justification"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">解释</span>
      <span class="sh-py">jiěshì</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Function 2 · Softened Justification</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">解释用法 jiěshì yòngfǎ · Justification Pattern</span>
      <strong>[Explanation or choice] + 嘛</strong> → frames one's own action or decision as the natural, logical thing to do<br>
      我不去嘛，太远了。I'm not going — it's too far (obviously). ／ 你问他嘛，他最清楚。Ask him — he obviously knows best.
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">Action/Choice + 嘛</span>
          <span class="card-py">Action/Choice + ma</span>
          <span class="card-en">___ is the natural/logical thing; of course ___ (when explaining oneself)</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">Particle 语气词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          When explaining one's own choices, behavior, or reasoning, 嘛 frames the explanation as the logical outcome anyone would arrive at. The speaker sounds neither apologetic nor forceful: the course of action was simply obvious. This use is common in casual spoken Mandarin when someone is accounting for a decision they made.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我不去嘛，太远了，而且我没时间。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ bú qù ma, tài yuǎn le, érqiě wǒ méi shíjiān.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I'm not going — it's too far, and I don't have time. (obviously)</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">你问他嘛，他做过这个，他最清楚。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Nǐ wèn tā ma, tā zuòguo zhège, tā zuì qīngchǔ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Ask him — he's done this before, he obviously knows best.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我选这个嘛，价格合适，质量也不错。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ xuǎn zhège ma, jiàgé héshì, zhìliàng yě búcuò.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I picked this one — the price is right and the quality is decent. (the logic is obvious)</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我早走嘛，路上堵车，不早点儿走不行。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ zǎo zǒu ma, lùshang dǔchē, bù zǎodiǎnr zǒu bùxíng.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I left early — the traffic, obviously — you have to leave early.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">语感 yǔgǎn · Feel</span>
          The effect is de-escalating. Saying 我不去 alone sounds like a flat refusal. Adding 嘛 reframes it: the decision follows naturally from circumstances the listener can see for themselves. It does not apologize; it explains by pointing at the obvious.
        </div>
      </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="exasperation"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">无奈</span>
      <span class="sh-py">wúnài</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Function 3 · Mild Exasperation</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">无奈用法 wúnài yòngfǎ · Exasperation Pattern</span>
      <strong>[Statement the listener missed] + 嘛</strong> → mild frustration that the obvious had to be said<br>
      你早说嘛！You should have said so earlier! ／ 这不对嘛。This isn't right — (can't you see?).
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-red">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">Overlooked fact + 嘛</span>
          <span class="card-py">Overlooked fact + ma</span>
          <span class="card-en">___ obviously! / come on, ___! / that's clearly ___</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">Particle 语气词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          When the listener has missed something the speaker considers obvious, 嘛 carries a note of mild frustration. The speaker is not accusing the listener of stupidity; they are signaling that the conclusion was available, and the listener should have reached it. The exasperation is restrained. It implies "the answer was right there."
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">你早说嘛！我早就可以帮你了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Nǐ zǎo shuō ma! Wǒ zǎo jiù kěyǐ bāng nǐ le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">You should have said so earlier! I could have helped you long ago.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这不对嘛，你再看一遍。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhè bú duì ma, nǐ zài kàn yī biàn.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">This isn't right — (obviously) — look at it again.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">答案就在书上嘛，你怎么没找到？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Dá'àn jiù zài shū shang ma, nǐ zěnme méi zhǎodào?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">The answer is right there in the book — how did you miss it?</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">说清楚嘛，我不是你肚子里的蛔虫。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Shuō qīngchǔ ma, wǒ bú shì nǐ dùzi lǐ de huíchóng.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Say it clearly — I'm not a mind reader.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">语气调节 yǔqì tiáojié · Calibrating the Tone</span>
          The line between warm explanation and mild exasperation is entirely in delivery. 你早说嘛 with a relaxed falling tone reads as "you could have just told me." Said sharply with a rising-then-falling contour, it tips into reproach. Context and the relationship between speakers determine which reading lands.
        </div>
      </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="contrast"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">辨析</span>
      <span class="sh-py">biànxī</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Contrast · 嘛 vs 吧 vs 呢</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">三粒子比较 sān lìzǐ bǐjiào · Three Particles Compared</span>
      <strong>嘛</strong> ma → commits to a claim and asserts its obviousness: "X, as you can see / should know"<br>
      <strong>吧</strong> ba → hedges and invites agreement: "X, right?" / "let's do X" / "probably X"<br>
      <strong>呢</strong> ne → continues or questions: "and what about X?" / softens ongoing questions / marks a state as in progress<br><br>
      The test: 嘛 closes. 吧 reaches toward the listener for agreement. 呢 opens outward.
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="嘛">
      <div class="scholar-label">练习 liànxí · Practice Contrast</div>
      <p>Take the sentence <strong>他不来。</strong> "He's not coming." Add each particle and feel the difference:</p>
      <p><strong>他不来嘛。</strong> = He's not coming — obviously. (the speaker is stating a fact they consider self-evident; no response is being sought)<br>
      <strong>他不来吧？</strong> = He's not coming, right? (the speaker thinks so but wants confirmation; the question is open)<br>
      <strong>他不来呢？</strong> = He's not coming? / What about him not coming? (musing, wondering, or following up on a previous point)</p>
      <p>嘛 and 吧 are often the hardest to separate because both can follow declarative sentences. The distinction is in stance: 嘛 needs no agreement from the listener because the claim is already obvious; 吧 is reaching toward the listener because the speaker wants the claim validated. A speaker who says 他不来嘛 does not expect a reply. A speaker who says 他不来吧？ is genuinely checking.</p>
      <p>呢 belongs to a different register of interaction. Where 嘛 and 吧 are about the speaker's relationship to a claim, 呢 is about the shape of the conversation — keeping it open, returning it, or marking something as ongoing.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-teal">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">嘛 vs 吧 — side by side</span>
          <span class="card-py">ma vs ba</span>
          <span class="card-en">The most common confusion</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">Particle 语气词</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          Both follow declarative sentences. The difference is whether the speaker is asserting an obvious truth (嘛) or checking a shared assumption (吧). In practice: if the speaker already knows and is just pointing, use 嘛. If the speaker believes something but wants the listener to confirm, use 吧.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他是老师嘛。（陈述事实，不需要回应）</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā shì lǎoshī ma. (chénshù shìshí, bù xūyào huíyìng)</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He's a teacher — obviously. (stating a fact; no reply needed)</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他是老师吧？（寻求确认）</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā shì lǎoshī ba? (xúnqiú quèrèn)</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He's a teacher, right? (seeking confirmation)</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这样做嘛，肯定没问题。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhèyàng zuò ma, kěndìng méi wèntí.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Do it this way — obviously there'll be no problem.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">这样做吧，应该没问题。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zhèyàng zuò ba, yīnggāi méi wèntí.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Let's do it this way — it should be fine. (suggestion, slightly uncertain)</div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="register"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">语域</span>
      <span class="sh-py">yǔyù</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Tone and Register</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="嘛">
      <div class="scholar-label">语域说明 yǔyù shuōmíng · Register Notes</div>
      <p>嘛 is colloquial spoken Mandarin. It rarely appears in formal writing, official documents, or prepared speeches. In casual dialogue, texting, social media comments, and everyday conversation, it is extremely common. Novels and screenplays use it freely in character dialogue as a marker of natural, unguarded speech.</p>
      <p>In northern Mandarin dialects, Beijing speech especially, 嘛 is very frequent and carries particular warmth when used between friends or family. A parent explaining something to a child, a friend accounting for a choice, a colleague pointing out the obvious without wanting to sound harsh — 嘛 fits all of these. The particle signals that the speaker is not performing authority; they are just pointing at what is plainly the case.</p>
      <p>Two fixed expressions built around 嘛 appear frequently in colloquial speech and are worth knowing as single units: <strong>可不是嘛</strong> (kě bú shì ma) — "isn't that the truth" / "exactly, obviously" — used to strongly agree with something just said; and <strong>对嘛</strong> (duì ma) — "right, obviously" / "that's it" — a short affirmation that what was just said or done is correct. Both lean on 嘛's function of treating the conclusion as already obvious and simply confirming it aloud.</p>
      <p>The only consistent pitfall for learners is confusing 嘛 ma with 吗 ma in listening. In careful speech they have slightly different prosody: 吗 in a question tends to carry a slight rise, while 嘛 in an obvious-conclusion statement falls or stays flat. In fast casual speech the difference is subtle. Context is the more reliable guide: if the sentence is clearly a statement and nothing is being asked, what you heard is almost certainly 嘛.</p>
