    <span class="section-anchor" id="intro"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">介绍</span><span class="sh-py">jièshào</span><span class="sh-en">Introduction: What This Text Is</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="心">
      <div class="scholar-label">经文洞见 jīngwén dòngjiàn · About the Text</div>
      <p>The Heart Sutra is the shortest and most chanted Buddhist scripture in East Asia. The full Sanskrit title is <em>Prajñāpāramitāhṛdaya Sūtra</em>; <em>hṛdaya</em> can be rendered as "heart" in the sense of essence or core, and that is what the text claims to be: the condensed essence of the vast Prajñāpāramitā literature, which in its longest form runs to twenty-five thousand and a hundred thousand verses. The Heart Sutra distills the doctrine of emptiness (空 kōng) into a single page that can be chanted in under three minutes.</p>
      <p>The text takes the form of a discourse delivered by the bodhisattva Avalokiteśvara (观自在菩萨 Guānzìzài Púsà, "the bodhisattva of sovereign perception" in this version of the title) to the disciple Śāriputra. Avalokiteśvara reports what he saw when practicing the deep prajñāpāramitā: that the five aggregates (五蕴 wǔyùn, namely form, sensation, perception, mental formations, and consciousness) are all empty of inherent existence. From this seeing, every category of Buddhist analysis (the senses, the elements, the chain of dependent origination, the four noble truths) is shown to be empty as well. The text closes with a mantra that performs in sound what the discourse has described in argument.</p>
      <p>The page below presents the full Xuanzang translation, broken into ten passages. Each passage carries an audio button (▶ click to play; click the voice indicator to switch between 女 Xiaoxiao and 男 Yunxi). Pinyin is hidden by default; toggle it on with the control above the text, or open it for one passage at a time using the small button on each card. The English translation is in-house, calibrated to be precise and unmystified; the commentary fold on each card opens a brief note where it earns one.</p>

      <div class="passage" id="p1">
        <span class="passage-marker">第一段 · dì-yī duàn · 1 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">观自在菩萨，行深般若波罗蜜多时，照见五蕴皆空，度一切苦厄。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Guān zì zài pú sà, xíng shēn bō rě bō luó mì duō shí, zhào jiàn wǔ yùn jiē kōng, dù yī qiè kǔ è.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">When the bodhisattva of sovereign perception was practicing the deep perfection of wisdom, he illumined and saw that the five aggregates are all empty, and he crossed beyond all suffering and distress.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p1-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p1-note" hidden>
          <p><strong>观自在 Guānzìzài</strong> is Xuanzang's rendering of <em>Avalokiteśvara</em>, the bodhisattva more commonly known in China as 观音 Guānyīn (perceiver of sounds, derived from <em>Avalokitasvara</em>, an alternative Sanskrit form). Xuanzang chose 观自在 (perception that is self-mastered, sovereign) to capture <em>īśvara</em> (lord, sovereign) at the end of the name. The two renderings name the same figure; 观自在 is technical, 观音 is devotional.</p>
          <p><strong>五蕴 wǔyùn</strong> are the five heaps or aggregates that Buddhist analysis breaks the experiencing person into: 色 sè (form, body, materiality), 受 shòu (sensation, feeling-tone), 想 xiǎng (perception, recognition), 行 xíng (mental formations, volitional activity), 识 shí (consciousness). The claim that they are <em>jiē kōng</em> (all empty) is the claim around which the entire sutra organizes itself.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p2">
        <span class="passage-marker">第二段 · dì-èr duàn · 2 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">舍利子，色不异空，空不异色；色即是空，空即是色。受想行识，亦复如是。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Shè lì zǐ, sè bù yì kōng, kōng bù yì sè; sè jí shì kōng, kōng jí shì sè. Shòu xiǎng xíng shí, yì fù rú shì.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">Śāriputra, form is no different from emptiness; emptiness is no different from form. Form is exactly emptiness; emptiness is exactly form. Sensation, perception, mental formations, consciousness: it is the same with all of them.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p2-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p2-note" hidden>
          <p>The most famous lines in any Buddhist text. The structure is deliberate: the sutra first says that form and emptiness are not different (不异 bù yì), then escalates to the stronger claim that they are exactly the same (即是 jí shì). The first formulation rules out separation; the second rules out duality. Once form has been treated this way, the same move applies to the other four aggregates without elaboration: <em>yì fù rú shì</em> (it is also like this) does the work for them all.</p>
          <p>The line 色即是空，空即是色 is quoted across East Asian literature, philosophy, calligraphy, and now advertising: far beyond Buddhist contexts. When Chinese speakers want a four-character formula for "appearance and reality are not two," this is the line they reach for.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p3">
        <span class="passage-marker">第三段 · dì-sān duàn · 3 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">舍利子，是诸法空相，不生不灭，不垢不净，不增不减。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Shè lì zǐ, shì zhū fǎ kōng xiàng, bù shēng bù miè, bù gòu bù jìng, bù zēng bù jiǎn.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">Śāriputra, all dharmas are marked with emptiness: not arising, not ceasing; not defiled, not pure; not increasing, not decreasing.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p3-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p3-note" hidden>
          <p><strong>诸法 zhū fǎ</strong> means "all dharmas": every constituent of experience that Buddhist analysis identifies. The six pairs of negations (arising/ceasing, defiled/pure, increasing/decreasing) cover the three spheres in which something might be said to have substantial existence: in time (does it come and go?), in moral status (is it pure or impure?), in quantity (is there more or less of it?). To deny each pair is to deny that emptiness itself can be characterized in any of these ways. Emptiness is not a thing that exists or fails to exist; it is the absence of inherent self-existence in everything.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p4">
        <span class="passage-marker">第四段 · dì-sì duàn · 4 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">是故空中无色，无受想行识；无眼耳鼻舌身意；无色声香味触法；无眼界，乃至无意识界。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Shì gù kōng zhōng wú sè, wú shòu xiǎng xíng shí; wú yǎn ěr bí shé shēn yì; wú sè shēng xiāng wèi chù fǎ; wú yǎn jiè, nǎi zhì wú yì shí jiè.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">Therefore in emptiness there is no form, no sensation, no perception, no mental formations, no consciousness; no eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind; no sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, mental objects; no realm of seeing, and so on, to no realm of mental consciousness.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p4-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p4-note" hidden>
          <p>The sutra now systematically empties out the standard Buddhist categories of analysis. First the five aggregates again; then the six sense faculties (eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind); then the six sense objects (sights, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, mental objects); then the eighteen <em>dhātus</em> (realms), abbreviated by the formula <em>nǎi zhì</em> (and so on, up to). These are not denied as <em>experiences</em>; the claim is that they have no inherent self-existence on which something fixed could be predicated. The Heart Sutra is doing in compressed form what the long Prajñāpāramitā texts do across thousands of pages: applying the analysis of emptiness to every Buddhist category one by one.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p5">
        <span class="passage-marker">第五段 · dì-wǔ duàn · 5 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">无无明，亦无无明尽，乃至无老死，亦无老死尽。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Wú wú míng, yì wú wú míng jìn, nǎi zhì wú lǎo sǐ, yì wú lǎo sǐ jìn.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">There is no ignorance and no ending of ignorance, and so on, to no aging-and-death and no ending of aging-and-death.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p5-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p5-note" hidden>
          <p>The twelve-link chain of dependent origination (十二因缘 shí'èr yīnyuán) is the Buddha's analysis of how suffering arises: ignorance gives rise to volitional formations, which give rise to consciousness, and so on through twelve links to aging-and-death. The Heart Sutra empties the entire chain, both the links themselves and their cessation. To say there is no ending of ignorance is not to say that liberation is impossible: it is to deny that liberation is the production of a substance called "the ending of ignorance." The chain itself is empty; what it describes does not have the kind of existence that could be added to or subtracted from.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p6">
        <span class="passage-marker">第六段 · dì-liù duàn · 6 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">无苦集灭道；无智亦无得。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Wú kǔ jí miè dào; wú zhì yì wú dé.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">There is no suffering, no origin, no cessation, no path; no wisdom and also no attainment.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p6-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p6-note" hidden>
          <p>The Four Noble Truths (suffering 苦, its origin 集, its cessation 灭, and the path to its cessation 道) were the content of the Buddha's first sermon at Sarnath. Mahāyāna Buddhism does not reject them; it relocates them. The Prajñāpāramitā move is to apply emptiness even to the foundational teaching, to keep the doctrine from being clung to as a substantial thing. <em>Wú zhì yì wú dé</em> (no wisdom and also no attainment) finishes the move: not even the wisdom that perceives emptiness, nor the attainment of awakening itself, escape the analysis. There is nothing to grasp at any point.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p7">
        <span class="passage-marker">第七段 · dì-qī duàn · 7 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">以无所得故，菩提萨埵，依般若波罗蜜多故，心无挂碍；无挂碍故，无有恐怖，远离颠倒梦想，究竟涅槃。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Yǐ wú suǒ dé gù, pú tí sà duǒ, yī bō rě bō luó mì duō gù, xīn wú guà ài; wú guà ài gù, wú yǒu kǒng bù, yuǎn lí diān dǎo mèng xiǎng, jiū jìng niè pán.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">Because there is nothing to attain, the bodhisattva, relying on the perfection of wisdom, has a mind free of obstruction; free of obstruction, he is without fear, far from inverted dreaming, and reaches the ultimate nirvana.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p7-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p7-note" hidden>
          <p>The pivot. After six passages of negation, the seventh shows what the negation accomplishes. <em>Yǐ wú suǒ dé gù</em> (because there is nothing to attain) reads as a result clause: <em>because</em> nothing has been treated as substantial, the mind has nothing to cling to, and the cascade follows: no obstruction, no fear, no inverted thinking, the ultimate nirvana. <em>颠倒梦想 diāndǎo mèngxiǎng</em> (inverted dreaming) names the basic cognitive distortion that takes empty things to be substantial. The path of the Heart Sutra is not the addition of insight but the subtraction of the obstruction that emptiness reveals.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p8">
        <span class="passage-marker">第八段 · dì-bā duàn · 8 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">三世诸佛，依般若波罗蜜多故，得阿耨多罗三藐三菩提。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Sān shì zhū fó, yī bō rě bō luó mì duō gù, dé ā nòu duō luó sān miǎo sān pú tí.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">All buddhas of the three times, relying on the perfection of wisdom, attain unsurpassed, complete, perfect awakening.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p8-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p8-note" hidden>
          <p><strong>三世 sān shì</strong> are the three times: past, present, future. <em>三世诸佛</em> means every buddha across all of time. <strong>阿耨多罗三藐三菩提 ānòuduōluó sānmiǎo sānpútí</strong> is a transliteration of Sanskrit <em>anuttarā samyaksaṃbodhi</em>: unsurpassed (anuttarā), complete (samyak), perfect awakening (saṃbodhi). The transliteration was kept rather than translated because the term is liturgically dense; rendering it semantically would lose the technical specificity. Every buddha, the line says, reached this same awakening through this same perfection of wisdom: including the historical Buddha, including every buddha in any time.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p9">
        <span class="passage-marker">第九段 · dì-jiǔ duàn · 9 of 10</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">故知般若波罗蜜多，是大神咒，是大明咒，是无上咒，是无等等咒，能除一切苦，真实不虚。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Gù zhī bō rě bō luó mì duō, shì dà shén zhòu, shì dà míng zhòu, shì wú shàng zhòu, shì wú děng děng zhòu, néng chú yī qiè kǔ, zhēn shí bù xū.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">Therefore know the perfection of wisdom is the great divine mantra, the great bright mantra, the unsurpassed mantra, the unequalled mantra. It can remove all suffering, true and not empty.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p9-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p9-note" hidden>
          <p>A pivot from analysis to liturgy. The text now declares that the perfection of wisdom is itself a mantra: a sound formula that performs what it names. The four titles (great divine, great bright, unsurpassed, unequalled) are conventional Buddhist superlatives that mark a teaching as foundational. The closing words <em>zhēn shí bù xū</em> (true and not empty) are pointed: in a text that has spent eight passages emptying every category, the mantra itself is asserted to be true and not empty. The reader is meant to feel the shift. Some commentators read <em>bù xū</em> here as "not vain, not in vain": the mantra works, it is not idle speech.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="passage" id="p10" data-passage-type="mantra">
        <span class="passage-marker">第十段 · dì-shí duàn · 10 of 10 · The Mantra</span>
        <div class="passage-head">
          <span class="passage-cn">故说般若波罗蜜多咒，即说咒曰：揭谛揭谛，波罗揭谛，波罗僧揭谛，菩提萨婆诃。</span>
          <span class="passage-py">Gù shuō bō rě bō luó mì duō zhòu, jí shuō zhòu yuē: Jiē dì jiē dì, bō luó jiē dì, bō luó sēng jiē dì, pú tí sà pó hē.</span>
        </div>
        <div class="passage-en">Therefore I speak the mantra of the perfection of wisdom, saying: Gate, gate, pāragate, pārasaṃgate, bodhi svāhā.</div>
        <button type="button" class="passage-note-toggle" aria-controls="p10-note" aria-expanded="false">注 open</button>
        <div class="passage-note" id="p10-note" hidden>
          <p>The mantra is left untranslated by Xuanzang: written phonetically in Chinese to preserve the Sanskrit sound. A traditional approximation in English: <em>gone, gone, gone beyond, gone altogether beyond, awakening, hail</em>. <em>Gate</em> (Sanskrit, "gone") repeats; <em>pāragate</em> means gone to the other shore; <em>pārasaṃgate</em> means altogether to the other shore; <em>bodhi</em> is awakening; <em>svāhā</em> is the standard mantra-closing exclamation, often rendered as "hail" or left as a sound. The whole sutra has been moving toward this closing: argument resolves into chant, the discourse becomes the practice, the pages of doctrine become a sound that carries the practitioner across.</p>
        </div>
      </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="mantra"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">真言</span><span class="sh-py">zhēnyán</span><span class="sh-en">The Mantra: How to Read It</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="咒">
      <div class="scholar-label">真言洞见 zhēnyán dòngjiàn · The Mantra</div>
      <p>The mantra is in Sanskrit, transliterated rather than translated. The Chinese characters <strong>揭谛揭谛波罗揭谛波罗僧揭谛菩提萨婆诃</strong> exist to encode the sounds <em>gate gate pāragate pārasaṃgate bodhi svāhā</em>; the characters carry no semantic load relevant to the meaning. The Tang-dynasty pronunciations preserved in liturgical reading approximate the Sanskrit closely. The standard contemporary Mandarin reading, used in Chinese temples, is <em>jiēdì jiēdì, bōluó jiēdì, bōluó sēng jiēdì, pútí sà pó hē</em>. Japanese Buddhist liturgy reads the same characters as <em>gyatei gyatei, haragyatei, harasōgyatei, boji sowaka</em>. Both retain the structure even where the phonetics drift.</p>
      <p>The mantra is not a code for a hidden message. The Heart Sutra states that the perfection of wisdom <em>is</em> the mantra; the analysis and the chant are two forms of the same content. Practitioners chant the whole sutra and then chant the mantra alone, often three or seven or one hundred and eight times. The repetition is the point. What the analysis demonstrates discursively, the mantra performs sonically.</p>
    </div>

          <span class="card-py">sè jí shì kōng, kōng jí shì sè</span>
          <span class="card-en">form is exactly emptiness; emptiness is exactly form</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">经典 Classic · 心经 Heart Sutra</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The most quoted line of the Heart Sutra and arguably of any Mahāyāna text. The eight characters fold the entire teaching of emptiness into a single chiastic formula. Quoted across East Asian literature, philosophy, calligraphy, contemporary fiction, and advertising. When a Chinese speaker reaches for a four-character (or eight-character) formula for "appearance and reality are not two," this is what they reach for.

        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">心无挂碍</span>
          <span class="card-py">xīn wú guà ài</span>
          <span class="card-en">a mind free of obstruction</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-n">经典 Classic · 心经 Heart Sutra</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          From passage 7. <em>挂碍 guà ài</em> means hindrance or obstruction in the literal sense of something snagging or hooking. <em>心无挂碍</em> describes the mind that, having seen emptiness, has nothing left to snag on. The phrase is now used outside Buddhist contexts as a description of psychological freedom from anxiety or obsessive attachment. A bridge between classical doctrine and contemporary therapeutic vocabulary.

          The opening of the closing mantra. Even taken out of liturgical context, these lines carry a strong recognizable rhythm in Chinese Buddhist culture; readers and listeners hear the rest of the mantra by implication when these first phrases sound. The repetition: <em>gate gate</em> doubled, then <em>pāragate</em>, then <em>pārasaṃgate</em>: is the practice's basic shape: not adding, not arriving, just going, then going further.

    <span class="section-anchor" id="history"></span>
    <div class="section-head"><span class="sh-cn">历史</span><span class="sh-py">lìshǐ</span><span class="sh-en">History &amp; Transmission</span><span class="sh-rule"></span></div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="奘">
      <div class="scholar-label">译史洞见 yìshǐ dòngjiàn · Translation History</div>
      <p>The Heart Sutra exists in multiple Chinese translations spanning four centuries. The earliest extant translation is attributed to Kumārajīva (鸠摩罗什, c. 402-413 CE) under the title 摩诃般若波罗蜜大明咒经 (the Great Bright Mantra Sutra of Mahāprajñāpāramitā). Xuanzang's 649 CE translation, made shortly after his return from India, became the dominant version and remains the text used liturgically across the Chinese-speaking Buddhist world. There are at least seven other Tang-dynasty translations attested in the canon, including renderings by Fa Yue, Bhagavataddharmra, Prajñā, and Faqing. Most are now of historical interest only; Xuanzang's reading carried the day for its precision, its compression, and the prestige of its translator.</p>
      <p>Xuanzang (玄奘, 602-664 CE) is the most famous figure in the Chinese Buddhist translation tradition. He left China illegally in 629, traveled overland through Central Asia to India, studied at Nālandā monastery for years, and returned to Chang'an in 645 with hundreds of Sanskrit texts and a mandate from Emperor Taizong to translate them. He devoted the rest of his life to the project, producing translations of seventy-six sutras and śāstras: over thirteen hundred fascicles: that defined Chinese Buddhist textual culture for the rest of the imperial period. The Heart Sutra was a small part of this output but became its most widely known piece. The popular novel <em>Journey to the West</em> (西游记) fictionalized Xuanzang's pilgrimage three centuries later as the figure of Tang Sanzang, accompanied by Sun Wukong and other companions; the historical Xuanzang made the trip alone.</p>
      <p>Modern scholarship has raised the question of whether the Heart Sutra was originally composed in India or whether it was assembled in China, possibly by Xuanzang himself or a close associate, by abridging passages from the longer Prajñāpāramitā literature. Jan Nattier's 1992 article "The Heart Sūtra: A Chinese Apocryphal Text?" made the most influential argument for Chinese composition. The debate continues; the text's standing as scripture in the living tradition does not depend on its resolution.</p>
