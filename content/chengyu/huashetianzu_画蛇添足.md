      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">画蛇添足</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">huà shé tiān zú</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">He finished first, so he kept drawing. The idiom for ruining a good thing by overdoing it.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="story"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">The Story · 故事 gùshi</h2>
      <div class="scholar" data-glyph="蛇">
        <div class="scholar-label">战国策 Zhànguó Cè · Warring States period</div>
        <p>A group of retainers in the state of Chu were given a single jug of wine to share among them. There was not enough for everyone to drink properly, so they devised a contest. Each man would draw a snake on the ground. Whoever finished first would win the wine outright.</p>
        <p>One man finished his snake well before the others. He picked up the jug, looked around at the still-bent backs of his friends, and said: "I have time to spare. I will give my snake legs." He took up his stick and began adding feet to his drawing.</p>
        <p>Another man finished while the first was still drawing. He snatched the jug from beside the first man's hand and drank, saying: "A snake has no legs. How can you give it legs? What you have drawn is not a snake. The wine is mine."</p>
        <p>The story comes from the <strong>战国策</strong> (Zhànguó Cè, "Strategies of the Warring States"), a late Warring States compilation of political anecdotes assembled in its received form by Liú Xiàng in the first century BCE. The original parable was political — used by an envoy to persuade a Chu general against attacking Qi, on the grounds that the general had already won enough and any further victory would only undo what he had achieved.</p>
      </div>


    <span class="section-anchor" id="usage"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Usage · 用法 yòngfǎ</h2>
      <div class="cards">
        <div class="card c-red">
          <div class="card-head">The completed thing made worse by addition</div>
          <p>The idiom describes any case where a person, having done something well, keeps going past the point where it was good. The legs do not improve the snake. They make it not-a-snake. The destruction is not because the work was bad but because the work was finished and the worker would not stop.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-ochre">
          <div class="card-head">Applied examples</div>
          <p>A clean piece of writing ruined by an extra explanatory paragraph. A solved business problem reopened by a meeting that was supposed to "tighten" the solution. A negotiation already won, then lost by a final demand. A novice cook seasoning a dish that did not need it. The Chinese phrase carries a specific embarrassment — the doer was the one who undid their own success.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-teal">
          <div class="card-head">In a sentence</div>
          <p>这篇文章本来很好，你最后加的那段就是画蛇添足。(Zhè piān wénzhāng běnlái hěn hǎo, nǐ zuìhòu jiā de nà duàn jiù shì huà shé tiān zú.) — This article was already good. The paragraph you added at the end is drawing legs on a snake.</p>
        </div>
      </div>


    <span class="section-anchor" id="characters"></span>
      <h2 class="section-head">Character Analysis · 字解 zìjiě</h2>
      <div class="cards">
        <div class="card c-ochre">
          <div class="card-head">画 huà</div>
          <p>To draw, to paint. Pictograph of a hand holding a brush above a field, originally meaning to mark out boundaries. The traditional form 畫 shows the brush (聿) above 田 (field) more clearly. Compounds: 画家 (painter), 漫画 (manga), 计划 (plan, with 划 huá the same root).</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-ochre">
          <div class="card-head">蛇 shé</div>
          <p>Snake. The 虫 (chóng, "insect/reptile" radical) on the left tags it as a creeping creature; the right side 它 was originally a pictograph of a coiled snake itself, and is now a separate character meaning "it." So 蛇 is, etymologically, a snake-radical added to a snake-pictograph for clarity — a kind of redundancy the parable itself warns against.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-teal">
          <div class="card-head">添 tiān</div>
          <p>To add, to append. The 氵 (water radical) on the left suggests adding liquid; the phonetic 忝 carries the sound. Used widely for additions of any kind: 添加 (add), 添麻烦 (cause trouble, literally "add trouble"). The connotation is often slightly negative — adding when nothing more was needed.</p>
        </div>
        <div class="card c-teal">
          <div class="card-head">足 zú</div>
          <p>Foot, leg. Also "enough, sufficient" as an adjective: 足够 (zúgòu, sufficient). The double meaning is part of the idiom's quiet pun — by adding feet (足), the painter shows he did not understand 足 (enough). What was sufficient he made insufficient.</p>
        </div>
      </div>
