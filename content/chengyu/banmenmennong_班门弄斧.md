    <header class="topic-hero">
      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Chengyu · 成语 chéngyǔ</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">班门弄斧</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">bān mén nòng fǔ</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">Brandishing an axe at Lu Ban's door , the idiom for presuming to show off your skill in front of someone who has spent a lifetime mastering it.</p>
    </header>

    <!-- ── LITERAL PARSING ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="parsing"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">字解</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zì jiě</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Literal Parsing — The Four Characters</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="斧">
      <div class="scholar-label">字解 zì jiě · Character by Character</div>
      <p><strong>班 Bān</strong> , the surname of 鲁班 Lǔ Bān, the legendary master carpenter of the Spring and Autumn period. In the idiom, 班 stands alone as a name-reference: 班门 means "Lu Ban's door," by extension the threshold of his domain and standard.</p>
      <p><strong>门 mén</strong> , door, gate. The threshold of a master's workshop. To stand at someone's 门 is to present yourself in their domain, under their measure.</p>
      <p><strong>弄 nòng</strong> , to play with; to brandish; to show off. The character captures the gesture perfectly: not using a tool seriously, but performing with it, making a display of handling it.</p>
      <p><strong>斧 fǔ</strong> , axe. The woodworker's primary tool, and the one most naturally associated with Lu Ban's craft. To brandish an axe is to claim mastery of the very thing by which Lu Ban defined himself.</p>
      <p>Together the four characters compress a whole situation into an image: you have brought an axe to the door of the man who invented woodworking, and you are showing off with it.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── ORIGIN ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="origin"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">出处</span>
      <span class="sh-py">chūchù</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Origin — Mei Zhiyuan at the Yellow Crane Tower</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="门">
      <div class="scholar-label">梅之焕 Méi Zhīhuàn · Ming dynasty</div>
      <p>The origin is attributed to <strong>梅之焕 Méi Zhīhuàn</strong>, a Ming dynasty poet and official who visited the <strong>黄鹤楼</strong> (Huánghè Lóu, Yellow Crane Tower) in Wuhan and found its walls covered in poems left by visitors over the centuries. Faced with this accumulation of mediocre verse at a site made sacred by one great poem, he wrote his own response:</p>
      <blockquote style="margin: 1rem 0; padding: 0.75rem 1rem; border-left: 3px solid var(--accent); font-style: italic;">
        采石江边一堆土，李白之名高千古。<br>
        来来往往一首诗，鲁班门前弄大斧。
      </blockquote>
      <p>A heap of earth on the bank at Caishi , yet Li Bai's name stands a thousand years. Visitors come and go, each leaving a poem, brandishing a great axe before the door of Lu Ban.</p>
      <p>The reference layered on itself: just as it would be absurd to show off carpentry in front of Lu Ban, it is equally absurd to leave inferior verse at a tower whose defining poem, Cui Hao's 黄鹤楼 (Yellow Crane Tower poem), had already ended that particular contest. Even Li Bai, who visited the same tower, is said to have put down his brush on seeing Cui Hao's inscription, declaring there was nothing left to write. Mei Zhiyuan's poem said: these later visitors did not know when to put the axe down.</p>
      <p>The idiom circulated widely from this moment and entered the standard chengyu repertoire. The longer classical form <strong>鲁班门前弄大斧</strong> (brandishing a great axe before Lu Ban's door) contracted over time to <strong>班门弄斧</strong>.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- ── WHO WAS LU BAN ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="luban"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">鲁班</span>
      <span class="sh-py">lǔ bān</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Who Was Lu Ban?</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="班">
      <div class="scholar-label">历史背景 lìshǐ bèijǐng · Historical Background</div>
      <p><strong>鲁班</strong> (507–444 BCE, approximately) was a master engineer and craftsman of the state of Lu during the Spring and Autumn period. His full name was 公输班 Gōngshū Bān. He is credited in Chinese tradition with inventing the saw, the carpenter's square, the ink-line tool (墨斗 mòdǒu), the plane, and the lock and key , as well as siege machines and a flying wooden bird. Whether historical inventions or retrospective attribution, these stories convey his status: the founding figure of Chinese craftsmanship.</p>
      <p>He became the <strong>patron saint of carpenters, stonemasons, and engineers</strong>. Workshops and guilds historically kept his image or tablet. The phrase 班门 passed into the language as a synonym for the highest standard of any craft: whatever your skill, there is always a 班门 before which it becomes ordinary.</p>
      <p>The idiom's power depends on understanding that 班门 is not just "an expert's door" in general but a specific, legendary standard. The axe-wielder is not simply outclassed , they are oblivious to how outclassed they are.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">用法</span>

    <div class="cards">

      <div class="card c-ochre">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">班门弄斧</span>
          <span class="card-py">bān mén nòng fǔ</span>
          <span class="card-en">self-deprecating register — "I'm showing off before a master, please advise"</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V phrase 动词短语</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          The most common use in formal and semiformal speech. A speaker introduces their own presentation, essay, or performance by acknowledging that the audience likely knows more than they do. The formula <strong>班门弄斧，请多指教</strong> is a standard opener in academic talks, business presentations, and public remarks. The speaker is not genuinely claiming ignorance , they are performing appropriate humility (谦虚 qiānxū) before an audience that may include people of greater expertise.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">班门弄斧，请各位多多指教。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Bān mén nòng fǔ, qǐng gèwèi duōduō zhǐjiào.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I'm brandishing my axe before Lu Ban's door , please, everyone, offer your guidance.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">在这些专家面前谈这个话题，真是班门弄斧了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zài zhèxiē zhuānjiā miànqián tán zhège huàtí, zhēn shì bān mén nòng fǔ le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Raising this topic in front of these experts , I'm really showing off in front of the master.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">我只是初学者，今天来这里分享，班门弄斧，见笑了。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Wǒ zhǐshì chūxuézhě, jīntiān lái zhèlǐ fēnxiǎng, bān mén nòng fǔ, jiànxiào le.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">I'm only a beginner , coming here to share today is showing off before the master. Don't laugh at me.</div>
          </div>
        </div>
        <div class="note">
          <span class="note-label">用法注意 yòngfǎ zhùyì · Usage Note</span>
          In this self-deprecating register, 班门弄斧 functions as a social lubricant , it signals that the speaker is aware of the gap between their knowledge and the audience's, and is not claiming false equivalence. Omitting it in contexts where the audience is clearly more expert can read as arrogance.
        </div>
      </div>

      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">
          <span class="card-cn">班门弄斧</span>
          <span class="card-py">bān mén nòng fǔ</span>
          <span class="card-en">critical register — "they're showing off before the master, unaware of how little they know"</span>
        </div>
        <div class="tags"><span class="tag tag-v">V phrase 动词短语</span></div>
        <div class="card-def">
          When applied to a third party, the idiom becomes critical , often gently mocking. The subject is not merely less skilled than the expert; they are oblivious to the gap. The critical charge in 班门弄斧 is as much about lack of self-awareness as it is about lack of skill. A person who knows they are outclassed and proceeds carefully is not 班门弄斧-ing; the person who proceeds confidently without knowing they are outclassed is.
        </div>
        <div class="examples">
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他在那位老画家面前大谈绘画技巧，真是班门弄斧。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā zài nà wèi lǎo huàjiā miànqián dà tán huìhuà jìqiǎo, zhēn shì bān mén nòng fǔ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Lecturing at length about painting technique in front of that old painter , that's really showing off at Lu Ban's door.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">他竟然去指导那位资深工程师，简直是班门弄斧。</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Tā jìngrán qù zhǐdǎo nà wèi zīshēn gōngchéngshī, jiǎnzhí shì bān mén nòng fǔ.</div>
            <div class="ex-en">He actually went to instruct that senior engineer , it was really a case of showing off before the master.</div>
          </div>
          <div class="example">
            <div class="ex-cn">在这位棋圣面前讲什么棋术，不是班门弄斧吗？</div>
            <div class="ex-py">Zài zhè wèi qíshèng miànqián jiǎng shénme qíshù, bù shì bān mén nòng fǔ ma?</div>
            <div class="ex-en">Talking about chess technique in front of this grandmaster , isn't that showing off at Lu Ban's door?</div>
          </div>
        </div>

    <!-- ── CONTRAST ── -->
    <span class="section-anchor" id="contrast"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">辨析</span>
      <span class="sh-py">biànxī</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Contrast — Related Idioms of Misplaced Imitation</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="弄">
      <div class="scholar-label">辨析 biànxī · Distinguishing Related Idioms</div>
      <p>三 idioms in the classical repertoire describe different flavors of misplaced imitation or overreach. Knowing the distinctions matters for precise use.</p>
      <p><strong>班门弄斧</strong> , you have skill, but you are showing it off in front of someone whose skill vastly exceeds yours, unaware of how the comparison reads. The problem is the audience, and the obliviousness to it.</p>
      <p><strong>东施效颦 dōng shī xiào pín</strong> , you imitate the surface behavior of a beautiful or admired person without understanding what made it attractive, producing the opposite of the intended effect. (东施, an ugly woman, imitated the face-touching gesture of the beautiful 西施, not knowing the gesture was attractive because 西施 was in pain from a genuine ailment.) The problem is imitation without understanding.</p>
      <p><strong>邯郸学步 hándān xué bù</strong> — you go to learn the admired way of doing something, but in trying to copy it you forget how you used to do it yourself, ending up with neither. A young man from Yan went to Handan to learn the elegant Handan walk; he returned to Yan crawling on hands and knees, having forgotten how to walk at all. The problem is abandoning your own competence in the pursuit of someone else's.</p>
    </div>

    <!-- RETENTION SCHOLAR BOX -->
    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="斧" style="margin-top:2.5rem;">
      <div class="scholar-label">记忆法 jìyìfǎ · Retention Image</div>
      <p>The image is precise and unforgiving: you have walked up to Lu Ban's workshop door — the man who invented the saw, the square, and the plane — and you are standing there, waving your axe around. He is watching. You do not notice him watching.</p>
      <p>That last detail is the idiom's sharpest edge. The problem is not the axe or even the showing off. The problem is not knowing where you are standing. 班门弄斧 is always, at its core, a failure of situational awareness about one's own skill level relative to the context. In the self-deprecating register, the speaker preempts this judgment by naming it first. In the critical register, someone else names it for them.</p>
      <p>Mei Zhiyuan's original poem adds a layer worth keeping: even Li Bai, one of the greatest poets in Chinese history, knew to put down his brush at the Yellow Crane Tower. The mark of mastery is knowing when not to brandish the axe.</p>
