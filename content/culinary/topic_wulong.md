      <span class="topic-hero-eyebrow">Culinary · 饮食 yǐnshí</span>
      <h1 class="topic-hero-title">乌龙茶</h1>
      <span class="topic-hero-title-py">wū lóng chá</span>
      <p class="topic-hero-desc">The most complex and technically demanding tea category — partially oxidized, spanning a full spectrum from near-green to near-black, with a vocabulary of flavor unmatched in tea.</p>
    </header>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="processing"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">制法</span>
      <span class="sh-py">zhìfǎ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Processing — The Art of Partial Oxidation</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="龙">
      <div class="scholar-label">半发酵 bàn fājiào · Between Green and Black</div>
      <p>Oolong processing is the most complex in the tea world, and the variations between styles within the category are wide enough that a light Tieguanyin and a heavily roasted Da Hong Pao can seem like different beverages entirely. The shared principle is partial oxidation: the leaf is deliberately oxidized to a degree between the zero oxidation of green tea and the full oxidation of black tea — ranging from roughly 15% (for the lightest Taiwanese high mountain oolongs) to 85% (for dark-roasted Wuyi rock oolongs). Where the process is stopped determines almost everything about the final flavor.</p>
      <p>The oolong production sequence typically runs: <strong>晒青 shài qīng</strong> (solar withering, 15–45 min in sunlight to begin moisture loss and heat up the leaf) → <strong>晾青 liàng qīng</strong> (shade withering, indoors, several hours) → <strong>做青 zuò qīng</strong> (cyclical rolling and resting to bruise the leaf edges and control oxidation) → <strong>杀青 shā qīng</strong> (heat-fixing when the target oxidation level is reached) → <strong>揉捻 róuniǎn</strong> (rolling to shape the leaf) → <strong>干燥 gānzào</strong> (drying). For roasted styles, <strong>焙火 bèi huǒ</strong> (charcoal roasting) follows as a final stage.</p>
      <p>The <strong>做青</strong> step is the heart of oolong production — and the step most difficult to master. The processor periodically rolls and shakes the leaf in bamboo baskets to bruise the leaf edges while the center of the leaf remains relatively intact; this creates a differential oxidation pattern — oxidized edges, less-oxidized center — visible in the finished leaf as a "red edge, green center" (绿叶红边 lǜ yè hóng biān) pattern. The number of rolling cycles, their intensity, and the duration of each resting interval between cycles determine the final oxidation level and flavor profile. A skilled maker adjusts these variables in real time in response to the temperature, humidity, and the behavior of each specific batch of leaf.</p>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="regions"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">产区</span>
      <span class="sh-py">chǎnqū</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Major Regions — Fujian, Guangdong, Taiwan</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">铁观音 Tiěguānyīn · Iron Goddess of Mercy</div>
        <p>From Anxi county in southern Fujian, the most famous oolong in China and the tea most closely associated with the Hokkien tea culture that spread across Southeast Asia. Traditional Tieguanyin is tightly rolled into small balls (ball-rolled oolongs), medium oxidation, with a characteristic 兰花香 lán huā xiāng (orchid fragrance) and a long, sweet, honeyed finish. Contemporary production includes both the lightly oxidized, fresh "清香型 qīngxiāng" style (green, floral, made for immediate consumption) and the traditional "浓香型 nóngxiāng" style (roasted, complex, with greater aging potential). The name refers to Guanyin, the Buddhist bodhisattva of compassion.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">武夷岩茶 Wǔyí Yán Chá · Wuyi Rock Oolongs</div>
        <p>From the Wuyi Mountain UNESCO Heritage site in northern Fujian — volcanic rock terrain, mineral-rich soils, and mountain mists create the "rock rhyme" (岩韵 yán yùn) that defines these teas. The most famous is <strong>大红袍 Dà Hóng Páo</strong> (Big Red Robe), originally from six specific ancient bushes on a cliff face and now one of the most legendary names in tea; authentic "mother tree" Da Hong Pao is in a museum. The Wuyi rock oolong family includes <strong>水仙 Shuǐxiān</strong> (Water Sprite), <strong>肉桂 Ròuguì</strong> (Cinnamon, named for its spicy aromatic profile), and dozens of locally-named cultivars. All Wuyi oolongs are subjected to charcoal roasting that builds depth and longevity.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">凤凰单丛 Fènghuáng Dāncóng · Phoenix Single Bush</div>
        <p>From the Phoenix Mountain (凤凰山) in Chaozhou, Guangdong — the heartland of gongfu tea culture. Dan Cong oolongs are named after individual cultivar clones (单丛 "single bush") and classified by their natural aromatic profiles, which mimic fruits and flowers without additives. The major aromatic types include 鸭屎香 yā shǐ xiāng (Duck Shit Aroma, a deliberately misleading name to prevent theft of prized bushes — it smells like gardenia and honey), 桂花香 guì huā xiāng (osmanthus), 芝兰香 zhī lán xiāng (orchid), and 杏仁香 xìngrén xiāng (almond). Each type corresponds to a specific cultivar with a specific natural volatile compound profile.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <div class="card-head">高山茶 Gāoshān Chá · Taiwan High Mountain Oolongs</div>
        <p>Grown above 1,000m elevation in the central mountains of Taiwan — Ali Shan (阿里山), Li Shan (梨山), and Da Yu Ling (大禹岭) among the famous areas. Taiwan's high-altitude oolongs are the lightest-oxidized in the category (often 15–25%), tightly ball-rolled, extremely floral and creamy, with a long sweet finish and almost no astringency. The style developed in Taiwan in the mid-twentieth century and represents a departure from the more heavily processed Fujian tradition. <strong>东方美人 Dōngfāng Měirén</strong> (Oriental Beauty), Taiwan's signature heavily-oxidized oolong bitten by leafhoppers, is the notable exception.</p>
      </div>
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="roasting"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">焙火</span>
      <span class="sh-py">bèi huǒ</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Roasting — The Fire That Defines Character</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="scholar" data-glyph="火">
      <div class="scholar-label">炭火焙茶 tàn huǒ bèi chá · Charcoal Roasting</div>
      <p>Roasting is not a universal step in oolong production — light Taiwan high-mountain oolongs are typically unroasted or only minimally so — but it is the defining feature of the Fujian tradition, particularly in the Wuyi rock oolong school. Traditional charcoal roasting (<strong>炭焙 tàn bèi</strong>) is one of the most demanding skills in tea production: the processor buries the lit charcoal under a layer of ash to control temperature precisely, places the tea in baskets over the heat source, and roasts in multiple sessions over a period of days or weeks, allowing the tea to rest between sessions so the heat penetrates evenly without scorching.</p>
      <p>Roasting at different temperatures and durations produces distinct effects on the flavor. Light roasting (<strong>轻焙 qīng bèi</strong>) preserves the fresh floral character while removing the raw "green" edge. Medium roasting (<strong>中焙 zhōng bèi</strong>) develops toasty, caramel, and dried fruit notes. Heavy roasting (<strong>足焙 zú bèi</strong>) produces deep caramelization, dark chocolate, and mineral characters — the full expression of the rock oolong style — and simultaneously reduces astringency while building body. The roast level is a creative decision by the producer, and the same base tea processed to different roast levels becomes a substantially different product.</p>
      <p>Roasted oolongs also age well. A heavily roasted Wuyi rock oolong will continue to evolve in storage as the roast character integrates with the base tea character, eventually producing a layered, complex tea in which the roast and the mineral origin notes are inseparable. The practice of re-roasting aged tea — 复焙 fù bèi — to refresh the roast character and remove any storage notes is a standard part of the Wuyi producer's repertoire.</p>
    </div>

    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">冲泡</span>
      <span class="sh-py">chōngpào</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Brewing — The Gongfu Method</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="pattern">
      <span class="label">乌龙茶冲泡参数 · Oolong Brewing Parameters</span>
      <strong>水温 shuǐwēn (Water Temperature)</strong> → 90–95°C for lightly oxidized; 95–100°C for heavily roasted Wuyi styles and Da Hong Pao; never use lower temperatures that suppress the aromatic complexity<br>
      <strong>茶水比 chá shuǐ bǐ (Leaf-to-Water Ratio)</strong> → High: 5–7g per 100ml for gongfu method; oolongs reward a high leaf ratio with multiple steeps<br>
      <strong>浸泡时间 jìnpào shíjiān (Steep Time)</strong> → First steep (after wash): 20–40 sec; subsequent steeps extend by 10–15 sec each; quality oolongs yield 6–12+ steeps<br>
      <strong>茶具 cháju (Vessel)</strong> → Gàiwǎn (lidded bowl) preferred for floral light oolongs; Yixing clay teapot ideal for Wuyi rock styles (clay absorbs and mellows the roast character over time)<br>
      <strong>洗茶 xǐ chá (Tea Wash)</strong> → Standard practice for oolongs: one quick rinse (5–10 sec) discarded before the first drinking steep; awakens the tightly rolled leaves
    </div>

    <span class="section-anchor" id="vocab"></span>
    <div class="section-head">
      <span class="sh-cn">词汇</span>
      <span class="sh-py">cíhuì</span>
      <span class="sh-en">Key Vocabulary</span>
      <span class="sh-rule"></span>
    </div>

    <div class="cards">
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <span class="card-cn">做青</span>
        <span class="card-py">zuò qīng</span>
        <span class="card-en">working the green — the oxidation control step</span>
        <span class="tag-v">v.</span>
        <p>The defining production step for oolong: cyclically rolling and shaking the leaf in bamboo baskets to bruise the leaf edges while the center remains intact, creating differential oxidation. The number and intensity of cycles, and the duration of resting between cycles, determine the oxidation level and final flavor profile. The most difficult step to master in all of tea production.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <span class="card-cn">岩韵</span>
        <span class="card-py">yán yùn</span>
        <span class="card-en">rock rhyme — the mineral terroir of Wuyi</span>
        <span class="tag-n">n.</span>
        <p>The characteristic mineral, rocky quality in the aftertaste of authentic Wuyi rock oolongs, attributed to the volcanic rock soil of the Wuyi Mountain origin area. One of tea's most specific terroir concepts. A tea without 岩韵 is not considered a genuine Wuyi rock tea regardless of other qualities.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <span class="card-cn">音韵</span>
        <span class="card-py">yīn yùn</span>
        <span class="card-en">Guanyin rhyme — Tieguanyin's signature quality</span>
        <span class="tag-n">n.</span>
        <p>The characteristic lingering finish of high-quality Tieguanyin — a long, sweet, orchid-like aftertaste that is considered the defining marker of authentic Anxi Tieguanyin. Analogous to 岩韵 for Wuyi teas, 音韵 is a terroir-and-cultivar concept specific to this single tea type.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card c-sienna">
        <span class="card-cn">绿叶红边</span>
        <span class="card-py">lǜ yè hóng biān</span>
        <span class="card-en">green leaf red edge — the visual sign of oolong oxidation</span>
        <span class="tag-n">n.</span>
        <p>The characteristic appearance of a properly processed oolong leaf after 做青: the edges are oxidized and reddish-brown while the center of the leaf remains green. The pattern is visible in the wet leaves after brewing and is used as a quality check by experienced producers and buyers.</p>
      </div>
