跨越

kuàyuè to leap across, to span, to transcend

To stride-cross. The verb that carries weight: not just passing a boundary but spanning it — a river, a generation, a technological era, the long wall of history.

字源 zìyuán Decomposition — 跨 stride + 越 cross over
字源洞见 zìyuán dòngjiàn · Etymological Insight

跨 kuà + 越 yuè. Both characters carry the foot-radical 足 / 走 — 跨 has 足 (foot) on the left, 越 has 走 (walking) wrapping the bottom. The compound concentrates a single image: striding wide enough to cross over.

跨 on its own means to stride, to step over with a wide gait, to mount or ride astride. It evokes the geometric act of crossing — the legs spanning the gap. 越 on its own means to cross over, to surpass, to exceed (see the dedicated 越 character page). Where 越 emphasizes the boundary being passed, 跨 emphasizes the action of spanning. Joined, they describe a crossing that is both decisive and significant — not stepping casually past a line, but bridging a real gap.

The compound is overwhelmingly modern in its productive usage. Pre-modern Chinese preferred shorter forms (越 alone, or 越过). 跨越 became a workhorse of 20th-century journalism and political speech, where bridging eras and leaping across stages of development are recurring themes. Its register is formal and slightly elevated; in casual speech, 越过 or 过 do the everyday work.

用法 yòngfǎ Three Senses — Literal, Figurative, Idiomatic
跨越 kuàyuè (literal) to stride across; to span (a physical gap)
V 动词 dòngcí
The literal sense: a bridge spans a river, a leap clears a gap, a single step crosses a fence. Common subjects are bridges, roads, railways, and human movement.
这座桥跨越长江。
Zhè zuò qiáo kuàyuè Chángjiāng.
This bridge spans the Yangtze River.
他一步跨越了那条小溪。
Tā yí bù kuàyuè le nà tiáo xiǎo xī.
In one stride he crossed the stream.
跨越 kuàyuè (figurative) to span (eras, generations, cultures); to bridge
V 动词 dòngcí
The figurative sense, more common in writing than the literal. The object is abstract: a historical era, a cultural divide, a generation gap, a technological threshold. The connotation is of significant breadth bridged.
这部小说跨越了三个时代。
Zhè bù xiǎoshuō kuàyuè le sān gè shídài.
This novel spans three eras.
音乐能跨越文化的隔阂。
Yīnyuè néng kuàyuè wénhuà de géhé.
Music can bridge cultural divides.
他们的友谊跨越了几十年。
Tāmen de yǒuyì kuàyuè le jǐ shí nián.
Their friendship has spanned decades.
跨越式 kuàyuè shì leap-forward style; "leap-across" type (modifier)
Modifier 修饰语
The modifier form, suffixing 式 (shì, type/style) to make an adjective. Most common in 跨越式发展 (kuàyuè shì fāzhǎn, "leap-forward development"), the standard term in Chinese policy and journalism for skipping intermediate stages of economic or technological growth.
中国实现了跨越式发展。
Zhōngguó shíxiàn le kuàyuè shì fāzhǎn.
China has achieved leap-forward development.
家族 jiāzú The Crossing-Verb Family
辨析 biànxī · Distinguishing Among Synonyms

Modern Chinese has a small family of compound verbs built on 越 plus a manner-of-motion partner. They share the meaning "to cross over" but split the work by what kind of crossing is meant.

跨越 kuàyuè — to stride across; spans wide gaps, often abstract. The most figurative.
越过 yuèguò — to cross over; the everyday neutral verb. Smallest register weight of the family.
翻越 fānyuè — to climb over; physical and effortful, especially for mountains and walls.
超越 chāoyuè — to surpass; the competitive sibling, used for exceeding a person, a record, a limit.
穿越 chuānyuè — to cut through; for moving across a region (forest, desert) or, in popular fiction, time-travel.

Substitution check: a bridge 跨越 a river (formal); a person 越过 a fence (everyday); an army 翻越 a mountain pass (effortful); an athlete 超越 a record (competitive); a hiker 穿越 a forest (cutting through). With 长城 as object, 翻越长城 is most physical (climbing over the wall), 跨越长城 is most figurative (transcending what the wall stands for), and 越长城 alone is the abbreviated literary form that keeps both readings open.

越过 yuèguò to cross over; to get past
V 动词 dòngcí
The neutral, everyday version. Lower register than 跨越; smaller scale than 翻越. Use for ordinary obstacles and boundaries — a fence, a stream, a difficulty.
翻越 fānyuè to climb over (especially mountains and walls)
V 动词 dòngcí
Physical, effortful crossing. Stock verb for mountain expeditions, escape, and the Long March. 翻越长城 means literally climbing over the Great Wall.
超越 chāoyuè to surpass; to exceed
V 动词 dòngcí
Competitive crossing. Surpassing a competitor, exceeding a quota, transcending a category. The most abstract sibling.
穿越 chuānyuè to pass through; to cut across; (popular fiction) to time-travel
V 动词 dòngcí
Cutting through a region. Also the standard verb for popular time-travel fiction (穿越剧, 穿越小说) where a modern character is thrown into a past dynasty.
跨越长城 kuàyuè chángchéng "Crossing the Wall" — The Figurative Phrase
文化洞见 wénhuà dòngjiàn · Cultural Note

The Great Wall is one of Chinese's most reliable metaphor objects, and 跨越 / 越 / 翻越 are the verbs that act on it. The phrase 越长城 (yuè chángchéng), and its longer figurative cousin 跨越长城, can be used in three distinct ways:

Literal. A historical, geographic, or tourist crossing — through one of the controlled passes (山海关, 嘉峪关) or, today, climbing the brick parapet at Badaling.

Mao-era heroic. The folk saying 不到长城非好汉 ("if you have not reached the Great Wall you are not a true hero"), from Mao's 1935 poem 清平乐·六盘山, reads the wall as a personal test. To "cross" it is to prove oneself. This reading is canonical in Chinese primary-school curricula and tourist signage.

Figurative — transcending an obstacle. In contemporary Chinese, 跨越长城 (crossing the wall of X) is a productive metaphor template: 跨越历史的长城 ("cross the long wall of history"), 跨越文化的长城 ("bridge the wall between cultures"), 跨越技术的长城 ("leap the wall of technology"). The wall stands in for any large, intimidating obstacle; the verb supplies the image of decisive crossing.

Foreign learners searching for the phrase often arrive expecting either a literal wall-climbing tip or a single fixed idiom. The truer answer is that 越长城 is an open figure that any modern Chinese speaker can deploy — sometimes literal, sometimes Mao-allusive, sometimes purely metaphorical. For the wall itself, see 长城 The Great Wall.

现代 xiàndài Modern Political Usage — 跨越式发展
用法洞见 yòngfǎ dòngjiàn · Usage Note

Beyond figurative speech, 跨越 has a specific political-economic life in contemporary Chinese as the verb of leapfrogging developmental stages. The phrase 跨越式发展 (kuàyuè shì fāzhǎn, "leap-forward development") is standard in policy documents, state media, and provincial five-year plans. It typically describes regions or sectors that compress what was historically a multi-decade transition — from agriculture to industry, from analogue to digital, from rural to urban — into a much shorter span.

Related fixed phrases: 跨越发展 (no 式) is the same idea, more compact; 实现跨越 (shíxiàn kuàyuè, "to achieve a leap forward") names the policy goal as a verb-object; 历史性跨越 (lìshǐxìng kuàyuè, "a historic leap") is the rhetorical ceiling, used when claiming that a transition is epoch-making rather than incremental.

Reading Chinese economic journalism without recognizing this verb is reading at a discount: 跨越 is doing real ideological work, framing development as a leap rather than a slog. Whether the leap was real is a separate question. The word is the thing to spot.
