A guided walk through the Chinese year — the lunar calendar, the solar terms, and the great festivals that mark the turning of time.

The Double Calendar

双历 · shuāng lì · the two-track year

The Chinese calendar is a double helix: the lunar calendar (阴历 yīnlì) governs festivals and traditional life; the solar calendar (节气 jiéqì) tracks the 24 solar terms that govern farming, food, and the rhythms of the natural year. These two systems interweave through the year, producing a calendar dense with meaning — not just dates but obligations, foods, rituals, and stories.

Every festival is a node where cosmology, family life, and history converge. The New Year brings the zodiac animal and the reunion dinner; Qingming brings the obligation of remembrance; Mid-Autumn brings the full moon and the mooncake. Underneath all of it, the 24 solar terms mark the body of the agricultural year with a precision that satellites have since confirmed but never displaced.

Reading Path

节气 · The Backbone

节气 · The 24 Solar Terms — Start here: the structural frame of the whole year. The 24 terms divide the solar orbit into equal segments, each carrying its own name, character, and agricultural instruction. Everything else in the calendar hangs from this spine.

生肖 · The Zodiac

生肖 · The Chinese Zodiac — Read second: the 12-year animal cycle that underlies the lunar calendar and shapes how each New Year is named, characterised, and anticipated. Understanding the zodiac is understanding the texture of any given year.

春节 · New Year (Overview)

春节 · Spring Festival — The largest festival in the Chinese world. The lunar New Year brings reunion dinners, red envelopes, the lantern festival on the fifteenth day, and a week of obligations that touch every family. This is the cultural and historical overview.

春节 · Vocabulary

春节 · Festival Vocabulary & Phrases — The companion vocabulary entry: the greetings, the ritual phrases, the names of foods and customs. Where the culture entry gives context, this one gives the words you will actually hear and use.

清明 · Remembrance

清明 · Qingming Festival — The spring festival of tomb-sweeping and ancestral remembrance. Qingming falls on a solar term — one of the few major festivals anchored to the solar rather than lunar calendar — which makes it a vivid illustration of how the two systems coexist.

中秋 · Moon Festival

中秋 · Mid-Autumn Festival — The full moon of the eighth lunar month: mooncakes, family reunion, the legend of Chang'e. Mid-Autumn is the counterpoint to Spring Festival — quieter, more contemplative, built around the moon rather than the firework.

红白喜事 · Social Calendar

红白喜事 · Red and White Events — Weddings and funerals: the two poles of the social calendar that fall outside the fixed festival cycle but structure Chinese life just as powerfully. Red for joy, white for mourning — and a precise set of obligations for each.

命运 · Cosmological Backdrop

命运 · Fate and Destiny — The philosophical substrate beneath the calendar: the belief that time is not neutral but charged, that certain days are auspicious or inauspicious, and that one's fate (命 mìng) intersects with the turning of the year in ways that can be read and, sometimes, navigated.
