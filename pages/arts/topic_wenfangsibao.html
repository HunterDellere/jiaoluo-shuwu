文房四宝

wén fáng sì bǎo Four Treasures of the Study

The four instruments through which a scholar thought, wrote, painted, and demonstrated who he was: brush, ink, inkstone, and paper.

笔 bǐ The Brush — Construction, Types & History
器物历史 qìwù lìshǐ · Object History

The 毛笔 máobǐ (hair brush) is the oldest of the Four Treasures and the one that gives the entire tradition its character. Unlike the Western dip pen or quill, which drags a fixed nib across the page in a consistent line, the Chinese brush responds dynamically to the writer's pressure, angle, and speed: press it down and the stroke broadens; lift and it narrows to a hair's point; twist it and the stroke reveals the round structure of the tip. This responsiveness means that every stroke is simultaneously an act of control and a registration of the physical state of the person who made it. The brush does not merely convey marks; it conveys states.

The construction of a traditional brush is elegant in its simplicity: a bundle of animal hair (goat, wolf [actually weasel], rabbit, deer, or mixtures thereof) is bound tightly at the base and inserted into a hollow handle of bamboo, wood, ivory, or jade. The hair is shaped into a tapering point, with the outermost layer providing the working tip and the inner hair acting as a reservoir that holds ink and releases it gradually as the brush is used. A good brush has three qualities: 尖 jiān (a fine point), 齐 qí (even hair, meaning when wet the tip should be perfectly aligned), and 圆 yuán (roundness, meaning the body of the brush should be a perfect cone). A fourth quality, 健 jiàn (resilience, meaning the tip should spring back when pressed and released), is sometimes added to make a set of four paralleling the Four Treasures themselves.

Archaeological evidence for brush use in China dates to at least the Shang dynasty (c. 1600–1046 BCE): brushed inscriptions found on pottery and oracle bones demonstrate that writing was brush-mediated from very early in the literate tradition. The brush predates the development of the inkstone and the solid ink stick; early brushwork used liquid pigments directly. By the Han dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE), the brush-and-inkstone combination was established as the standard scholar's tool. The General Meng Tian 蒙恬, who supervised the construction of the Great Wall under the First Emperor, is credited by tradition with inventing the brush, a legend that reflects the brush's association with the consolidation of imperial civilization rather than any archaeological reality.

Regional brush-making traditions persist to the present day. The most celebrated brushes come from Huzhou 湖州 in Zhejiang province, where the craft has been practiced continuously for over a thousand years. The phrase 湖笔 Hú bǐ (Huzhou brush) is still used as a quality designation. Different hair types produce different effects: goat hair makes a soft, absorbent brush suitable for large cursive strokes and ink wash; weasel hair makes a stiffer, more resilient brush favored for fine regular script and detailed work; mixtures balance the two qualities. The choice of brush is the first aesthetic decision a calligrapher or painter makes.

墨 mò The Ink — Pine Soot, Craft & Tonal Gradation
制墨工艺 zhìmò gōngyì · Ink Craft

Traditional Chinese ink 墨 mò is a solid material, a compressed cake or stick (usually rectangular, sometimes elaborately molded into decorative forms) made from carbon black (typically pine soot 松烟 sōngyān or oil-lamp soot 油烟 yóuyān) bound with animal glue and various additives including musk, camphor, and other aromatic substances. The making of a fine ink stick is itself a skilled craft with a long history: the soot is collected, purified, mixed with glue and additives, kneaded into a dough, dried, and pressed into molds. High-quality ink sticks were gifts fit for emperors, inscribed with gold and colored lacquer, stored in silk-lined boxes, and treated as objects of art in their own right.

To use the ink, the calligrapher or painter grinds the stick against the moistened surface of the inkstone in a slow circular motion, adding drops of water as needed, until the liquid reaches the desired concentration. This act of grinding is not merely utilitarian; it is preparatory in a deeper sense, a settling of the mind before work begins. The time spent grinding ink is time spent in the mental state that the work requires. Many classical texts recommend that the scholar grind his own ink rather than delegating the task: the act of preparation is part of the practice.

The resulting liquid ranges from a near-opaque black to a dilute grey that is barely perceptible on white paper, depending on the ratio of ink to water. Concentration alone gives the ink-wash painter the full range of tones that an oil painter achieves with color mixing. A skilled ink painter can suggest sunlit water, misty distance, dense forest shadow, and rocky texture using nothing but varying concentrations of the same black liquid. The 五墨 (Five Tones of Ink) described in painting theory, from scorched black to near-clear, are all achievable from a single grinding on a single inkstone.

The two main types of ink have different characters. 松烟 sōngyān (pine soot) ink is a matte, cool, slightly blue-black, favored by calligraphers for its rich visual depth and its tendency not to reflect light. 油烟 yóuyān (oil soot) ink has a warmer, slightly brownish tone and a subtle sheen, often preferred for painting because it produces slightly more varied tonal effects. Great ink sticks from the Song and Ming dynasties survive in museum collections and are studied by connoisseurs for their formulation and quality, much as old wines are studied for their vintage character.

砚 yàn The Inkstone — The Great Producing Regions
砚台鉴赏 yàntái jiànshǎng · Inkstone Connoisseurship

The inkstone 砚 yàn, a flat stone surface with a shallow depression for the ground ink, is the most durable and, in many ways, the most prestigious of the Four Treasures. Unlike brushes and paper, which are consumable, and ink sticks, which are used up, a fine inkstone endures indefinitely and can become a heirloom passed through generations. The greatest inkstones have been the subjects of dedicated monographs, poems, and colophons; scholars collected them with the intensity that others brought to jade or bronze. Su Shi and Mi Fu both wrote extensively about inkstone aesthetics.

Inkstone quality depends on the stone itself: its ability to grind ink efficiently, its capacity to retain water without evaporating too quickly, and its surface texture, fine enough to produce smooth, fully suspended ink particles without being so smooth that the grinding action is ineffective. The best stones have a subtle tooth that grinds the ink stick evenly and a natural moisture that keeps the ink from drying too fast during work. They are cool to the touch and, when tapped, produce a resonant sound that connoisseurs use to assess density and quality.

Four producing regions dominate the classical hierarchy of Chinese inkstones. The 端砚 Duānyàn from Duanxi 端溪 in Guangdong province (a narrow quarry valley near Zhaoqing) has been regarded as the finest in China since at least the Tang dynasty. Duanxi stone is purple-grey with occasional green and blue tonal variations, and its surface has a quality described as 嫩 nèn (tender, fine-grained) that produces exceptionally smooth ink without wearing the brush excessively. The stone is also famous for its "eyes" 石眼 shíyǎn, circular markings resembling bird's eyes or fish eggs formed by geological processes, which are highly prized by collectors and sometimes incorporated into the inkstone's decorative design. The 歙砚 Shèyàn from She county 歙县 in Anhui province is the second in the traditional ranking: darker, harder than Duanxi stone, with distinctive gold and silver flecks called 金星 jīnxīng (gold stars) and 银星 yínxīng (silver stars). The 洮砚 Táoyàn from the Tao River in Gansu, the rarest of the four, is valued for its greenish color and exceptional water retention. The 澄泥砚 Chéngnísòng from Shanxi is the exception: made not from natural stone but from specially prepared river clay fired in a kiln, it was favored during the Tang and Song dynasties but declined later as natural stone inkstones became more available.

纸的文化 zhǐ de wénhuà · Paper Culture

Paper was invented in China. The traditional attribution to the Han dynasty official Cai Lun 蔡伦 around 105 CE records a significant improvement in the craft rather than an absolute beginning, as earlier forms of paper have been found archaeologically. It remained for more than a millennium a Chinese monopoly before the technology spread west through Central Asia. For calligraphy and painting, the choice of paper is as significant as the choice of brush or ink. Different papers absorb ink differently, respond to the brush differently, and determine what kinds of marks are possible.

宣纸 xuānzhǐ, made in Jingxian county 泾县 in Anhui province, is the gold standard for Chinese calligraphy and painting. Its name derives from the old administrative name for the region, Xuanzhou 宣州. The paper is made primarily from the bark of the 青檀 qīngtán (wingceltis) tree combined with rice straw, processed through a lengthy series of soaking, bleaching, beating, and sheet-forming stages. The result is a paper of exceptional durability, subtle texture, and highly controllable absorbency.

Xuan paper exists in two main varieties that require fundamentally different techniques. 生宣 shēngxuān (raw or unsized Xuan) is highly absorbent: ink dropped on it blooms and spreads immediately, creating the soft, unpredictable wash effects characteristic of ink-wash landscape painting. 熟宣 shúxuān (cooked or sized Xuan) has been treated with alum and other sizing agents that reduce its absorbency, allowing more controlled, precise work; it is used for fine figure painting, detailed bird-and-flower painting, and styles of calligraphy that require crisp, unwavering line quality. The distinction matters: a painter who attempts detailed work on raw Xuan will find the ink spreading beyond the brush tip; one who attempts atmospheric washes on sized Xuan will find the ink sitting on the surface without the soft diffusion that gives wash paintings their characteristic depth.

Well-stored Xuan paper is genuinely archival: paintings and calligraphy on Xuan paper from the Tang and Song dynasties survive in museum collections, legible and often vibrant after more than a thousand years. This durability was a conscious design priority: the scholar's work was meant to endure, and the material it was made on needed to support that ambition.

道德意义 dàodé yìyì The Brush as Moral Instrument
文化意涵 wénhuà yìhán · Cultural Meaning

The Four Treasures were never merely tools in the Chinese scholarly tradition. They were, individually and collectively, objects through which the scholar's character expressed itself and was cultivated. The dictum 字如其人 zì rú qí rén ("the character is like the person," meaning how you write reveals who you are) applies directly to the brush and, by extension, to the other Three Treasures that make the brush stroke possible. A careless choice of paper, a hastily ground ink, an inkstone left unwashed: these were not merely practical failures but intimations of moral laxity. The care taken with the tools reflected the care taken with the work and, behind that, with the character that produced the work.

This moral loading of material culture was reinforced by the biographical tradition of calligraphy criticism. The great masters, Wang Xizhi, Yan Zhenqing 颜真卿, Su Shi, and Huang Tingjian 黄庭坚, were praised not only for the quality of their strokes but for their moral characters, which the strokes were understood to manifest. Yan Zhenqing's calligraphy was held to embody his loyalty and integrity (he was executed by the Tang rebels he refused to serve); his vigorous, blocky strokes were read as the visual equivalent of his moral uprightness. This is not metaphor in the loose modern sense; it was a systematic theory of the relationship between inner character and outer form, and it meant that the act of writing was always also an act of moral self-examination.

The study 文房 wénfáng itself, the room where the Four Treasures lived, was a curated environment for this kind of work. A scholar's study was typically arranged with a writing desk, a rack for hanging brushes, a water container, a brush washer, and various small vessels for additional equipment. The aesthetic of the study, quiet, uncluttered, oriented toward a window for natural light or a garden view, was a material expression of the mental state that good work required. To enter a fine scholar's study was to enter a space shaped for attention, for the kind of focused, unhurried engagement that calligraphy demands and rewards. The Four Treasures were the instruments of that engagement; the study was its theater.

词汇 cíhuì Key Vocabulary
n 湖笔 hú bǐ

Huzhou brush — brushes made in Huzhou, Zhejiang province, regarded as the finest in China. The term is used as a quality designation, much as "Huizhou ink" signals quality in the ink category. Huzhou brush-making has been a continuous craft tradition for over a thousand years.

n 徽墨 huī mò

Huizhou ink — ink sticks produced in the Huizhou region of Anhui province, regarded as the finest in China. Huizhou is also home to the She inkstone 歙砚 and Xuan paper, making Anhui province the geographic center of the Four Treasures tradition.

n 端砚 duān yàn

Duan inkstone — from Duanxi in Guangdong province. The finest inkstone in the traditional Chinese hierarchy. Prized for its fine grain (described as 嫩 nèn, tender), water retention, and the distinctive circular "eyes" 石眼 that appear in the stone and are incorporated into decorative designs.

n 宣纸 xuān zhǐ

Xuan paper — made in Jingxian, Anhui province. The standard for Chinese calligraphy and painting. Made from wingceltis bark and rice straw. Comes in raw (生宣, highly absorbent) and sized (熟宣, controlled absorbency) varieties. Archival: properly stored examples survive over a thousand years.

n 文房 wénfáng

The scholar study, the room where the Four Treasures are kept and used. Literally "the room of writing." The aesthetic of the study, quiet, ordered, with good natural light, was itself a moral and aesthetic statement about the conditions necessary for serious intellectual and artistic work.

n 飞白 fēi bái

Flying white — the dry-brush effect in calligraphy and painting in which the hairs of the brush separate as the ink runs low, leaving streaks of white paper showing through the stroke. A controlled accident requiring a very specific brush loading; highly prized when achieved with apparent effortlessness.
