四大名著

sì dà míngzhù

The four novels every educated Chinese person has read — the canonical stories of war, outlaws, pilgrims, and dreams.

三国演义 · Romance of the Three Kingdoms

罗贯中 · Luo Guanzhong, 14th c.

三国演义 (Sānguó Yǎnyì) covers the collapse of the Han dynasty and the three-way war between Wei, Shu, and Wu (220–280 CE). Based on historical records but dramatically amplified, it is simultaneously a military strategy manual, a study in loyalty and betrayal, and the source of dozens of idioms still in active use.

Its characters are archetypes: the brilliant strategist Zhuge Liang (諸葛亮), the fraternal bond of Liu Bei, Guan Yu, and Zhang Fei sealed in the Peach Garden Oath, the treacherous Cao Cao who becomes a shorthand for villainy. The phrase 说曹操，曹操到 ("speak of Cao Cao and Cao Cao arrives") is the Chinese equivalent of "speak of the devil."

Businessmen study it for negotiation strategy. Military schools teach it. Its line 天下大势，合久必分，分久必合 ("the world under heaven, long united must divide; long divided must unite") opens the novel and remains one of the most quoted observations about historical cycles.

水浒传 · Water Margin

施耐庵 · Shi Nai'an, 14th c.

水浒传 (Shuǐhǔ Zhuàn, also translated as Outlaws of the Marsh) follows 108 outlaws who gather at Liangshan Marsh, driven there by corruption, injustice, and bad luck. It is the first great Chinese novel of the common people — its heroes are not nobles, generals, or scholars but soldiers, monks, butchers, and former officials who have been wronged.

The novel raised the concept of 江湖 (jiānghú) — the world outside official society, where different rules apply — to a moral universe. Its bandits follow a code of brotherhood more rigorous than the corrupt officials they resist. The tension between loyalty to brothers and loyalty to the state runs through every chapter.

It is also one of the first works in Chinese to use spoken vernacular rather than classical literary language — a revolutionary choice that made it accessible to everyone and influenced every subsequent novelist.

西游记 · Journey to the West

吴承恩 · Wu Cheng'en, 16th c.

西游记 (Xīyóu Jì) follows the monk Xuanzang on his journey from Tang China to India to retrieve Buddhist scriptures, protected by the Monkey King Sun Wukong, the pig-demon Zhu Bajie, and the reformed water dragon Sha Wujing. Ostensibly a pilgrimage story, it is simultaneously Buddhist allegory, Daoist satire, and one of the funniest long works in any tradition.

孙悟空 (Sūn Wùkōng, Sun Wukong) has become one of the most recognizable characters in world mythology — the trickster who has perfected seventy-two transformations, can travel 108,000 li in a single somersault, and whose staff (如意金箍棒, Rúyì Jīngū Bàng) expands and shrinks at will. His rebellion against heaven, his defeat by the Buddha, and his eventual redemption through the pilgrimage is the story's spine.

The novel has generated more adaptations — films, television series, games, manga — than almost any other pre-modern Chinese text. Son Goku in Dragon Ball is directly based on Sun Wukong.

红楼梦 · Dream of the Red Chamber

曹雪芹 · Cao Xueqin, 18th c.

红楼梦 (Hóng Lóu Mèng) is widely considered the greatest Chinese novel — a vast, melancholy portrait of the Jia clan's decline, centered on the doomed love triangle of Jia Baoyu, Lin Daiyu, and Xue Baochai. Where the other three great novels deal in war, outlaws, and adventure, Dream turns inward: its subject is feeling itself.

Cao Xueqin wrote from what appears to be autobiographical memory — the novel is saturated with the texture of aristocratic life, women's quarters, garden parties, poetry competitions, and the slow financial ruin that ends everything. The first eighty chapters are Cao's; the final forty were completed (or reconstructed) by Gao E after Cao's death.

An entire academic field — 红学 (Hóngxué, "Red Studies") — is devoted to decoding its autobiographical content, its Buddhist and Daoist allegory, and the textual history of the manuscripts. It has generated more scholarly commentary than any other Chinese novel.

Cultural Legacy · 影响 yǐngxiǎng

共同遗产 · Shared Inheritance

The four novels share a publication window of roughly 1350–1750 CE — the Ming and Qing dynasties — a period when Chinese vernacular fiction reached its mature form. Together they established the grammar of Chinese storytelling: the ensemble cast, the interlocking sub-plots, the alternation of high drama and comedy, the embedding of poems within prose narrative.

Their characters are everywhere in contemporary Chinese: in product names, political rhetoric, business analogies, internet memes, and everyday idioms. To know them is not to have read old books — it is to understand the referential layer beneath modern Chinese speech.
