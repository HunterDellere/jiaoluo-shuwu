//角落書屋/Slices/Body
耳	ěr	ear; to hear; handle — The pictograph of an ear that anchors Chinese words for listening, fame, and the furred handles on bronze vessels.
脚	jiǎo	foot, base — The everyday word for foot, and by extension the base of any standing thing: a mountain, a wall, a script. The flesh-and-bone counterpart to literary 足.
口	kǒu	mouth, opening, entrance — A simple rectangle for an open mouth: one of the most productive radicals in the script, present in hundreds of characters for speaking, eating, and openings.
目	mù	eye; to look at; section heading — The pictograph of an eye that became the Chinese radical for everything seen, watched, and classified.
女	nǚ	woman, female — A kneeling figure with crossed arms: one of the most productive radicals in the script, present in characters for family relationships, feelings, and cultural concepts.
跑	pǎo	to run; to flee; to operate — The everyday verb of running: a foot-radical character whose semantic range stretches from "the legs in motion" to "running an errand" to "the engine is running" to a small but productive family of escape-and-flee compounds.
手	shǒu	hand — A five-fingered hand drawn from life: one of the most productive radicals in the script, present in hundreds of action characters.
眼	yǎn	eye, glance — The everyday word for eye, and by extension any small opening, any moment of looking, any judgment made through sight. The body-part counterpart to literary 目.
足	zú	foot; sufficient; enough — The foot that became the word for sufficiency, and the radical anchoring every character involving legs, running, and roads.
气功	qì gōng	qigong — Cultivating the vital breath: China's ancient practice of aligning body, breath, and mind.
武术	wǔ shù	martial arts — The art of stopping weapons: how China systematized combat into a living philosophy of the body.
中医	zhōng yī	Traditional Chinese Medicine — Two thousand years of systematic medical thought: qi, yin-yang, the Five Elements, acupuncture, and the living tradition that treats the person, not the disease.
