//角落書屋/Slices/HSK 4
德	dé	virtue, moral power — Virtue not as rule-following but as accumulated moral power: what radiates from a person whose conduct has been straight long enough to become second nature.
耳	ěr	ear; to hear; handle — The pictograph of an ear that anchors Chinese words for listening, fame, and the furred handles on bronze vessels.
感	gǎn	to feel, to resonate — Feeling, perception, gratitude, and the cosmology of sympathetic resonance.
广	guǎng	wide, broad, to spread — A radical and a word: the cliff-shelter that became the prefix for everything expansive, from Guangzhou to Guangdong to radio broadcasts.
角	jiǎo	corner, horn, angle — Corner, horn, angle: the character that opens this site's name (角落書屋).
旧	jiù	old; used; former; worn — The antonym of 新, and much more than it. 旧 carries both the weight of cherished tradition and the stigma of backwardness, depending entirely on who is speaking.
跨	kuà	to stride; to span; to bestride — The character of the long stride: a foot-radical verb that reaches from "step over a threshold" to "span an era" to the modern policy vocabulary of cross-border, cross-industry, cross-cultural everything.
礼	lǐ	rites, ceremony, propriety — The character that encodes Confucian social order: the ceremonial forms that bind family, society, and cosmos into one coherent whole.
命	mìng	life, fate, command — A mouth speaking an order: 令 above, 口 inside. The character that holds together three of the heaviest words in Chinese: life, destiny, and the command from above.
目	mù	eye; to look at; section heading — The pictograph of an eye that became the Chinese radical for everything seen, watched, and classified.
其	qí	its, that, their — A winnowing basket that became the pronoun holding classical Chinese together: the silent 'its' behind thousands of literary phrases.
善	shàn	goodness; virtue; skilled — The character at the heart of Mencius argument that human nature is originally good: the moral sprouts waiting to be cultivated.
死	sǐ	death, to die, dead, fixed — A figure kneeling beside a corpse. The character behind the strongest verb of finality in Chinese, the bedrock of every taboo, and the intensifier that turns 累 into 累死了.
无	wú	nothing, without, non-being — A dancer with arms outstretched, holding nothing. The character behind every Chinese negation of having, every Daoist non-doing, and the philosophical pole that pairs with 有 to frame the whole cosmos.
行	xíng	to walk, to act, a row, a trade — One of the great polyphonic characters: xíng "to walk; to act; conduct" and háng "row; profession; firm." A pictograph of a four-way crossroads, the place where walking and the world are organized.
言	yán	speech, word, language — The root of every word about words: the mouth open, the tongue moving, the speech rising.
云	yún	cloud — The simplified 云 is older than the traditional 雲, a rare case where the modern form recovers the ancient one.
智	zhì	wisdom, moral intelligence — The Confucian virtue of knowing what is right to do; separately, the Buddhist prajñā that sees through the nature of things altogether.
足	zú	foot; sufficient; enough — The foot that became the word for sufficiency, and the radical anchoring every character involving legs, running, and roads.
道德	dào dé	morality, ethics, virtue — The compound at the center of Chinese moral philosophy: the Way and its virtue, inseparable and mutually defining.
跨越	kuà yu è	to leap across, to span, to transcend — The compound that lets the Chinese language span gaps: physical, historical, technological, generational. The verb of choice when "越长城" is read figuratively.
礼	lǐ	ritual, propriety, gift, courtesy — The cosmic blueprint Confucius spent his life trying to restore: and why manners are a civilizational technology.
礼貌	lǐ mào	politeness, courteous manner — The everyday word for politeness: what 礼 (ritual propriety) becomes when it is shrunk down to the scale of ordinary social life. Polite forms, polite faces, and the precise way modern Chinese distinguishes 礼貌 from 客气, 礼节, and the older 礼 itself.
命运	mìng yùn	fate, destiny — Fate, destiny, and the tension between cosmic order and human will.
天命	tiān mìng	the Mandate of Heaven — The political and cosmological concept that legitimized three thousand years of Chinese dynasty: heaven's appointment of the rightful ruler.
无为	wú wéi	non-action; acting in accord with nature — 无为 is the Daoist elimination of forced, ego-driven action: the master principle behind effortless effectiveness, sage governance, and Zhuangzi's cook whose knife stays forever sharp.
智慧	zhì huì	wisdom; prajna — 智慧 brings together Confucian moral discernment and Buddhist prajna under one compound: two traditions, one word, different objects of knowing.
画蛇添足	huà shé tiān zú	Drawing Legs on a Snake — He finished first, so he kept drawing, and lost the prize. The idiom for ruining a good thing by overdoing it.
与其…不如	yǔ qí bù rú	rather than … it would be better to — Comparative preference construction: the most elegant way to argue for one choice over another.
